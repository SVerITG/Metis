# Emergency and Outbreak Research Ethics

## Learning objectives
- Explain why emergencies alter but do not remove ethical obligations.
- Describe common ethical tensions in outbreak and emergency research.
- Recognize why urgency requires clearer, not weaker, justification.

## Prerequisites
- Ethics in global health partnerships.

## Content
Emergencies compress timelines, increase uncertainty, and can change the balance of risk and benefit. But urgency does not suspend ethics. Instead, it makes clarity about necessity, proportionality, consent procedures, data use, and oversight even more important.

Outbreak research may involve rapid protocol changes, public health mandates, vulnerable populations, and difficult consent environments. Ethics review may need to be accelerated or adaptive, but the underlying obligation to justify burdens and protect participants remains.

## Key takeaways
- Emergencies change procedures, not the need for ethical justification.
- Adaptive oversight can be appropriate when timelines are compressed.
- Outbreak research often combines high urgency with high participant vulnerability.

## Self-check questions
1. Why does urgency not remove ethical obligations?
2. What is one ethical challenge common in outbreak research?
3. Why might adaptive ethics review be needed?
4. What must still be justified in emergency research?
5. Why are vulnerable populations especially important in emergencies?

## Answer key
1. Because risk, fairness, and participant protection still matter under pressure.
2. Rapid consent, data sharing, or changing protocols during response.
3. Because studies may need rapid amendments or review under time pressure.
4. Necessity, proportionality, risk management, and governance.
5. Because crisis settings can magnify coercion, dependency, and harm.
