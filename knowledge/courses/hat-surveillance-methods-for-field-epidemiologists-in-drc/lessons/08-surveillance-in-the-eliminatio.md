# Lesson 8 — Surveillance in the Elimination Endgame: Verification and Reintroduction Risk

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you will be able to:
- **Evaluate** a health zone's evidence package against WHO criteria for elimination as a public health problem (EPHP).
- **Appraise** the threats to a valid elimination claim, including cryptic transmission and surveillance decay.
- **Judge** the reintroduction risk of a focus that has reached very low case numbers and recommend a post-validation surveillance posture.

## Prerequisites
- All previous lessons — this is the capstone that integrates detection, data, vector, and analysis.
- Lesson 4's prevalence trap and Lesson 5's data-quality dimensions are especially load-bearing here.

## Content

### Section 1: What "elimination" actually requires
WHO frames gHAT elimination in two steps:
- **Elimination as a public health problem (EPHP)** — the operational, country-level target (around 2030): fewer than 1 reported case per 10,000 people per year, averaged over the assessment period, in at least 90% of foci, with reported indigenous cases below a national ceiling, **and adequate surveillance to substantiate the claim**.
- **Interruption of transmission (EOT / verification of no transmission)** — zero new indigenous cases — a higher and later bar.

The phrase "adequate surveillance to substantiate the claim" is the crux. A zone with low numbers but weak surveillance has not eliminated anything provable — it has merely stopped looking. **Low cases achieved by poor detection look identical, on paper, to low cases achieved by genuine decline.** Distinguishing the two is the field epidemiologist's central evaluation task.

### Section 2: Evaluating an evidence package — what makes a claim credible
A defensible EPHP dossier for a zone should let you check, with evidence:
- **Incidence below threshold, sustained.** Not one good year — a multi-year average below 1 per 10,000 per focus (Lesson 7).
- **Maintained surveillance sensitivity.** Passive surveillance is functioning (facilities testing, positivity tracked — Lesson 2) and reactive/active screening still happens where indicated (Lesson 3). Falling cases *with* maintained or improved coverage is the credible pattern; falling cases *with* collapsing coverage is not.
- **Data quality.** Completeness, timeliness, consistency, and georeferencing all sound (Lesson 5). Every case located to a village.
- **Vector picture consistent.** Where tsetse control is used, entomological monitoring shows suppression, not just absence of reports (Lesson 6).
- **No worrying residuals.** No clustering of recent cases, no unexplained late-stage presentations (which signal undetected earlier transmission).

> **DRC-grounded example — evaluating a dossier.** A Kwilu zone submits: incidence 0.2/10,000 for three straight years; passive surveillance positivity stable; one active round last year at 78% coverage with one confirmed case; all 5 cases over three years georeferenced; tsetse FTD down 85% and holding. **Verdict: credible — recommend validation, with continued passive surveillance.** Contrast a neighbouring zone: incidence 0.1/10,000 but no active screening in four years, two facilities not reporting, and a recent late-stage case with no traced village. **Verdict: not credible — the low number likely reflects detection failure; fix surveillance before any claim.**

### Section 3: The threats — cryptic transmission and surveillance decay
Two specific dangers haunt the endgame:
- **Cryptic transmission.** Asymptomatic human carriers and a possible (still-debated) animal reservoir can sustain transmission below the radar. Crump et al. (2024) modelled DRC timelines and showed cryptic transmission can extend the true road to elimination well beyond what reported cases suggest. *This is a contested area* — the size and importance of the animal reservoir for gHAT is not settled science; treat strong claims in either direction with caution and frame your evaluation around uncertainty, not certainty.
- **Surveillance decay.** As cases vanish, political attention, funding, and staff drift away — the very moment surveillance must stay sharp. A focus can be re-seeded silently and only re-announce itself years later as a cluster of late-stage cases. The endgame paradox: success removes the visible reason to keep funding the thing that produced the success.

### Section 4: Judging reintroduction risk and setting a post-validation posture
After a zone reaches very low numbers, judge reintroduction risk on:
- **Vector presence.** Persistent tsetse = persistent transmission *potential* even at zero cases.
- **Connectivity.** Population movement from still-endemic foci (trade, fishing, displacement) can import infection.
- **Residual reservoir uncertainty.** The unresolved cryptic-transmission question.
- **Surveillance strength.** A sensitive passive network can catch re-emergence early; a decayed one cannot.

The recommended posture is **integrated, sustained, sensitive post-validation surveillance**: keep passive detection alive in every facility, retain reactive screening capacity, maintain minimal entomological sentinels where vectors persist, and keep georeferencing every case. The goal shifts from *finding many cases* to *being certain you would catch the next one fast*.

## Summary
- EPHP requires sustained sub-threshold incidence AND surveillance adequate to prove it; low numbers from poor detection do not count.
- Evaluating a dossier means checking sustained incidence, maintained coverage, data quality, a consistent vector picture, and absence of worrying residual clusters.
- Cryptic transmission (a genuinely contested, uncertain area) and surveillance decay are the two main threats to a valid claim.
- Post-validation, the posture shifts to sustained, sensitive, integrated surveillance designed to catch the next case fast — judged against vector presence, connectivity, reservoir uncertainty, and surveillance strength.

## Exercises
1. You are handed two zone dossiers with identical incidence (0.15/10,000). One has 80% active-screening coverage and full georeferencing; the other has no screening in three years and two non-reporting facilities. Evaluate each against EPHP criteria and justify which (if any) you would recommend for validation.
2. A programme manager proposes ending all surveillance in a zone that hit zero cases two years ago. Using cryptic transmission and surveillance decay, write a one-paragraph argument for a minimal sustained surveillance posture instead, and specify what that posture includes.
3. Explain why the importance of an animal reservoir for gHAT should be presented as contested rather than settled, and how that uncertainty changes how you word an elimination claim.
