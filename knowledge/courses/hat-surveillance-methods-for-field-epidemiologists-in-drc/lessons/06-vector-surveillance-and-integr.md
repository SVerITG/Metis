# Lesson 6 — Vector Surveillance and Integrated Tsetse Control

## Content

### Section 1: The vector and why it matters for gHAT
gHAT cannot spread without **tsetse flies** (*Glossina*). In Central African gHAT foci the relevant species are typically **riverine/palpalis-group tsetse** (e.g., *Glossina fuscipes*, *G. palpalis*) that live in the dense vegetation along rivers, streams, and lake edges — exactly where people fetch water, fish, wash, and farm. This overlap of human activity and fly habitat is what concentrates transmission in riverine micro-foci.

Because humans are the principal reservoir for gHAT, case finding and treatment alone can reduce transmission. But adding **vector control** attacks transmission from the other side and can accelerate decline — particularly valuable where case finding is hard or where residual transmission persists. The modern consensus is **integrated control**: medical (find-and-treat) plus entomological (suppress the vector).

### Section 2: Tiny targets — a low-cost vector tool
The breakthrough tool for gHAT vectors is the **"tiny target"**: a small (roughly 25 × 25 cm) panel of insecticide-treated blue cloth plus netting, deployed along riverbanks. Riverine tsetse are attracted to the blue cloth, land, and pick up a lethal insecticide dose. Tiny targets are cheap, light, need no power, and can be deployed by trained community teams — a good fit for DRC logistics.

Field evaluations (Tirados, Courtin et al., 2015, and subsequent studies in Uganda and DRC foci) showed tiny targets can reduce riverine tsetse densities by ~80–90% within months of deployment, contributing to measurable declines in transmission when combined with case finding.

> **DRC-grounded example.** In a Bandundu-area riverine focus with persistent cases despite annual screening, the programme maps the river network used by three affected villages and deploys tiny targets every ~50–100 m along ~10 km of high-contact riverbank, refreshing them before each rainy season. The aim is not to eradicate tsetse everywhere but to break the human–fly contact along the specific water-contact stretches.

### Section 3: Entomological monitoring — measuring whether it works
Deploying targets without monitoring is faith, not surveillance. The standard monitoring tool is the **trap** (e.g., pyramidal or biconical traps) set at fixed sentinel sites. The core entomological indicator is:

> **apparent density** = flies caught / (trap × days deployed)  — often written "flies/trap/day" (FTD)

You establish a **baseline** FTD before deploying targets, then re-trap the same sentinel sites at intervals. A successful deployment shows FTD falling sharply (often 80%+) and staying low. Monitoring also tracks:
- **Spatial coverage** — are targets placed along all high-contact stretches, or are there gaps the flies exploit?
- **Target condition** — cloth fades, gets stolen, or is overgrown; insecticide decays. Targets must be checked and replaced.
- **Seasonality** — tsetse densities and human water-contact both shift with rains; monitoring must span seasons.

> **Worked example.** Baseline trapping at 6 sentinel sites gives a mean of 8.0 flies/trap/day. Three months after deploying tiny targets, re-trapping gives 1.0 flies/trap/day.
> Reduction = (8.0 − 1.0) / 8.0 = 87.5%.
> Interpretation: a strong knock-down consistent with effective deployment — but confirm it holds across the next rainy season before crediting the intervention, and check whether the few remaining flies cluster at one under-covered site.

### Section 4: Integrating vector data with case data
Entomological and medical surveillance should be read **together**. Falling FTD plus falling case yield is the coherent signal of a focus heading toward elimination. Falling cases but stable FTD is a warning that the vector — and therefore the transmission potential — is still present, so vigilance and possibly continued targets are needed even if cases look good. This integrated reading feeds directly into the endgame judgements of Lesson 8.

## Summary
- gHAT depends on riverine tsetse whose habitat overlaps human water-contact zones, concentrating transmission in micro-foci.
- Integrated control combines medical case finding with vector suppression; tiny targets are a cheap, deployable tool that can cut riverine tsetse densities by 80–90%.
- Entomological monitoring uses traps and the flies/trap/day indicator against a baseline to verify the vector is actually being suppressed.
- Vector and case data must be interpreted together; low cases with persistent vectors is a warning, not an all-clear.
