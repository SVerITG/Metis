# Lesson 2 — Passive Surveillance and Health-Facility Detection

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you will be able to:
- **Apply** the gHAT suspect-case definition at a fixed health facility.
- **Execute** the rapid-diagnostic-test (RDT) to parasitological-confirmation algorithm in the correct order.
- **Calculate** a facility's passive-detection positivity rate and interpret what it tells you about coverage.

## Prerequisites
- Lesson 1 (disease cycle, two stages, DRC programme structure).
- Comfort with the terms screening test, confirmatory test, true/false positive.

## Content

### Section 1: What passive surveillance is — and its limits
**Passive surveillance** detects patients who present *on their own* to a fixed health facility because they feel ill. It is cheap, continuous, and runs every day the clinic is open — but it has a structural weakness for a chronic disease like gHAT: stage-1 symptoms (intermittent fever, headache) are non-specific and overlap with malaria, so patients often do not seek care until stage 2, when they are already neurologically affected and harder to cure. Passive surveillance therefore tends to catch cases **late and incompletely**, which is exactly why it must be paired with the active case finding of Lesson 3.

As the DRC moves toward elimination, **strengthening passive surveillance is the strategic priority**: when prevalence is very low, sending mobile teams to screen whole villages becomes inefficient, so a dense network of facilities able to test and confirm becomes the backbone of detection.

### Section 2: The suspect-case definition
A **suspect gHAT case** in passive surveillance is, operationally, a person presenting to a facility in or near a known endemic focus with clinical features compatible with gHAT — for example prolonged or recurrent fever not explained by a confirmed alternative, severe headache, marked weight loss, posterior-cervical lymphadenopathy (Winterbottom's sign), or sleep-cycle and neuropsychiatric disturbance — who is then eligible for serological screening.

The case definition is a deliberate filter: too narrow and you miss cases; too broad and you waste confirmatory tests. Field staff apply it to decide **who gets an RDT**.

> **DRC-grounded example.** At a health centre in Mai-Ndombe, a clinician sees 60 febrile patients in a week. Of these, 7 also report persistent headache and have a palpable neck gland and live near a riverine focus — those 7 meet the suspect definition and are offered a gHAT RDT. The other 53 are managed for their likely diagnoses. The case definition turned 60 undifferentiated fevers into 7 testable suspects.

### Section 3: The diagnostic algorithm — screen, then confirm
gHAT diagnosis is a **staircase**, because no single field test is both sensitive and specific enough to act on alone:

1. **Serological screening.** A suspect receives a rapid diagnostic test (an individual gHAT RDT) or, in some settings, the Card Agglutination Test for Trypanosomiasis (CATT). These detect antibodies — they are sensitive but **not** specific enough to diagnose on their own. A positive screen means "investigate further", not "treat".
2. **Parasitological confirmation.** A seropositive person must have trypanosomes *seen* — in lymph-node aspirate, blood (often after a concentration method such as the mini-anion-exchange centrifugation technique, mAECT), or cerebrospinal fluid. Confirmation is the gate to treatment.
3. **Staging (when required).** Historically a lumbar puncture established stage and therefore drug choice. With fexinidazole, staging is being simplified for eligible patients (see Lesson 4).

The golden rule: **never treat on a positive screen alone.** Treating false positives wastes scarce drugs, exposes patients to drug toxicity, and corrupts your case counts.

### Section 4: A simple facility indicator
A facility's **passive-detection positivity rate** is:

> positivity = (parasitologically confirmed cases) / (suspects screened)

A very low positivity with high screening volume can mean your case definition is too broad (over-testing); a high positivity with low volume can mean you are only catching obvious late-stage cases and missing earlier ones. Track it alongside the *number* screened — neither number means much alone.

## Summary
- Passive surveillance is continuous and cheap but catches gHAT late; it becomes the detection backbone as elimination nears.
- The suspect-case definition is the filter deciding who gets a screening test.
- Diagnosis is a staircase: serological screen (sensitive) → parasitological confirmation (specific) → staging when required. Never treat on a screen alone.
- Positivity rate plus screening volume together describe how well a facility's passive detection is working.

## Exercises
1. Write the suspect-case definition you would post on the wall of a rural health centre in three short bullet points a busy nurse can apply in 30 seconds.
2. A facility screened 200 suspects this quarter and parasitologically confirmed 4. Compute the positivity rate. Give one interpretation if the same facility had screened only 20 and confirmed 4.
3. A nurse wants to start treatment on a patient with a positive RDT but no confirmation because the patient lives far away and may not return. List the risks of treating, and propose one operationally realistic alternative.
