# Lesson 1 — gHAT and the DRC Surveillance Landscape

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you will be able to:
- **Describe** the gambiense human African trypanosomiasis (gHAT) transmission cycle and its two clinical stages.
- **Explain** why the Democratic Republic of the Congo carries the largest share of the global gHAT burden and what "elimination as a public health problem" (EPHP) means for the country.
- **Identify** how the national programme (PNLTHA-RDC) and the health-zone structure organise surveillance responsibilities from the village up to the national level.

## Prerequisites
- Basic epidemiology vocabulary (incidence, prevalence, case, reservoir, vector).
- No prior knowledge of trypanosomiasis is assumed.

## Content

### Section 1: The disease and its cycle
Human African trypanosomiasis (HAT, sleeping sickness) is caused by protozoan parasites of the genus *Trypanosoma* transmitted by tsetse flies (*Glossina*). Two forms exist. The **gambiense** form (*T. b. gambiense*, gHAT) occurs in West and Central Africa, is chronic, and accounts for the overwhelming majority of reported cases. The **rhodesiense** form (East/Southern Africa) is acute and zoonotic. This course is about **gHAT**, the form relevant to the DRC.

The gHAT cycle: a tsetse fly takes a blood meal from an infected human, ingests trypanosomes, the parasite develops in the fly over ~3 weeks, and the fly then inoculates a new human at a subsequent bite. Humans are the principal reservoir for gHAT (with a possible minor animal reservoir under investigation), which is precisely why human-focused case finding and treatment can drive transmission down.

The clinical course has two stages:
- **Stage 1 (haemolymphatic):** parasites in blood and lymph — fever, headache, lymphadenopathy (the classic posterior-cervical "Winterbottom's sign").
- **Stage 2 (meningoencephalitic):** parasites cross into cerebrospinal fluid — sleep-cycle disruption, neuropsychiatric signs, and, untreated, death.

Staging historically required a lumbar puncture, because treatment differed by stage. The arrival of **fexinidazole** (an oral drug effective across both stages in many patients) is changing this picture — a point we return to in Lesson 4.

> **DRC-grounded example.** In a Kwilu province health zone, a 34-year-old cassava farmer presents with three months of intermittent fever, weight loss, and a swollen neck gland. A passive-surveillance nurse who knows the gHAT case definition recognises Winterbottom's sign, performs a rapid test, and refers for confirmation. That single act of recognition is the front line of surveillance.

### Section 2: Why the DRC matters
The DRC reports roughly **70% of all gHAT cases worldwide** in recent years. The disease is focal: transmission clusters in specific riverine and forest-edge foci where tsetse, people, and historical transmission overlap — provinces such as Kwilu, Kwango, Mai-Ndombe, Bandundu, Kasai, and parts of the former Equateur and Orientale provinces. National counts have fallen dramatically — from tens of thousands of cases per year around 1998 to under a thousand per year recently — but the DRC is the country that will determine whether the global EPHP target is met.

WHO defines two milestones for gHAT:
- **Elimination as a public health problem (EPHP)** — targeted for 2030, broadly fewer than 1 reported case per 10,000 people per year in at least 90% of endemic foci, plus a national case ceiling.
- **Interruption of transmission (EOT)** — zero new indigenous cases — an aspiration beyond EPHP.

Crump et al. (2024, *Parasites & Vectors*) modelled the DRC elimination timeline and warned that **cryptic (undetected) transmission** — asymptomatic carriers and a possible animal reservoir — can keep transmission smouldering even when reported cases approach zero. The practical lesson for a field epidemiologist: low reported numbers are not the same as zero transmission, and surveillance must get *more* sensitive, not less, as you approach the target.

### Section 3: How surveillance is organised in the DRC
The DRC health system is decentralised into **provinces → health zones (zones de santé) → health areas (aires de santé) → health facilities and villages**. The health zone is the operational management unit — typically 100,000–300,000 people, a general reference hospital, and a network of health centres.

gHAT surveillance is coordinated by the national programme historically known as **PNLTHA** (Programme National de Lutte contre la Trypanosomiase Humaine Africaine). It runs two complementary engines that the rest of this course unpacks:
- **Passive surveillance** — fixed health facilities detect patients who come on their own (Lesson 2).
- **Active case finding** — mobile teams travel to at-risk villages and screen the population (Lesson 3).

Layered on top are **vector surveillance and tsetse control** (Lesson 6), a **data pipeline** feeding DHIS2 and the WHO HAT Atlas (Lesson 5), and an **analysis and verification** function (Lessons 7–8). As a field epidemiologist, your job sits at the seam between these engines: making sure cases are found, counted correctly, reported on time, and acted on.

## Summary
- gHAT is a chronic, tsetse-transmitted, human-reservoir disease with two clinical stages; this course is about the gambiense form relevant to the DRC.
- The DRC carries about 70% of the global gHAT burden and is the pivotal country for the 2030 EPHP target.
- As cases fall, cryptic transmission means surveillance must become more sensitive — low numbers are not proof of zero transmission.
- Surveillance is organised through the health-zone structure under the national programme (PNLTHA-RDC), combining passive detection, active case finding, vector control, and a data/verification pipeline.
