# Lesson 3 — Active Case Finding with Mobile Teams

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you will be able to:
- **Apply** the logic of mobile-team active screening to choose which villages to visit and when.
- **Plan** a single active-screening round, including the screen-confirm workflow in the field.
- **Compute** screening coverage and yield for a target village and interpret both figures.

## Prerequisites
- Lesson 2 (case definition and screen-confirm diagnostic algorithm).
- Basic arithmetic of proportions.

## Content

### Section 1: Why active case finding exists
Active case finding (ACF) sends a **mobile team** into at-risk communities to screen people who feel well, because gHAT carriers can be infectious for months while asymptomatic or only mildly symptomatic. ACF is the historic engine that drove DRC case numbers down from the late 1990s. It is powerful but **expensive per case found**, and its efficiency collapses as prevalence falls — screening 5,000 people to find zero cases is a poor use of a team that could instead support passive surveillance in several facilities.

The field epidemiologist's judgement is therefore: **where is active screening still worth it?** The answer is risk-targeted ACF — concentrate teams on villages with recent cases, historical foci, tsetse presence, and poor facility access, rather than blanket-screening everywhere.

### Section 2: Planning a screening round
A round has predictable building blocks:
- **Target selection.** Use the village-level history: cases in the last 1–3 years, proximity to a known focus, distance to the nearest testing facility, and prior screening coverage. A village last screened five years ago near a focus with a recent case is a higher priority than one screened last year with none.
- **Advance mobilisation.** Local leaders and community health workers announce the visit so people gather on the day — coverage depends almost entirely on turnout.
- **The screening station.** On the day, the team serologically screens attendees (CATT or RDT). Seropositives go through the same staircase as Lesson 2: parasitological confirmation (lymph aspirate, blood concentration), and staging/treatment for confirmed cases. The difference from passive surveillance is volume and setting, not the diagnostic logic.
- **Referral and follow-up.** Seropositive-but-unconfirmed individuals may be asked to return or be followed, because some are early infections; this is a real operational headache in mobile settings.

> **DRC-grounded example.** A mobile team in Kwango plans a round of 6 villages over 5 days. Village A had 2 confirmed cases last year and 1,200 residents; the team prioritises it for a full day. Villages D and E were screened last year with zero positives and sit far from any focus; the team drops them this round and reallocates the time to a previously unscreened hamlet near the river.

### Section 3: Coverage and yield
Two numbers summarise a round at the village level.

**Screening coverage** — what fraction of the target population you actually screened:
> coverage = (people screened) / (estimated eligible population)

Low coverage (say under 60–70%) means the people who *stayed home* may be exactly those still transmitting — coverage is the single biggest threat to ACF validity.

**Yield (detection rate)** — how many confirmed cases per screened:
> yield = (confirmed cases) / (people screened)

Yield is usually expressed per 1,000 or per 10,000 screened. Falling yield over successive rounds is the expected and *desired* signal of declining transmission — but only if coverage stayed high. Falling yield with falling coverage tells you nothing reassuring.

> **Worked example.** A village of 1,500 eligible residents: the team screens 1,050 and confirms 3 cases.
> Coverage = 1,050 / 1,500 = 70%. Yield = 3 / 1,050 ≈ 2.9 per 1,000 screened.
> Interpretation: coverage is acceptable but not high; the 450 unscreened residents are a real blind spot, so a follow-up or a return visit for absentees is warranted before concluding the village is low-risk.

### Section 4: When to step down active screening
As foci approach elimination, programmes shift from routine ACF toward **reactive screening** — triggered by a passively detected case — plus strengthened passive surveillance. Knowing when a focus has crossed that threshold is a surveillance-evaluation question we return to in Lesson 8.

## Summary
- Active case finding uses mobile teams to detect asymptomatic and early carriers; it is powerful but costly and loses efficiency as prevalence falls.
- Plan rounds by risk-targeting villages, mobilising the community in advance, and running the standard screen-confirm staircase in the field.
- Coverage (fraction screened) and yield (confirmed per screened) interpret a round; low coverage undermines any reassurance from low yield.
- Near elimination, routine ACF gives way to reactive screening plus stronger passive surveillance.

## Exercises
1. You have one mobile team for one week and a list of 10 villages with their last-screening date, distance to a focus, and recent case counts. Describe the three criteria you would rank them on and pick your top 3.
2. A round screened 800 of an estimated 1,000 eligible people and confirmed 1 case. Compute coverage and yield (per 1,000). Write one sentence on whether you would call this village low-risk.
3. Yield in a focus has dropped from 5/1,000 to 0.5/1,000 over three years, but coverage fell from 85% to 45% over the same period. What can and cannot you conclude about transmission?
