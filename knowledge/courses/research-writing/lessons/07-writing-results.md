# Writing the Results

## Learning objectives
- Organize results in a sequence that mirrors the research question and analysis plan.
- Report estimates, uncertainty, and denominators clearly.
- Use tables and figures to reduce cognitive load rather than duplicate text.
- Avoid interpretive drift that belongs in the discussion.

## Prerequisites
- Writing the methods.
- Basic biostatistics.

## Content

### Section 1: The job of the results section
The results section should tell the reader what was found, in what order, and with what degree of precision. It is not the place to re-argue the study rationale or to explain what the findings mean for policy. Its job is narrower and more disciplined: present the evidence cleanly.

Strong results sections feel inevitable. The reader can move from participant flow, to descriptive characteristics, to main estimates, to secondary or sensitivity analyses without wondering why each element appears when it does.

### Section 2: Follow the logic of the question
The simplest organizing principle is to mirror the study aim and methods. If the paper has one primary question and two secondary questions, the results should be structured in the same order. This gives the reader a stable map.

A common sequence is:

- sample or data flow
- baseline or descriptive summaries
- primary analysis
- secondary analyses
- sensitivity analyses or subgroup analyses

For surveillance evaluations, the sequence may instead be:

- system coverage and reporting structure
- data completeness and timeliness
- attribute-level results
- triangulation with interviews or document review

The exact order depends on the study, but the principle is constant: results should answer the question, not merely list outputs.

### Section 3: Start with what was analyzed
Readers need to know what actually made it into the analysis. This means reporting the study denominator, exclusions, missing records, and any mismatch between the planned and realized sample.

For quantitative papers, that often includes:

- number of observations screened
- exclusions and reasons
- final analytic sample
- missing data by key variable

For systematic reviews, it includes search yield, deduplication, title and abstract screening, full-text exclusions, and final included studies. For surveillance evaluations, it may include numbers of facilities, districts, or reporting units assessed.

If readers cannot tell what data underlie the analysis, every later estimate becomes harder to trust.

### Section 4: Report numbers with context
Epidemiology readers need more than p-values. They need counts, denominators, estimates, and uncertainty. Good practice is to report absolute numbers alongside proportions or rates whenever possible.

For example, "reporting completeness was 78%" is weaker than "reporting completeness was 78% (187/240 expected weekly reports)." Likewise, an odds ratio is clearer when readers can see the underlying event frequencies.

Where relevant, include:

- numerators and denominators
- means with standard deviations or medians with interquartile ranges
- effect estimates with confidence intervals
- p-values only as a supplement to estimates

This is especially important in small studies, where statistical significance can be unstable and absolute counts matter a great deal.

### Section 5: Worked example
Imagine a paper evaluating a post-elimination disease surveillance system in 32 peripheral facilities.

A strong results sequence could be:

1. State that 32 of 36 eligible facilities were assessed and explain why 4 were excluded.
2. Describe system characteristics, such as staff training, register availability, and referral pathways.
3. Present reporting performance, for example completeness and timeliness over a defined period.
4. Report agreement between facility registers and laboratory confirmation logs.
5. Present interview findings explaining common causes of delayed reporting.
6. End with a short sensitivity analysis, such as excluding facilities with incomplete archival registers.

Notice that interpretation is still limited. The results show the pattern; the discussion later explains its meaning.

### Section 6: Tables and figures should do real work
Tables and figures should carry information that would be cumbersome in prose. A good table can summarize facility characteristics, regression coefficients, or surveillance indicators across districts. A good figure can show time trends, flow diagrams, or maps.

Common mistakes include:

- repeating every table cell in the text
- including tables with no clear analytic purpose
- hiding key results in supplementary files
- using graphics that look attractive but obscure the numbers

The prose should guide the reader to the important pattern in the table or figure, not duplicate it line by line.

### Section 7: Avoid interpretive drift
Results sections often become weaker when authors slip into explanation or advocacy. Sentences such as "this demonstrates that the system is inadequate" or "these findings confirm the urgent need for reform" belong in the discussion unless the statement is purely descriptive and directly supported by the result just presented.

Interpretive drift matters because it blurs the boundary between observation and judgment. Readers should be able to see the factual result before they encounter your interpretation of it.

### Section 8: Report secondary analyses honestly
Subgroup and sensitivity analyses can strengthen a paper, but only if they are reported transparently. Readers need to know whether they were pre-specified or exploratory, why the subgroup matters, and whether multiple testing might change confidence in the findings.

Secondary analyses should not overshadow the primary result. If the main finding is modest and uncertain, it is misleading to shift attention entirely to an interesting subgroup.

## Key takeaways
- Results should follow the logic of the question and the methods, not the order in which outputs were produced.
- Readers need counts, denominators, estimates, and uncertainty, not just p-values.
- Tables and figures should reduce cognitive load and carry information efficiently.
- The results section reports what was found; interpretation belongs mainly in the discussion.
- Secondary analyses are useful only when they are clearly labeled and proportionately presented.

## Self-check questions
1. Why should a results section usually begin with participant or data flow?
2. What is missing if a paper reports only percentages without counts or denominators?
3. How does interpretive drift weaken the results section?
4. What makes a table useful rather than decorative?
5. Why should subgroup analyses be labeled clearly as pre-specified or exploratory?
6. What is one sign that the sequence of results does not match the study question well?

## Answer key
1. Because readers first need to know what data or participants actually entered the analysis.
2. The reader cannot judge scale, precision, or the stability of the estimate.
3. It mixes observation with interpretation, making it harder to separate evidence from author judgment.
4. It organizes important information more clearly than prose and supports the argument of the paper.
5. Because exploratory findings generally deserve more caution than analyses planned in advance.
6. The section jumps between unrelated outputs without a clear path from primary to secondary findings.
