# Budget Impact and Affordability

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish cost-effectiveness from affordability.
- Explain what budget impact analysis contributes.
- Recognize why scale-up feasibility matters.

## Prerequisites
- Cost-effectiveness analysis.

## Content
An intervention can be cost-effective and still unaffordable. Cost-effectiveness asks whether the health gain is good value for the resources used. Budget impact asks whether the payer can actually absorb the total cost within a planning period.

This is especially important for public health programmes that require national rollout, commodity procurement, workforce expansion, or ongoing maintenance. Decision-makers need both efficiency and affordability evidence. A strategy that is efficient in theory may still be unrealistic if the initial budget shock is too large.

## Key takeaways
- Cost-effective does not mean affordable.
- Budget impact analysis supports implementation planning.
- Scale and timing of expenditure matter for real-world decisions.

## Self-check questions
1. Why can an intervention be cost-effective but unaffordable?
2. What is the main purpose of budget impact analysis?
3. What type of decision especially needs budget impact evidence?
4. Why does rollout scale matter?
5. What happens if analysts ignore affordability?

## Answer key
1. Because total upfront cost may exceed available budgets.
2. To estimate the fiscal consequences of adopting an intervention.
3. National or large-scale implementation decisions.
4. Because total expenditure depends on how many people or sites are covered.
5. Policymakers may endorse options they cannot actually fund.
