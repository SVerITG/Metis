# Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

## Learning objectives
- Explain the logic of incremental cost-effectiveness analysis.
- Define comparator, incremental cost, incremental effect, and ICER.
- Recognize common errors in reading cost-effectiveness results.

## Prerequisites
- Measuring health outcomes for economic evaluation.

## Content
Cost-effectiveness analysis compares the additional cost of an option with the additional health benefit it produces relative to a comparator. The comparator is critical. An intervention can look attractive against doing nothing but less attractive against an already efficient alternative.

The incremental cost-effectiveness ratio, or ICER, divides additional cost by additional effect. But the ICER only makes sense when readers understand the comparator, outcome, and assumptions. Dominated options, which cost more and achieve less, should generally be rejected immediately.

## Key takeaways
- Cost-effectiveness is always relative to a comparator.
- Incremental thinking matters more than average cost alone.
- ICERs need context to be interpreted correctly.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is the comparator central in cost-effectiveness analysis?
2. What does an ICER describe?
3. What is a dominated strategy?
4. Why can average cost be misleading?
5. What should readers check before trusting an ICER?

## Answer key
1. Because value depends on what realistic alternative is being replaced.
2. The extra cost per extra unit of health gain.
3. An option that costs more and performs worse than another option.
4. Because decisions are about marginal differences between options.
5. The comparator, outcome measure, assumptions, and perspective.
