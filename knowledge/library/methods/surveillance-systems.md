# Surveillance Systems

> Reference card — WHO frameworks, surveillance types, evaluation, digital surveillance.

---

## WHO Frameworks

### International Health Regulations (IHR 2005)
- **Purpose:** Prevent, protect against, control and respond to international spread of disease
- **Core capacities:** 13 areas including surveillance, laboratory, risk communication, points of entry
- **Notification:** Potential Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) — assessed via decision instrument (Annex 2)
- **Monitoring:** Joint External Evaluation (JEE), State Party Annual Report (SPAR)

### Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response (IDSR)
- **Purpose:** Strengthen national surveillance in African region (WHO AFRO)
- **Principle:** Single integrated system for priority diseases rather than vertical silos
- **Components:** Case detection, reporting, analysis, response, feedback
- **Priority diseases:** Country-specific list; includes epidemic-prone, diseases targeted for elimination, diseases of public health importance
- **Reporting:** Immediate (epidemic-prone) + weekly/monthly (routine)

### PHI Competency Framework
- **Purpose:** Define core competencies for public health intelligence
- **Domains:** Surveillance, risk assessment, risk communication, applied epidemiology

### COHFE (Competencies for One Health Field Epidemiology)
- **Purpose:** Competency framework bridging human, animal, and environmental health surveillance
- **Application:** Training programmes, workforce development for One Health epidemiologists

## Surveillance Types

### Indicator-Based Surveillance (IBS)
- **Definition:** Systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of structured data from defined sources
- **Sources:** Health facilities, laboratories, vital registration, sentinel sites
- **Flow:** Case definition -> detection -> notification -> compilation -> analysis -> action
- **Examples:** Notifiable disease reporting, sentinel influenza surveillance, lab-based AMR surveillance

### Event-Based Surveillance (EBS)
- **Definition:** Organized collection, monitoring, assessment of unstructured information about health events
- **Sources:** Media reports, community rumours, social media, ProMED, other informal sources
- **Process:** Detection -> triage (filtering) -> verification -> risk assessment -> response
- **Strengths:** Earlier detection than IBS; captures events outside health system
- **Challenges:** High noise, verification burden, sustainability

### Relationship
- IBS and EBS are complementary — WHO recommends both for comprehensive early warning
- IBS provides systematic baseline; EBS provides early signals and captures gaps

## Evaluation of Surveillance Systems

### CDC Framework (Updated Guidelines for Evaluating Public Health Surveillance Systems, 2001/2004)
Key attributes to evaluate:

| Attribute | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Simplicity** | Ease of operation; minimal complexity for objectives |
| **Flexibility** | Ability to adapt to changing needs |
| **Data quality** | Completeness, validity of data collected |
| **Acceptability** | Willingness of participants to contribute |
| **Sensitivity** | Proportion of cases detected; ability to detect outbreaks |
| **Positive predictive value** | Proportion of reported cases that are true cases |
| **Representativeness** | Accuracy in describing occurrence by person, place, time |
| **Timeliness** | Speed between steps in the system |
| **Stability** | Reliability and availability of the system |

### Evaluation Steps
1. Engage stakeholders
2. Describe the system (purpose, operation, resources)
3. Focus the evaluation design
4. Gather credible evidence on attributes
5. Justify and share conclusions and recommendations

## Digital Surveillance Tools

| Tool | Type | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **ProMED** | EBS | Program for Monitoring Emerging Diseases (ISID); moderated email reports since 1994 |
| **HealthMap** | EBS | Automated aggregation of online sources; real-time disease intelligence |
| **EIOS** | EBS | Epidemic Intelligence from Open Sources (WHO); aggregates and triages open-source signals |
| **BlueDot** | AI-powered | Machine learning for early detection and tracking of outbreaks |

| **Go.Data** | Outbreak | WHO tool for outbreak investigation — case management, contact tracing, transmission chains |
| **SORMAS** | Outbreak | Surveillance Outbreak Response Management and Analysis System; used in Africa |

## Post-Elimination Surveillance Challenges

- **Declining vigilance:** Health workers less familiar with rare diseases; index of suspicion drops
- **Reduced funding:** Difficult to justify surveillance investment when cases approach zero
- **Maintaining sensitivity:** Need high sensitivity with very low prevalence — costly per case detected
- **Integration dilemma:** Vertical programmes efficient pre-elimination but unsustainable; integration into routine systems risks losing sensitivity
- **Population movement:** Cross-border importation risk; need regional coordination
- **Diagnostics:** May need higher-performance diagnostics as prevalence drops (higher PPV threshold)
- **Example (elimination-phase disease):** Transition from active screening (mobile teams) to passive surveillance integrated into peripheral health facilities; rapid diagnostic deployment critical

## Surveillance Data Quality Indicators

- **Completeness:** % of expected reports received
- **Timeliness:** % of reports received within deadline
- **Consistency:** Internal consistency of data fields
- **Case confirmation rate:** % of suspected cases with lab confirmation
- **Zero reporting:** Facilities that report "zero cases" vs those that simply don't report

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **WHO PHI framework launch:** WHO published the **Public Health Intelligence Competency Framework on 27 November 2025** and the companion **curriculum on 26 November 2025**, organizing PHI around the technical functions **detect, verify, analyse, assess** plus collaborative information exchange.
- **PHI is now explicitly a workforce tool:** WHO's **3 December 2025** launch webinar framed the PHI materials as tools for recruitment, self-assessment, curriculum design, and competency-based workforce development.
- **EIOS remains operationally important:** WHO's 2025 technical-meeting reporting showed continued investment in Epidemic Intelligence from Open Sources, confirming that event-based surveillance is still a live implementation area rather than a COVID-era artifact.
- **Pandemic governance changed materially:** The **WHO Pandemic Agreement was adopted on 20 May 2025**, so preparedness and surveillance discussions should now be framed around implementation rather than negotiation.
- **Digital health governance was extended:** On **23 May 2025**, the World Health Assembly endorsed extension of WHO's Global Strategy on Digital Health to **2027**, explicitly linking digital systems, AI, interoperability, and public-health capacity.

## Practical Examples

- **Republic of Korea and EIOS:** Kim et al. (2025) assessed WHO's EIOS platform in the Republic of Korea, giving a practical event-based surveillance example outside the usual generic framework discussions.
- **District surveillance evaluation:** Alemu et al. (2019) used the CDC attribute framework to evaluate Ethiopia's Dangila district system, illustrating how usefulness, timeliness, and acceptability can be assessed in routine field settings.
- **Open-source epidemic databases:** Badker et al. (2025) showed how open-source digital biosurveillance can be structured into a reusable epidemic database, connecting event-based signal detection to analytical infrastructure.
- **Evaluation methods themselves have been compared:** Calba et al. (2015) is useful when deciding whether CDC-style attribute assessment is enough or whether a broader systems-evaluation approach is needed.
