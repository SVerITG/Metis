# Human African Trypanosomiasis (rhodesiense) — Annex Disease Summary

## Disease and epidemiology

- Rhodesiense human African trypanosomiasis (rHAT) is caused by infection with *Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense* and accounts for approximately **2% of all HAT cases**.
- rHAT is a **zoonosis** (of wild and domestic animals) that occasionally affects humans — distinct from gHAT, which is primarily a human disease.
- rHAT causes **acute infection** which rapidly develops:
  - **Haemolymphatic stage:** bouts of fever, chancre at the infection site, headaches, joint pains, frequently cardiac and renal symptoms
  - **Neurological stage:** sleep disturbances, behavioural changes, confusion, sensory and motor disturbances — when the parasite crosses the blood–brain barrier
- HAT is mainly transmitted through the bite of an infected tsetse fly of the genus *Glossina*; other modes include mother-to-child transmission.

## Core strategic interventions

| Intervention type | Details |
|---|---|
| Preventive chemotherapy | N/A |
| WASH | Safe water supply to reduce contact with tsetse flies |
| Vector control | Insecticide spraying of livestock; baits and traps including impregnated screens; sterile insect release |
| Veterinary public health | Treatment of animals (cattle, pigs); restricted application of insecticides |
| Case management | **Suramin** (haemolymphatic stage); **melarsoprol** (neurological stage) |
| Case finding | Mainly through **passive screening** (clinical suspects attending health facilities) — differs from gHAT's active screening approach |

**Note on treatment contrast with gHAT:** rHAT relies on suramin and melarsoprol (the latter with significant toxicity) rather than the newer, safer medicines available for gHAT (fexinidazole, eflornithine + nifurtimox).

## WHO 2030 target: Elimination as a public health problem

Target definition: **<1 case per 10,000 people per year in each health district of the country, averaged over the previous 5-year period**

| Indicator | 2020 (provisional) | 2023 | 2025 | 2030 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Countries validated for elimination as a public health problem | 0 (0%) | 2 (15%) | 4 (31%) | **8 (61%)** |
| Area with >1 HAT case per 10,000/year (5-year average) | 10,000 km² | 5,000 km² | 2,000 km² | **0 (100%)** |

*Note: Target for rHAT is elimination as a public health problem (not interruption of transmission, which is the target for gHAT). This reflects the zoonotic nature of rHAT — complete elimination of transmission from animal reservoirs is not feasible.*

## Risks that require mitigation

1. Challenges with integration of activities into a weak health system
2. Cessation of activities in low-prevalence settings

## Summary of critical actions to achieve targets

- Develop new field-adapted tools to detect rHAT (e.g. rapid diagnostic test, RDT) for use in primary health care facilities
- Develop safe and effective treatment (current treatment — especially melarsoprol — has significant toxicity)
- Integrate control and surveillance into national health systems
- Strengthen capacity and awareness in endemic countries
