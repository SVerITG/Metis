# Human African Trypanosomiasis (gambiense) — Annex Disease Summary

## Disease and epidemiology

- Gambiense human African trypanosomiasis (gHAT) is caused by infection with *Trypanosoma brucei gambiense* and accounts for **98% of all HAT cases**.
- gHAT affects mainly humans but the parasite can also be found in animals.
- gHAT causes **chronic infection in two stages**:
  - **Haemolymphatic stage:** bouts of fever, headaches, joint pains, and frequently enlarged lymph nodes
  - **Neurological stage:** sleep disturbances, behavioural changes, confusion, sensory and motor disturbances — when the parasite crosses the blood–brain barrier
- HAT is mainly transmitted through the bite of an infected tsetse fly of the genus *Glossina*; other modes include mother-to-child transmission.

## Burden of disease

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Reported cases (2019) | Fewer than **1,000** |
| People at risk | **53 million** |
| Endemic countries | **24** |
| Geographic distribution | Sub-Saharan Africa |

Number of gHAT cases declined dramatically from ~8,000 in 2009 to <1,000 by 2019 — an exceptional public health achievement.

## Progress against WHO 2020 targets

| Impact indicator | 2020 target | Current status |
|---|---|---|
| Number of HAT cases declared (global elimination as a public health problem) | <2,000 | **<1,000** (target exceeded) |

## Core strategic interventions

| Intervention type | Details |
|---|---|
| Preventive chemotherapy | N/A |
| WASH | Safe water supply to reduce contact with tsetse flies |
| Vector control | Baits and traps including impregnated screens |
| Veterinary public health | Treatment of animals (cattle, pigs); restricted application of insecticides |
| Case management | Pentamidine (haemolymphatic stage); eflornithine + nifurtimox or fexinidazole (neurological stage / combined); fexinidazole (haemolymphatic and not severe neurological stage) |
| Case finding | Active (mobile units visiting villages, screening whole population) and passive screening (clinical suspects attending health facilities) |

## Risks that require mitigation

1. Inability to screen and treat due to conflict and political instability in the most affected country (primarily DRC)
2. Lack of integration of activities into a weak health system
3. Asymptomatic infections and animal reservoirs as elimination is approached — could lead to resurgence
4. Reduction in surveillance once zero cases are reported locally, or cessation of activities in low-prevalence settings

## Summary of critical actions to achieve targets

- Integrate control and surveillance activities in the peripheral health system; identify and prepare sentinel sites for surveillance post-elimination
- Develop a long-term funding plan, including campaigns for resource mobilization
- Reinforce ownership of elimination and targets by endemic countries through advocacy to health authorities and heads of state (context of decreasing case numbers)
- Support development of effective treatments and specific, cost-effective diagnostics; adapt treatment and vector control to low-prevalence settings

## Detailed assessment of actions required

### Technical progress

| Dimension | Current status | Actions required |
|---|---|---|
| Scientific understanding | Key gaps in transmission knowledge (latent infections in humans, role of animal reservoirs); epidemiological situation not well known in some geographical areas | Evaluate role of epidemiological elements (latent infections, skin as reservoir, animal reservoirs); fill gaps; monitor environmental and climate changes; understand prevalence in regions with low/limited surveillance |
| Diagnostics | Screening with serological tests; parasite confirmation in blood, lymph nodes or CSF; determining stage by CSF from lumbar puncture; current screening imperfect; confirmation tools cumbersome; limited tools to assess absence of disease. DiTECT, FIND, IRD, ITM initiatives developing new tools | Develop field-adapted diagnostic/detection tools (e.g. simplified diagnostic not requiring confirmatory microscopy); ensure independent multicentre evaluation of new tools |
| Effective intervention | Case-finding (active and passive) is main activity; current interventions effective but need adaptation to evolving epidemiological situation; vector control tools demonstrated utility; trials for new simpler medicines ongoing | Adapt interventions to low/very-low prevalence scenario; develop safe/efficient single oral dose for both stages (e.g. acoziborole); develop paediatric formulation for children under 6 |

### Strategy and service delivery

| Dimension | Current status | Actions required |
|---|---|---|
| Operational and normative guidance | Global strategy for elimination defined; operational guidelines defined for different settings; verification of HAT elimination not yet fully developed | Develop and adopt guidance for assessing elimination as interruption of transmission; create guidance for targeting vector control activities; consider diagnostic algorithms with lower specificity if safer treatments become available |
| Planning, governance and programme implementation | HAT control led by National Sleeping Sickness Control Programmes (NSSCPs) with WHO network for HAT elimination coordinating stakeholders | Participate in advocacy for universal health coverage; countries need to integrate control and surveillance into strengthened national health systems |
| Monitoring and evaluation | HAT Atlas is helpful tool for planning and monitoring; global indicators and validation methods for elimination as a public health problem available | Use distribution data and case mapping tools to improve targeting of case-finding; develop high-throughput test to assess elimination and post-elimination surveillance; reinforce surveillance through sentinel sites with trained staff and equipment |
| Access and logistics | Access to treatment 100% ensured by manufacturer donation and WHO distribution until 2020; access to screening and diagnosis not ensured; distribution of diagnostic tools not systematic | Ensure availability and access of HAT diagnostic tools through involvement of manufacturers; better understand population screened coverage to focus on population at risk |
| Health care infrastructure and workforce | Decreasing HAT-skilled staff and decreasing prevalence makes experience difficult to gain; challenging integration of control/surveillance into weak health systems | Integrate control and surveillance activities in peripheral health system; identify and prepare sentinel sites; develop national plans for staff training, awareness and motivation |

### Enablers

| Dimension | Current status | Actions required |
|---|---|---|
| Advocacy and funding | Important funding (Belgian Government, Sanofi, Bayer, Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation) guaranteed for next 2–5 years; long-term support uncertain; ownership by endemic countries is weak | Develop long-term funding plan; maintain current support to avoid donor fatigue; reinforce country ownership through advocacy at highest levels (e.g. PATTEC) |
| Collaboration and multisectoral action | WHO network for HAT elimination provides coordination framework; interface with animal trypanosomiasis through FAO and PAAT; collaboration with some other NTD programmes | Enhance cross-border collaboration; ensure WHO coordination; establish collaboration with malaria programme on diagnosis and vector control; integrate interventions between zoonotic and anthropomorphic trypanosomiasis (One Health) |
| Capacity and awareness building | International Course on African Trypanosomiasis (ICAT) covers key capacity building; efforts of IRD, ITM, FIND, DNDi, Makerere University in coordination with NSSCPs | Capacity-building (cascade training/retraining) for treatment services; develop training to transition HAT expertise from specialized programmes into national health systems |
