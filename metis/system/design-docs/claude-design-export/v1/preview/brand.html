Construction

An owl's gaze, drawn as a stamp

  • Two concentric index rings — the outer catalog, the inner shelf.
  • Four compass axes — the bearings of a reader who knows the room.
  • A central pupil — the fix, the cursor, the point where Metis is paying attention.
  • 1.5 px stroke, round caps — drawn by the same hand as the iconography.
  • Minimum 24 px; clear-space = half the mark on every side.

Principles

01

A workbench, not a feed.

Metis is organized like a working archive — shelves, index cards, slipcases. There is no scroll of demands.

02

Labels before icons.

Where other tools reach for a glyph, Metis reaches for an UPPERCASE mono label. The word is the signifier.

03

Offer, don't prompt.

Metis observes first: "Two of yesterday's threads are converging." Offers are a short list of verbs underneath.

04

Rules over shadows.

The page is paper. Hierarchy is typographic. Shadows exist but are barely there. Edges do the work.

Origin

"Of all the gods, the wisest by far was Metis — she who knew the way of every thing."
— Hesiod, loosely

The Metis of the myth is the first wife of Zeus and the mother of Athena — wisdom, counsel, cunning. She is swallowed, and her wisdom becomes interior. That inheritance is the product's posture: a presence inside your work that knows the shelves, keeps the cards in order, and speaks — when it speaks — from the margin.
