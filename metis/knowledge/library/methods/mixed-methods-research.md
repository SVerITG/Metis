# Mixed Methods Research

> Reference card — core designs, integration strategies, and when quantitative alone is not enough.

---

## Core Designs

### Sequential Explanatory
- **Quant -> QUAL:** Quantitative phase first, then qualitative phase to explain or elaborate results
- Example: Survey identifies unexpected low treatment adherence; follow-up interviews explore barriers
- Qualitative sample often purposively selected from quantitative participants

### Sequential Exploratory
- **QUAL -> Quant:** Qualitative phase first to explore concepts, then quantitative phase to test or generalize
- Example: Focus groups identify community perceptions of screening; survey measures prevalence of those perceptions
- Often used to develop measurement instruments or interventions

### Concurrent / Convergent
- **Quant + QUAL simultaneously:** Both strands collected in parallel, analyzed separately, then merged
- Example: Survey of health workers and concurrent in-depth interviews analyzed together
- Requires careful planning of integration point

## Purposes of Mixing

- **Triangulation:** Seek convergence of findings from different methods on the same phenomenon
- **Complementarity:** Use one method to elaborate, enhance, or illustrate results from another
- **Expansion:** Extend breadth and range of inquiry by using different methods for different components
- **Development:** Use results from one method to inform the design of the other
- **Initiation:** Discover contradictions that lead to reframing the research question

## Integration Approaches

- **Joint display:** Side-by-side table or matrix showing quantitative results alongside qualitative themes
- **Following a thread:** Trace a finding across both datasets to see where they converge or diverge
- **Merged analysis:** Transform one data type (e.g., quantify qualitative themes) to enable combined analysis
- **Narrative weaving:** Write up findings by weaving both data types within each theme
- Integration should be explicit and planned, not an afterthought

## Connection to Implementation Science

- Mixed methods are central to implementation research (understanding how and why interventions work in context)
- Process evaluations of complex interventions almost always require mixed methods
- MRC framework for complex interventions explicitly recommends mixed methods for feasibility and evaluation

## When Quantitative Alone Is Not Enough

- Need to understand the "why" behind observed patterns
- Context matters: what works, for whom, under what conditions
- Measuring acceptability, feasibility, or appropriateness of interventions
- Studying complex health systems or behavior change
- Community perspectives and lived experience are essential to the research question
- Developing or adapting interventions for new settings

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **Reporting guidance is being updated:** A formal protocol to update the GRAMMS reporting guideline to **GRAMMS 2.0** was published on **17 March 2026**, reflecting how much mixed-methods practice has evolved since the original 2008 framework.
- **Applied teaching guidance remains active:** The **AMEE Guide No. 173** on mixed methods, published in **2025**, reinforces practical issues such as explicit rationale, integration planning, and team capability.
- **The main quality problem is still weak integration:** Recent methodological commentary continues to argue that many studies collect both quantitative and qualitative data but still fail to show clearly how the two strands inform one another.
- **Practical implication:** In 2026, the minimum standard is not "we did interviews and a survey"; it is a defensible rationale for mixing, an explicit integration point, and a reporting structure that makes the added value visible.

## Practical Examples

- **Implementation studies in emergency care:** Stolldorf et al. (2025) used a mixed-methods design to study barriers and facilitators to implementation work, showing how interviews and survey data can guide strategy refinement.
- **Workforce and service-delivery studies:** Molini Pennacchia et al. (2025) combined qualitative and quantitative evidence to understand workforce practices, illustrating mixed methods for complex service problems.
- **Process evaluations of complex interventions:** Mixed methods are often the most defensible design when outcome data alone cannot explain uptake, fidelity, or contextual variation across sites.
- **Surveillance and community engagement:** In public-health programmes, mixed methods are especially useful when coverage, acceptability, feasibility, and operational bottlenecks all matter at once.
