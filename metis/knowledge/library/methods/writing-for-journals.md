# Writing for Journals

> Reference card — reporting guidelines, manuscript structure, journal selection, and navigating peer review.

---

## EQUATOR Network and Reporting Guidelines

- **EQUATOR (Enhancing the QUAlity and Transparency Of health Research):** Central repository of reporting guidelines
- Key guidelines by study type:
  - **STROBE:** Observational studies (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional)
  - **CONSORT:** Randomized controlled trials
  - **PRISMA:** Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
  - **RECORD:** Routinely collected health data (extension of STROBE)
  - **STARD:** Diagnostic accuracy studies
  - **TRIPOD:** Prediction models (development and validation)
  - **CHEERS:** Health economic evaluations
  - **COREQ:** Qualitative research
  - **SPIRIT:** Trial protocols
- Use the checklist during writing, not after; improves quality from the start
- Many journals require completed checklists at submission

## IMRaD Structure

- **Introduction:** What is known, what is not known, what this study addresses (funnel shape)
- **Methods:** Study design, setting, participants, variables, data sources, statistical analysis; enough detail to replicate
- **Results:** Present findings aligned with methods; tables and figures do the heavy lifting; no interpretation
- **Discussion:** Key findings, comparison with literature, strengths and limitations, implications, future research
- Additional sections: Abstract, Acknowledgments, References, Supplementary material

## Abstract Writing

- **Structured abstract:** Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions — required by most medical journals
- **Unstructured abstract:** Single paragraph; common in some social science and general science journals
- Write the abstract last; it should stand alone
- Include key numbers: sample size, main effect estimate with confidence interval, p-value if relevant
- Stay within word limit (typically 250-350 words)

## Journal Selection Strategy

- **Scope fit:** Does the journal publish this type of study on this topic?
- **Audience:** Who needs to see this work? (specialists, generalists, policymakers)
- **Impact factor:** Consider but do not obsess; field-specific norms vary widely
- **Open access policy:** Gold OA (author pays APC), Green OA (self-archive), hybrid, diamond OA (no APC)
- **Turnaround time:** Check typical time to first decision (some journals publish this)
- **Practical tools:** Jane (Journal/Author Name Estimator), journal finder tools from Elsevier/Springer
- Have a ranked list of 3 journals ready before first submission

## Cover Letter Essentials

- Address to the editor by name if possible
- State the manuscript title and type (original research, review, etc.)
- 2-3 sentences on why this work matters and fits the journal
- Confirm: original work, not under consideration elsewhere, all authors approved, conflicts of interest disclosed
- Suggest (and exclude) reviewers if requested
- Keep it to one page

## Responding to Peer Review

- Read all reviews carefully; take a break before responding
- Create a point-by-point response document
- For each comment: quote the reviewer, describe what you changed (with page/line numbers), or explain respectfully why you did not change
- Never dismiss or argue with reviewers; reframe disagreements constructively
- Track all manuscript changes (use track changes or diff)
- Thank the reviewers and editor for their time

## Preprints

- **medRxiv:** Health sciences preprint server (managed by BMJ, Yale, Cold Spring Harbor)
- **SSRN:** Social sciences, economics, health policy
- **bioRxiv:** Basic biology and some translational science
- Benefits: establish priority, get early feedback, share with funders and policymakers immediately
- Most journals accept papers previously posted as preprints (check journal policy)
- Preprints are not peer-reviewed — always state this when citing

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **ICMJE guidance was updated again in January 2026:** The ICMJE site states that the recommendations were updated with new sections on **AI use in publishing** and **authors' access to data**, plus updates on trial registration, sponsor agreements, and reporting of support.
- **AI-related reporting guidance is more visible:** CONSORT-AI and related extensions are now more clearly surfaced in EQUATOR, reinforcing that AI-assisted trials and tools need design-specific reporting rather than generic novelty framing.
- **Writing standards are becoming more data-explicit:** Journals increasingly expect transparent reporting of data access, analytical code, and AI assistance alongside the traditional IMRaD structure.

## Practical Examples

- **Observational manuscripts:** STROBE plus clear protocol logic remains the baseline for epidemiology papers.
- **Diagnostic manuscripts:** STARD and STARD-AI help prevent underreporting of validation, exclusions, and model workflow.
- **Review papers:** PRISMA 2020 remains essential, especially when the search and screening pipeline is complex or software-assisted.
