# Human African Trypanosomiasis (HAT) — Sleeping Sickness

> Comprehensive reference card — biology, epidemiology, diagnostics, treatment, surveillance.

---

## Biology

### Parasitology
- **Causative agent:** Trypanosoma brucei (kinetoplastid protozoan)
- **Two subspecies causing human disease:**
  - *T.b. gambiense* (gHAT): West and Central Africa; chronic form; ~95% of reported cases
  - *T.b. rhodesiense* (rHAT): East and Southern Africa; acute form; ~5% of reported cases
- **Vector:** Tsetse fly (Glossina spp.) — ~30 species/subspecies; riverine and savanna types
- **Reservoir:**
  - gHAT: Primarily human (debate about animal reservoirs — pigs, dogs as potential cryptic reservoirs)
  - rHAT: Wildlife (bushbuck, hartebeest) and cattle — classic zoonosis
- **T.b. brucei:** Animal-infective only (not human-infective due to trypanolytic factor in human serum)

### Life Cycle
1. Tsetse fly takes blood meal; injects metacyclic trypomastigotes into skin
2. Parasites multiply in blood, lymph, and interstitial spaces (haemolymphatic stage 1)
3. Parasites cross blood-brain barrier into CNS (meningoencephalitic stage 2)
4. Tsetse ingests trypanosomes during blood meal; parasites develop in fly (~3 weeks)

### Antigenic Variation
- Variant Surface Glycoprotein (VSG) coat — parasite switches VSG genes to evade immune response
- Major obstacle to vaccine development (~1,600 VSG genes in genome)
- Causes characteristic relapsing parasitaemia

## Epidemiology

### Current Situation
- **Historical peak:** ~300,000 estimated cases/year in late 1990s (epidemic mainly DRC, Angola, Sudan)
- **Dramatic decline:** ~992 cases reported in 2019; continued decline since
- **WHO milestone:** gHAT validated as eliminated as public health problem in several countries
- **Remaining foci:** Mainly DRC (majority of remaining gHAT cases), also Central African Republic, Chad, Guinea, others
- **rHAT:** Low-level endemic in Uganda, Tanzania, Malawi, Zambia, Zimbabwe — linked to wildlife areas

### Geographic Distribution
- gHAT: West and Central African foci; riverine tsetse habitat
- rHAT: East and Southern African foci; savanna and forest tsetse
- DRC: ~70% of global gHAT cases; multiple active foci (Equateur, Bandundu, Kasai, Maniema, Orientale)

### WHO Targets
- **2020 target (achieved):** Elimination as a public health problem (gHAT): <1 case/10,000/year in >=90% of foci
- **2030 target:** Elimination of transmission (gHAT): zero cases
- **Sustainability challenge:** Maintaining surveillance to reach and verify zero

### Risk Factors
- Living/working near tsetse habitat (rivers, forests, vegetation)
- Agricultural activities, fishing, water collection
- Poverty and remote rural populations
- Conflict and displacement (disrupts surveillance and control)

## Diagnostics

### Screening Tests

| Test | Type | Use | Notes |
|------|------|-----|-------|
| **CATT** (Card Agglutination Test for Trypanosomiasis) | Serological; agglutination of fixed *T.b. gambiense* | Mass screening; field-deployable | Whole blood or dilutions; sensitivity ~87-98%; specificity variable |
| **HAT RDTs** (SD Bioline HAT, others) | Lateral flow immunochromatographic | Point-of-care screening; passive surveillance | Simpler than CATT; single test; no cold chain needed |
| **Trypanolysis test** | Serological; in vitro lysis | Seroprevalence studies; confirmation of serology | Higher specificity than CATT; lab-based only |

### Parasitological Confirmation

| Test | Sensitivity | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| **Lymph node puncture** | Variable (moderate) | From enlarged cervical lymph nodes; wet mount; quick |
| **mAECT** (mini Anion Exchange Centrifugation Technique) | High (~2-5 tryps/mL detection threshold) | Gold standard for blood concentration; field-usable with training |
| **CTC** (Capillary Tube Centrifugation) | Moderate | Quick; buffy coat examination |
| **Quantitative Buffy Coat (QBC)** | Moderate | Fluorescent microscopy |
| **Thick/thin blood film** | Low for gHAT (higher for rHAT) | Low parasitaemia in gHAT limits sensitivity |

### Stage Determination
- **Lumbar puncture required** to determine CNS involvement (stage 1 vs stage 2)
- **Criteria for stage 2:**
  - WBC >5 cells/microL in CSF, AND/OR
  - Trypanosomes in CSF
- **Stage determination drives treatment choice** (historically critical; less so with fexinidazole/acoziborole)

### Emerging Diagnostics
- **LAMP (Loop-mediated isothermal amplification):** Molecular; field-deployable; not yet routine
- **Immune trypanolysis:** For sero-surveillance
- **Discussion:** Moving toward test-and-treat algorithms that reduce need for parasitological confirmation and lumbar puncture

## Treatment

### gHAT Treatment (Historical and Current)

| Drug | Stage | Route | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|-------|
| **Pentamidine** | Stage 1 | IM (7 days) | Standard stage 1 treatment; well-tolerated; available since 1940s |
| **Suramin** | Stage 1 (rHAT) | IV | For rHAT only |
| **Melarsoprol** | Stage 2 | IV | Arsenical; 5% reactive encephalopathy (fatal); phased out for gHAT |
| **Eflornithine** | Stage 2 | IV (14 days, q6h) | Complex regimen; hospitalization required; gHAT only |
| **NECT** (Nifurtimox-Eflornithine Combination Therapy) | Stage 2 | IV eflornithine (7 days) + oral nifurtimox (10 days) | WHO-recommended stage 2 treatment since 2009; simplified eflornithine |
| **Fexinidazole** | Stage 1 and early stage 2 | Oral (10 days) | First all-oral treatment; WHO-recommended since 2019; game-changer for field use; DNDi/Sanofi |
| **Acoziborole** | Stage 1 and stage 2 | Oral (single dose) | Approved 2023; single-dose oral cure; could transform elimination strategy; DNDi development |

### rHAT Treatment
- Stage 1: Suramin (IV)
- Stage 2: Melarsoprol (still required — no alternatives yet approved)
- Fexinidazole: Under evaluation for rHAT

### Treatment Access
- Medicines donated by manufacturers; distributed through WHO
- DNDi (Drugs for Neglected Diseases initiative) central role in drug development

## Surveillance and Control

### Active Screening
- **Mobile teams** visit endemic villages for mass serological screening (CATT or RDT)
- **Sero-positive individuals** undergo parasitological confirmation
- **Historically the backbone** of gHAT control in DRC and Central Africa
- **Declining cost-effectiveness** as prevalence drops — fewer cases per person screened

### Passive Surveillance
- Case detection at health facilities when symptomatic patients present
- Requires RDT availability at peripheral health centres
- Training of health workers to suspect HAT and use RDTs
- Critical for sustainability as active screening scaled down

### Vector Control
- **Tiny targets:** Small insecticide-impregnated blue/black cloth targets; attracts and kills tsetse
- **Cost-effective:** ~$50/km2/year in riverine settings
- **Integration:** Combined with active screening for maximum impact
- **Sterile insect technique (SIT):** Used in limited settings (Senegal); expensive
- **Trapping:** For monitoring tsetse density

### Transition Strategy (Toward 2030)
1. **Phase 1 (attack):** Active screening + vector control in high-burden foci
2. **Phase 2 (consolidation):** Reduced active screening; strengthened passive surveillance; continued vector control
3. **Phase 3 (maintenance/elimination):** Passive surveillance integrated into health system; reactive screening around cases; serological surveillance
4. **Challenge:** Maintaining sensitivity with very low prevalence; cryptic reservoirs; cross-border coordination

## Key Research Questions

- Role of animal reservoirs in sustaining gHAT transmission at very low levels
- Optimal surveillance strategies for post-elimination verification
- Acoziborole implementation: how to operationalize test-and-treat without lumbar puncture
- Cost-effectiveness of alternative surveillance strategies (active vs passive vs reactive)
- Spatial and temporal dynamics of remaining foci
- Sero-positive, parasitologically unconfirmed individuals — what proportion are true infections?

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **Reported burden remains very low:** WHO's **World NTD Day 2026** material states that **583 HAT cases were reported in 2024**, underscoring how close the disease is to the 2030 target while also highlighting the challenge of maintaining sensitivity at very low prevalence.
- **Country validation continued in 2025:** WHO announced that **Guinea** eliminated HAT as a public health problem on **29 January 2025**, and **Kenya** achieved the same milestone on **8 August 2025**.
- **Interruption of transmission has not yet been validated:** WHO's February 2026 messaging notes that, despite multiple country validations and the low case count, **no country had yet validated interruption of gHAT transmission**.
- **Acoziborole advanced again in 2026:** On **27 February 2026**, Sanofi and DNDi announced a **positive CHMP opinion** for acoziborole as a **single-dose, three-tablet oral treatment** for most gHAT patients aged 12 years and older who weigh at least 40 kg.
- **The 2025 Lancet review is now the best recent synthesis:** Lejon, Lindner, and Franco's *Lancet* review from **15 March 2025** is one of the clearest current high-level summaries of biology, diagnostics, treatment, and elimination strategy.

## Practical Examples

- **Guinea elimination milestone:** WHO's **29 January 2025** validation note highlights how active screening, vector control, and programme adaptation through Ebola and COVID-19 supported elimination as a public health problem.
- **Kenya as a rhodesiense example:** WHO's **8 August 2025** announcement is useful because it shows that elimination as a public health problem is possible even in a zoonotic setting.
- **Atlas-based surveillance:** Simarro et al. and Franco et al. remain practical examples of how georeferenced continental surveillance data can support both operational targeting and elimination monitoring.
- **Therapeutic simplification:** Fexinidazole already changed practice by reducing dependence on prolonged inpatient regimens, and acoziborole may simplify this further if regulatory progress translates into country-level implementation.
