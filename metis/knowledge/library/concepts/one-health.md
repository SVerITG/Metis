# One Health

> Reference card — framework, zoonotic interface, tripartite collaboration, applications.

---

## Definition and Framework

### One Health Approach
- **Definition:** An integrated, unifying approach that aims to sustainably balance and optimize the health of people, animals, and ecosystems
- **Premise:** Human, animal, and environmental health are interconnected and interdependent
- **Scope:** Zoonotic diseases, antimicrobial resistance, food safety, vector-borne diseases, environmental contamination, biodiversity loss

### One Health Joint Plan of Action (OH JPA) 2022-2026
- Launched by the Quadripartite (2022)
- Six action tracks: health systems, emerging/re-emerging zoonoses, endemic zoonoses, food safety, AMR, environment
- Emphasis on national-level operationalization

## The Zoonotic Disease Interface

### Key Facts
- ~60% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic
- ~75% of emerging infections originate from wildlife
- Drivers of emergence: land-use change, deforestation, agricultural intensification, wildlife trade, climate change, urbanization

### Examples of Zoonotic Diseases
| Disease | Animal Reservoir | Transmission |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Rabies | Dogs, bats, wildlife | Bite/scratch |
| Ebola | Bats (suspected) | Contact with infected animals/humans |
| Avian influenza | Poultry, wild birds | Direct contact, aerosol |
| HAT (rhodesiense) | Cattle, wildlife | Tsetse fly vector |
| Brucellosis | Cattle, goats, sheep | Unpasteurized dairy, contact |
| Leptospirosis | Rodents | Water contaminated with urine |
| MERS | Camels | Respiratory |
| SARS-CoV-2 | Bats (suspected), intermediate host | Respiratory |
| Mpox | Rodents (suspected) | Contact |

### Spillover and Emergence
- **Spillover:** Pathogen jumps from animal reservoir to human host
- **Risk factors:** High human-animal-environment interface; biodiversity loss reduces dilution effect; intensive farming
- **Prevention:** Surveillance at the interface; reducing risky contacts; habitat preservation

## WHO/FAO/WOAH/UNEP Quadripartite

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| **WHO** | Human health; disease surveillance, response, normative guidance |
| **FAO** | Food and Agriculture Organization; food safety, livestock health, AMR in agriculture |
| **WOAH** (formerly OIE) | World Organisation for Animal Health; animal disease surveillance, standards, transparency |
| **UNEP** | UN Environment Programme; environmental dimension of health threats (joined 2022) |

### Tripartite/Quadripartite Initiatives
- **GLEWS+:** Global Early Warning System for major animal diseases including zoonoses
- **OFFLU:** Joint OIE-FAO network on animal influenza
- **AMR GAP:** Global Action Plan on Antimicrobial Resistance (tripartite)
- **ZODIAC:** Zoonotic Disease Integrated Action (FAO)
- **One Health Intelligence Scoping Study**

## Applications in Surveillance and Outbreak Response

### Integrated Surveillance
- **Joint human-animal surveillance:** Coordinated reporting between Ministries of Health and Ministries of Agriculture/Livestock
- **Shared laboratory capacity:** Unified testing for zoonotic pathogens
- **Example:** Coordinated rabies surveillance — human case surveillance + dog bite surveillance + animal rabies surveillance
- **Example:** HAT — tsetse surveillance + animal trypanosomiasis monitoring + human case detection

### Outbreak Investigation
- One Health teams: epidemiologists, veterinarians, ecologists, environmental scientists
- Traceback to animal source (food-borne outbreaks; wildlife spillover events)
- Environmental sampling (water, soil, vectors)
- Joint risk assessment

### Antimicrobial Resistance
- AMR in human pathogens, animal pathogens, food chain, environment
- Shared resistance genes across human and animal bacteria
- Surveillance: GLASS (WHO, human) + animal AMR monitoring
- Interventions: reduce agricultural antibiotic use; infection prevention; stewardship

## COHFE Competency Framework

### Overview
- **Full name:** Competencies for One Health Field Epidemiology
- **Purpose:** Define competencies needed for epidemiologists working at the human-animal-environment interface
- **Developers:** FAO, WHO, WOAH, with TEPHINET and other partners

### Core Competency Domains
1. **Collaborative partnerships** — Cross-sectoral collaboration and communication
2. **Surveillance** — Design and implement integrated surveillance across sectors
3. **Outbreak investigation** — Multisectoral outbreak response
4. **Study design and analysis** — Epidemiological methods applied across sectors
5. **Communication** — Risk communication across disciplines and to public
6. **Leadership and management** — Coordinate multisectoral teams
7. **Ethics** — Address ethical issues at the human-animal-environment interface

## Challenges and Gaps

- **Institutional silos:** Ministries of Health and Agriculture often have separate budgets, mandates, reporting
- **Data sharing:** Different data systems, standards, legal frameworks across sectors
- **Workforce:** Few professionals trained across multiple sectors
- **Funding:** One Health activities often fall between mandates — nobody's budget
- **Measurement:** Difficulty quantifying the added value of One Health approach
- **Equity:** LMIC burden highest but One Health capacity weakest

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **Implementation is becoming more concrete:** WHO published a factsheet on **3 March 2025** and continued promoting the national implementation guide for the One Health Joint Plan of Action, signalling a shift from general advocacy to country-level operationalization.
- **Governance support is expanding:** The Quadripartite launched a **One Health Governance Community of Practice on 30 April 2025**, which matters because governance failure is often the real bottleneck, not conceptual agreement.
- **The Quadripartite mandate was renewed:** WHO announced on **3 February 2026** that the Quadripartite Memorandum of Understanding on One Health was renewed through **2030**, reinforcing that this is a sustained institutional agenda rather than a short-lived post-COVID slogan.
- **Practical implication:** In 2026, a credible One Health programme should show governance, financing, data-sharing, and implementation mechanisms, not just mention multisectoral collaboration in principle.

## Practical Examples

- **Cambodia rabies control:** WHO's 2025 NTD report highlighted rabies elimination work in Cambodia as a practical One Health example linking dog vaccination, human post-exposure prevention, and coordinated surveillance.
- **rHAT control at the animal-human interface:** Rhodesiense HAT remains a classic illustration of One Health logic because livestock and wildlife reservoirs affect human transmission risk.
- **Avian influenza preparedness:** Joint animal and human surveillance systems remain one of the clearest examples of how One Health improves early warning and risk assessment.
- **AMR surveillance:** Linking human, veterinary, food, and environmental AMR data is a practical test of whether a country can move beyond siloed surveillance.
