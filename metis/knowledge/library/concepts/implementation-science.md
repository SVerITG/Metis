# Implementation Science

> Reference card — frameworks, study designs, and outcomes for understanding how evidence-based interventions work in practice.

---

## Why Implementation Science Matters

- Evidence-based interventions often fail when transferred to real-world settings
- Average lag of 17 years from evidence to routine practice (Balas & Boren, 2000)
- Focus shifts from "does it work?" (efficacy) to "does it work here, for whom, and why?" (effectiveness in context)
- Particularly relevant for NTD programs scaling up or adapting proven interventions

## RE-AIM Framework

- **Reach:** What proportion of the target population participates?
- **Effectiveness:** What is the impact on outcomes (including negative effects)?
- **Adoption:** What proportion of settings and staff adopt the intervention?
- **Implementation:** Is it delivered as intended (fidelity)? What does it cost?
- **Maintenance:** Is it sustained over time at individual and organizational levels?
- Useful for both planning and evaluating interventions

## CFIR (Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research)

- Organizes determinants of implementation into 5 domains:
  - **Innovation characteristics:** Evidence strength, adaptability, complexity, cost
  - **Outer setting:** Patient needs, external policies, peer pressure, cosmopolitanism
  - **Inner setting:** Culture, implementation climate, readiness, leadership engagement
  - **Individuals:** Knowledge, self-efficacy, role identity
  - **Implementation process:** Planning, engaging, executing, reflecting and evaluating
- Used for qualitative analysis of barriers and facilitators
- Updated in 2022 (CFIR 2.0) with refined constructs

## Proctor's Implementation Outcomes

- Distinct from clinical/service outcomes; measure implementation success:
  - **Acceptability:** Perception that the intervention is agreeable
  - **Adoption:** Decision to employ the intervention
  - **Appropriateness:** Perceived fit for the setting and population
  - **Feasibility:** Can it actually be done with available resources?
  - **Fidelity:** Degree to which it is delivered as designed
  - **Implementation cost:** Incremental cost of the implementation strategy
  - **Penetration:** Integration within a service setting
  - **Sustainability:** Maintained over time after initial implementation

## Hybrid Effectiveness-Implementation Designs

- **Type 1:** Primary aim is testing clinical effectiveness; secondary aim is understanding implementation
- **Type 2:** Dual aims — test both clinical effectiveness and implementation strategy simultaneously
- **Type 3:** Primary aim is testing implementation strategy; secondary aim is clinical outcomes
- Choice depends on: strength of evidence for the intervention, knowledge about implementation barriers

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **TDR Strategy 2024-2029:** WHO/TDR continues to invest in implementation research for epidemics, neglected diseases, and climate resilience while democratizing science through local capacity and equitable partnerships.
- **2025 implementation investments:** TDR launched new training fellowships in Ghana and Senegal and supported Nepal in building a pandemic preparedness evidence package, showing how implementation research is feeding decision-making during the recovery phase.
- **Implementation research networks:** Annual 2025 reports emphasize locally led operational research hubs, co-created solutions, and integration with One Health at the policy table.
