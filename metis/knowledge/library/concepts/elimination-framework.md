# Elimination Framework

> Reference card — WHO definitions, certification criteria, post-elimination surveillance, examples.

---

## WHO Definitions (Disease Reduction Spectrum)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Control** | Reduction of disease incidence, prevalence, morbidity, or mortality to a locally acceptable level as a result of deliberate efforts |
| **Elimination as a public health problem** | Achievement of measurable global targets set by WHO; disease still present but burden reduced below defined thresholds |
| **Elimination of transmission** | Reduction to zero incidence of infection caused by a specific pathogen in a defined geographical area, with continued measures required to prevent re-establishment |
| **Eradication** | Permanent reduction to zero worldwide incidence of infection; no further intervention measures needed |
| **Extinction** | The pathogen no longer exists in nature or in laboratories (only smallpox virus is debated) |

### Key Distinctions
- "Elimination as a public health problem" is operational and threshold-based (not zero)
- "Elimination of transmission" is the stricter definition (zero incidence with continued monitoring)
- Eradication is permanent and global — only achieved for smallpox (1980) and rinderpest (2011, animal)

## Certification and Validation

### Elimination of Transmission
- Requires sustained zero cases over defined period (disease-specific)
- Independent international commission verifies
- Continued surveillance and response capacity required post-certification
- **Example:** Dracunculiasis — 3 consecutive years of zero cases + adequate surveillance

### Elimination as Public Health Problem
- WHO validates that country meets agreed-upon targets
- Targets are disease-specific (e.g., HAT: <1 case per 10,000 in at-risk areas; trachoma: TF <5% in 1-9 year olds)
- Country submits dossier; WHO reviews and validates

## Post-Elimination Surveillance Principles

1. **Maintain high sensitivity** even as cases become extremely rare
2. **Integration into routine health systems** (sustainability vs sensitivity trade-off)
3. **Risk-based approach:** Focus on historically endemic areas, high-risk populations, border areas
4. **Cross-border coordination:** Importation risk management
5. **Rapid response capability:** Maintain ability to investigate and respond to single cases
6. **Diagnostics access:** Ensure RDTs and confirmation available at peripheral level
7. **Community awareness:** Maintain knowledge even when disease becomes rare
8. **Serological/entomological surveillance:** Complement case-based surveillance

### Common Challenges
- Declining political will and funding as visibility decreases
- Loss of clinical expertise (health workers never see cases)
- Difficulty justifying cost per case detected
- Risk of resurgence if surveillance weakens

## Examples of Elimination Efforts

### Achieved
- **Smallpox:** Eradicated 1980; last natural case 1977 (Somalia); ring vaccination strategy
- **Rinderpest:** Eradicated 2011 (animal disease — first veterinary eradication)
- **Guinea worm (dracunculiasis):** Near eradication; from 3.5M cases (1986) to <15 cases/year; Carter Center leadership
- **Polio:** Wild poliovirus type 2 eradicated (2015), type 3 (2019); type 1 remains in Afghanistan/Pakistan

### In Progress
- **HAT (g-HAT):** WHO target: elimination as public health problem by 2020 (achieved); elimination of transmission by 2030; from ~300,000 estimated cases (1998) to <1,000 reported (2022)
- **Malaria:** Elimination certified in 43 countries (by 2023); E-2025 initiative targets ~25 countries near zero
- **Yaws:** Target: eradication; azithromycin MDA strategy (Morges strategy)
- **Lymphatic filariasis:** Elimination as public health problem; MDA-based strategy
- **Trachoma:** Elimination as public health problem; SAFE strategy (Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement)

## WHO 2030 NTD Roadmap

### Vision
- Targets for 20 NTDs and disease groups for 2021-2030
- Aligned with SDGs and Universal Health Coverage

### Key Targets by 2030
- **Eradication:** Dracunculiasis, yaws
- **Elimination of transmission:** HAT (gambiense), rabies (dog-mediated human deaths)
- **Elimination as public health problem:** Leprosy, visceral leishmaniasis (South-East Asia), trachoma, LF, onchocerciasis, schistosomiasis, Chagas, soil-transmitted helminths
- **Control:** Dengue, snakebite, others

### Cross-Cutting Approaches
1. Accelerate programmatic action
2. Intensify cross-cutting approaches (WASH, vector control, One Health, veterinary public health)
3. Change operating models and culture (country ownership, integration, sustainability)
4. New tools: diagnostics, treatments, insecticides, vaccines

## Key Metrics for Monitoring Elimination

- **Incidence rate** (per 10,000 at-risk population)
- **Case detection rate** (active vs passive)
- **Surveillance sensitivity:** Proportion of cases detected
- **Reporting completeness and timeliness**
- **Geographical scope:** % of endemic areas with functional surveillance
- **Treatment coverage** (where applicable)

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **WHO reconfirms elimination momentum:** World NTD Day 2025/2026 messaging and ongoing NTD Roadmap implementation keep validation dossiers active, with elimination and transmission-progress announcements continuing into 2026.
- **Pandemic Agreement and One Health alignment:** The WHO Pandemic Agreement adopted 20 May 2025 explicitly calls for sustained surveillance, One Health coordination, and resilient elimination architectures able to detect and respond to single-case reintroductions.
- **Digital verification tools:** WHO’s 2025 extension of the Global Strategy on Digital Health to 2027 reinforces that elimination programmes now routinely rely on digital event-signal triage (e.g., EIOS) and interoperable health information pipelines for verification and rapid response.
