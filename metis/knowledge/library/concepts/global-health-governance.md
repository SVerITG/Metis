# Global Health Governance

> Reference card — institutional architecture, legal frameworks, pandemic preparedness, and the funding landscape.

---

## WHO Reform and Role

- World Health Organization: UN specialized agency for health; 194 member states
- Core functions: normative guidance, technical assistance, health intelligence, convening power
- Governance: World Health Assembly (annual), Executive Board, Regional Offices (6 regions)
- Ongoing reform: sustainable financing (assessed contributions vs voluntary), emergency response capacity, accountability
- WHO budget heavily dependent on voluntary and earmarked contributions (~80%); limits independence
- WHO Academy and Health Emergencies Programme as reform products

## International Health Regulations (IHR 2005)

- Legally binding instrument for 196 states parties
- Purpose: prevent, protect against, and respond to international spread of disease while avoiding unnecessary interference with trade and travel
- **Core capacities required:** Surveillance, laboratory, preparedness, response, risk communication, human resources, legislation, coordination, points of entry
- **PHEIC (Public Health Emergency of International Concern):** Declared by WHO Director-General on advice of Emergency Committee
- Joint External Evaluations (JEE) assess country compliance with IHR core capacities
- IHR amendments adopted in 2024 to strengthen obligations and equity provisions

## Pandemic Treaty Negotiations (INB)

- Intergovernmental Negotiating Body established 2021 to draft a pandemic agreement
- Key issues: pathogen access and benefit sharing (PABS), equity in countermeasure distribution, technology transfer, financing
- Tension between sovereignty and collective action
- One Health approach explicitly included
- Negotiations ongoing; target adoption at World Health Assembly

## Pandemic Fund (World Bank)

- Established 2022; hosted by World Bank
- Purpose: finance pandemic prevention, preparedness, and response (PPR) capacity in LMICs
- Fills gap between IHR requirements and available domestic financing
- First round of grants: surveillance, laboratory capacity, workforce
- Governance includes civil society and LMIC representation

## ACT-Accelerator Legacy

- Access to COVID-19 Tools Accelerator (2020-2023): COVAX (vaccines), diagnostics, therapeutics, health systems connector
- Achievements: delivered 2 billion+ vaccine doses to LMICs via COVAX
- Failures: inequitable distribution persisted; "vaccine apartheid" narrative; structural power imbalances
- Lessons: pre-negotiated manufacturing agreements, regional production capacity (mRNA hubs in Africa), need for permanent architecture

## Global Health Security vs Health Equity Tension

- Security framing: protect against external threats; tends to prioritize high-income country concerns
- Equity framing: strengthen systems for all; emphasizes LMIC capacity and access
- Both are needed but resource allocation and political attention often skew toward security
- Example: pandemic preparedness funding vs UHC investment
- Decolonizing global health movement challenges traditional power dynamics in governance and research

## Funding Landscape

- **Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance:** Vaccine procurement and delivery for LMICs; innovative financing (IFFIm, AMC)
- **The Global Fund:** HIV, TB, malaria; performance-based financing; country ownership model
- **Bilateral aid:** USAID (PEPFAR), DFID/FCDO, GIZ, AFD, DGDC/DGD — shifting priorities and budget pressures
- **Domestic financing:** Most LMIC health spending is domestic; Abuja target (15% of government budget to health) rarely met
- **Innovative financing:** Health taxes (tobacco, sugar), debt-for-health swaps, blended finance
- Overall trend: flat or declining ODA for health; increasing emphasis on domestic resource mobilization and sustainability

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **WHO Pandemic Agreement adopted:** On **20 May 2025** the WHO Pandemic Agreement was agreed, committing member states to equitable sharing of tests, therapeutics, and vaccines plus a 20% increase in assessed contributions to stabilize WHO financing, even as some countries (notably the U.S.) remain outside the treaty process.
- **Funding debates continue:** The WHO budget remains heavily reliant on voluntary contributions, leading to discussions about a new global health financing architecture centered on pandemic preparedness, including proposals for alternative surveillance systems and domestic resource mobilisation.
- **Global Fund replenishment at risk:** Replenishment negotiations in 2025 highlighted donor fatigue, but key partners pledged to maintain support for HIV, TB, and malaria, reinforcing the multi-donor funding base for disease control.
