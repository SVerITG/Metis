# Research Ethics

> Reference card — foundational principles, informed consent challenges, community engagement, and regulatory frameworks.

---

## Declaration of Helsinki

- World Medical Association declaration on ethical principles for medical research involving human subjects
- First adopted 1964; revised multiple times (latest: 2013, with ongoing updates under discussion)
- Core tenets: primacy of participant welfare, scientific validity as ethical prerequisite, vulnerable population protections, post-trial access
- Requires ethics committee review before study start
- Stronger protections than some national regulations; serves as global benchmark

## Belmont Report Principles (1979)

- **Respect for persons:** Autonomy of individuals; additional protections for those with diminished autonomy
- **Beneficence:** Maximize benefits, minimize harms; do no harm (non-maleficence)
- **Justice:** Fair distribution of research burdens and benefits; avoid exploiting vulnerable populations
- These three principles underpin most modern research ethics frameworks

## Informed Consent in Low-Resource Settings

- Literacy barriers: use oral consent, pictorial aids, community witnesses
- Power dynamics: participants may feel coerced by researchers perceived as authority figures
- Therapeutic misconception: participants may confuse research with clinical care
- Community consent: may be culturally required alongside individual consent, but does not replace it
- Language: consent materials in local languages, verified by back-translation
- Dynamic consent: ongoing process, not a one-time signature

## Community Engagement and Participatory Research

- Community Advisory Boards (CABs): formal mechanism for community input
- Participatory action research (PAR): community members as co-researchers
- Benefits: improved trust, culturally appropriate design, better recruitment and retention
- Engagement should start early (study design) and continue through dissemination
- Return of results to communities: ethical obligation, not optional

## Ethics of Surveillance vs Research

- **Surveillance:** Routine, mandated public health activity; typically exempt from individual consent under IHR
- **Research:** Systematic investigation to produce generalizable knowledge; requires ethical review
- Grey zone: operational research, program evaluation, enhanced surveillance
- Key question: Is the primary purpose public health practice or generalizable knowledge?
- When unclear: err on the side of ethics review

## IRB / Ethics Committee Process

- Protocol review before data collection begins (prospective review)
- Types of review: exempt, expedited, full board — depends on risk level
- Annual continuing review for ongoing studies
- Amendments required for protocol changes
- Adverse event reporting obligations
- Multi-site studies may need approval from each site's ethics committee
- Dual review common in North-South collaborations (sponsor country + host country)

## Data Protection

- **GDPR (EU):** Applies to processing of personal data of EU residents; lawful basis required; data minimization; right to erasure
- Implications for health research: pseudonymization, data processing agreements, data protection impact assessments
- Many LMIC countries developing similar legislation (Kenya Data Protection Act, South Africa POPIA)
- Particular sensitivity: genomic data, HIV status, mental health, stigmatized diseases (including HAT)
- Anonymization vs pseudonymization: truly anonymous data falls outside GDPR; pseudonymized data does not

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **The Declaration of Helsinki was revised in October 2024:** The World Medical Association states that the **2024 version** is now the only official version and older versions should not be cited except historically.
- **AI ethics is now an explicit health-research issue:** WHO published guidance on **large multi-modal models on 25 March 2025**, extending earlier AI ethics work into practical governance questions relevant to research, surveillance, and clinical systems.
- **Health-data and biobank ethics remain active concerns:** The Declaration of Taipei continues to complement Helsinki for databases and biobanks, which is directly relevant to longitudinal public-health and genomic research infrastructures.
- **Practical implication:** In 2026, ethics review increasingly has to cover data governance, secondary data use, model transparency, bias, and post-deployment harms alongside traditional consent and risk-benefit questions.

## Practical Examples

- **Community engagement in global-health research:** Tindana et al. remains a foundational example of why ethical research in LMIC settings requires engagement beyond formal consent.
- **Adaptive and platform trials:** Recent outbreak and emergency-response research has shown that rapid designs still require prospective ethics oversight, protocol amendments, and clear governance.
- **AI-assisted public-health tools:** WHO's 2025 LMM guidance is directly relevant when generative-AI tools are used to support data abstraction, triage, or participant communication.
- **Stigmatized disease research:** HAT, HIV, mental-health, and migration-linked studies remain practical cases where confidentiality and respectful return of results are ethically central.
