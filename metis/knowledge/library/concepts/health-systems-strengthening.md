# Health Systems Strengthening

> Reference card — WHO building blocks, UHC, system resilience, and integration of vertical programs.

---

## WHO Six Building Blocks

1. **Service delivery:** Effective, safe, quality health services for those who need them
2. **Health workforce:** Sufficient numbers, skill mix, distribution, and motivation
3. **Health information systems:** Production, analysis, dissemination, and use of reliable data
4. **Access to essential medicines:** Equitable access to quality, safe, efficacious products
5. **Health financing:** Adequate funding with financial protection (no catastrophic expenditure)
6. **Leadership and governance:** Strategic policy frameworks, regulation, accountability, partnerships

- Building blocks are interdependent — strengthening one in isolation rarely succeeds
- Critics note the framework is static; does not capture dynamics, interactions, or people-centeredness

## Universal Health Coverage (UHC) and Primary Health Care (PHC)

- **UHC:** All people receive quality health services without financial hardship
- Three dimensions of UHC cube: population coverage, service coverage, financial protection
- **PHC:** First contact, continuous, comprehensive, coordinated care; foundation for UHC
- Astana Declaration (2018) reaffirmed PHC as cornerstone of UHC and SDG 3
- Tracking progress: UHC service coverage index (SDG 3.8.1), catastrophic health expenditure (SDG 3.8.2)

## Health System Resilience

- Ability to prepare for, absorb, adapt to, and recover from shocks
- **COVID-19 lessons:** Fragile systems collapsed; need surge capacity, supply chain diversification, digital readiness
- **Ebola lessons (West Africa 2014-16):** Routine services disrupted; excess mortality from non-Ebola causes exceeded Ebola deaths
- Resilience requires: robust surveillance, flexible workforce, community trust, adaptive governance
- Tension between efficiency (lean systems) and resilience (redundancy, slack)

## Community Health Workers in LMICs

- Essential for extending PHC to remote and underserved populations
- Roles: health promotion, case finding, treatment delivery, referral, data collection
- Success factors: training, supervision, remuneration, community embeddedness, supply chain
- WHO guideline (2018) on health policy and system support to optimize CHW programs
- Risk of overburdening CHWs with too many vertical program tasks

## Integration of Vertical Programs into PHC

- Vertical (disease-specific) programs: NTDs, HIV, TB, malaria, immunization
- Integration rationale: efficiency, sustainability, reduced duplication, better access
- Integration challenges: loss of disease-specific expertise, diluted attention, funding silos
- Continuum from fully vertical to fully integrated; most programs sit somewhere in between
- For NTDs: mass drug administration through integrated platforms, case management through PHC, surveillance through HMIS
- HAT-specific: transitioning from mobile screening teams to passive detection at fixed health facilities requires PHC strengthening

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **WHO Results Report 2024-2025:** 431 million more people now access essential services without catastrophic costs, and 637 million are better protected thanks to targeted investments in workforce, surveillance, and supply-chain resilience.
- **Digital health strategy extension:** On 23 May 2025 the World Health Assembly extended the Global Strategy on Digital Health to 2027 and released guidance tying interoperable digital tools, AI governance, and workforce capacity to resilient systems.
- **UHC financing momentum:** The 2025 HealthWorks High-Level Forum (Dec 2025) emphasized National Health Compacts, sustainable financing, and finance-health collaboration to expand coverage and financial protection.
