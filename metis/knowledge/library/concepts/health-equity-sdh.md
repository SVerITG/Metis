# Health Equity and Social Determinants of Health

> Reference card — frameworks, measurement, SDGs, key concepts.

---

## Core Definitions

- **Health equity:** The absence of unfair, avoidable, or remediable differences in health among population groups defined socially, economically, demographically, or geographically (WHO)
- **Health inequality:** Measurable differences in health between individuals or groups — may or may not be unjust
- **Health inequity:** Inequalities that are considered unfair or unjust because they arise from social disadvantage, not biological variation or free choice
- **Social determinants of health (SDH):** The conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age, shaped by the distribution of money, power, and resources (CSDH, 2008)

---

## Solar and Irwin Conceptual Framework (CSDH 2010)

### Structural Determinants (of health inequities)
- **Socioeconomic and political context:** Governance, macroeconomic policies, social policies, public policies, culture, societal values
- **Socioeconomic position:** Social class, gender, ethnicity, education, occupation, income
- These create social stratification that determines differential exposure, vulnerability, and consequences

### Intermediary Determinants (of health)
- **Material circumstances:** Housing, neighbourhood, working conditions, food access
- **Behavioural/biological factors:** Diet, physical activity, tobacco/alcohol use
- **Psychosocial factors:** Stress, social support, coping
- **Health system:** Access, quality, affordability — can reduce or amplify inequities

### Pathway
Structural determinants -> Social stratification -> Differential exposure, vulnerability, and consequences -> Health inequities

## Measurement of Health Inequities

### Absolute Measures
| Measure | Formula | Interpretation |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Rate Difference** | Rate_disadvantaged - Rate_advantaged | Absolute gap between groups |
| **Slope Index of Inequality (SII)** | Regression-based; difference between most and least advantaged | Accounts for entire socioeconomic gradient, not just extremes |
| **Population Attributable Risk** | Excess burden attributable to inequality | Total cases avoidable if all had rate of most advantaged |

### Relative Measures
| Measure | Formula | Interpretation |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Rate Ratio** | Rate_disadvantaged / Rate_advantaged | Relative disparity between groups |
| **Relative Index of Inequality (RII)** | Ratio analog of SII using regression | Summary relative measure across gradient |
| **Concentration Index (CI)** | Based on concentration curve (like Gini) | Range -1 to +1; 0 = no inequality; captures full distribution |

### Concentration Curve
- Plots cumulative proportion of health variable (y-axis) against cumulative proportion of population ranked by socioeconomic status (x-axis)
- Below line of equality = health concentrated among advantaged
- Area between curve and line of equality = concentration index / 2

### Practical Notes
- **Always report both absolute and relative measures** — they can tell different stories
- Relative inequalities can increase even as absolute inequalities decrease (if everyone improves but advantaged improve faster)
- Disaggregate by equity stratifiers: wealth quintile, education, urban/rural, sex, ethnicity, geography

## SDGs and Health

### SDG 3: Good Health and Well-Being
Key targets relevant to public health:
- 3.1: Reduce maternal mortality to <70/100,000 live births
- 3.2: End preventable deaths of newborns and children under 5
- 3.3: End epidemics of AIDS, TB, malaria, NTDs; combat hepatitis, waterborne diseases
- 3.4: Reduce premature NCD mortality by one third
- 3.8: Achieve Universal Health Coverage (UHC) including financial risk protection
- 3.d: Strengthen capacity for early warning, risk reduction, and management of health risks

### Health Across the SDGs
- **SDG 1 (No poverty):** Poverty as root cause of poor health
- **SDG 2 (Zero hunger):** Nutrition, food security
- **SDG 4 (Quality education):** Education as strongest predictor of health
- **SDG 5 (Gender equality):** Gender-based health disparities
- **SDG 6 (Clean water/sanitation):** WASH as determinant of infectious disease
- **SDG 10 (Reduced inequalities):** Health equity
- **SDG 11 (Sustainable cities):** Urban health, air pollution
- **SDG 13 (Climate action):** Climate and health nexus
- **SDG 16 (Peace/justice):** Conflict and health; strong institutions

### UHC as an Equity Strategy
- UHC = all people receive quality essential health services without financial hardship
- Equity dimensions: Who is covered? What services? What financial protection?
- UHC service coverage index (WHO) tracks progress

## Key Frameworks and Reports

### Commission on Social Determinants of Health (CSDH)
- **Report:** *Closing the Gap in a Generation* (WHO, 2008)
- **Three overarching recommendations:**
  1. Improve daily living conditions
  2. Tackle inequitable distribution of power, money, and resources
  3. Measure and understand the problem and assess impact of action

### Marmot Review (England, 2010)
- **Report:** *Fair Society, Healthy Lives*
- **Six policy objectives:**
  1. Give every child the best start in life
  2. Enable all to maximize their capabilities and have control over their lives
  3. Create fair employment and good work for all
  4. Ensure a healthy standard of living for all
  5. Create and develop healthy and sustainable places and communities
  6. Strengthen the role and impact of ill-health prevention
- **Marmot Review 10 Years On (2020):** Documented stalled/reversed progress in England

### Other Important Frameworks
- **WHO Health Equity Monitor:** Database of disaggregated health data
- **Dahlgren-Whitehead rainbow model:** Layers of influence on health (individual -> social/community -> living/working conditions -> socioeconomic/cultural)
- **Whitehead criteria for inequity:** Systematic, socially produced, unfair, avoidable

## Practical Applications in Epidemiology

- **Equity-stratified analysis:** Disaggregate all health indicators by equity stratifiers (wealth, education, geography, sex)
- **Equity-focused surveillance:** Ensure surveillance captures marginalized populations
- **Pro-equity targeting:** Direct interventions to most disadvantaged first
- **Equity impact assessment:** Evaluate whether interventions reduce or widen inequities
- **Intersectionality:** Consider overlapping disadvantages (gender x poverty x geography)

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **WHO inequality monitoring updates:** In March 2026 WHO refreshed the Health Equity Data Repository and HEAT v7, adding more than 13 million disaggregated datapoints and a new determinants module aligned with the Operational Framework for Monitoring Social Determinants of Health Equity.
- **SDG equity progress remains mixed:** The 2025 SDG report highlights gains in some dimensions but continuing gaps in UHC access, clean water, and climate-resilient infrastructure, reinforcing the need for targeted redistributive policies.
- **Redistributive policy debates persist:** Late-2025 coverage from the World Inequality Lab, Oxfam, and WHO underscored stalled inequality reduction, underscoring calls for progressive taxation, social protection, and debt relief to protect health equity.
