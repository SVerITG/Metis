# Current Challenges in Global Health (2026)

> Living document — major themes shaping public health research and practice.

---

## Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

- **Scale:** ~4.95 million deaths associated with bacterial AMR in 2019 (Murray et al., Lancet 2022); updated estimate ~4.71 million deaths linked to AMR in 2021 (GRAM 2024); projections of 10M deaths/year by 2050 without action
- **Key pathogens:** ESKAPE organisms (Enterococcus, S. aureus, Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas, Enterobacter); drug-resistant TB, malaria, HIV
- **Drivers:** Overuse/misuse in human medicine, agricultural use, poor infection prevention, lack of new antibiotics
- **One Health dimension:** Shared resistance genes across human, animal, and environmental bacteria
- **Governance:** WHO Global Action Plan on AMR (2015); GLASS surveillance; UN High-Level Meeting on AMR (2024)
- **Pipeline problem:** Too few new antibiotics in development; market failure — need push/pull incentives

## Climate and Health

- **Direct impacts:** Heat-related mortality, extreme weather events, wildfires
- **Indirect impacts:** Vector range expansion (Aedes mosquitoes moving to higher altitudes/latitudes), food insecurity, water scarcity, displacement
- **NTD relevance:** Changing distribution of vectors (tsetse, sandflies, snails); drought/flooding affecting transmission
- **IPCC AR6:** Warns of cascading health risks; calls for health adaptation and mitigation co-benefits
- **Lancet Countdown:** Annual tracking of climate-health indicators
- **Response:** Climate-resilient health systems; climate-informed surveillance; green healthcare

## Pandemic Preparedness (Post-COVID)

- **Lessons:** Need for surge capacity, equitable vaccine access, supply chain resilience, transparent data sharing
- **Pandemic Treaty:** WHO negotiations ongoing (CA+ / Pandemic Agreement); contentious issues include pathogen access and benefit sharing (PABS), equity provisions
- **IHR amendments:** Updates to strengthen compliance and equity
- **100 Days Mission:** Diagnostics, therapeutics, and vaccines within 100 days of pandemic declaration
- **Institutions:** Pandemic Fund (World Bank); WHO Hub for Pandemic and Epidemic Intelligence (Berlin)
- **Risk:** Pandemic fatigue; political will declining; geopolitical fragmentation undermining cooperation

## Misinformation and Vaccine Hesitancy

- **Infodemic:** WHO term for excess information including misinformation during health emergencies
- **Vaccine confidence:** Declining in multiple countries; compounded by COVID-19 politicization
- **Routine immunization:** Post-COVID backsliding — 25M children missed basic vaccines in 2021
- **Tools:** WHO infodemic management; social listening; prebunking/inoculation approaches; trusted messengers
- **Research need:** Understanding drivers of hesitancy; effective communication strategies; community engagement

## Health Workforce Shortages

- **Scale:** Projected shortage of 10 million health workers by 2030, concentrated in LMICs (WHO)
- **Drivers:** Insufficient training capacity, brain drain (migration to HICs), burnout (post-COVID), ageing workforce
- **Impact:** Undermines UHC, surveillance capacity, outbreak response
- **Solutions:** Task shifting, community health workers, digital tools, retention incentives, investment in training
- **Epidemiology-specific:** Shortage of trained field epidemiologists in LMICs; FETP programmes critical

## Funding Cuts to Global Health

- **Trends:** Declining donor commitments; USAID restructuring; Global Fund replenishment challenges
- **Impact:** Threatens NTD programmes, HIV/TB/malaria control, surveillance capacity, research
- **Sustainability:** Push toward domestic financing, but LMICs have competing priorities
- **Risk:** Resurgence of controlled diseases if programmes disrupted; loss of elimination gains

## NCD Epidemic in LMICs

- **Burden:** NCDs cause 74% of global deaths; majority now in LMICs
- **Key NCDs:** Cardiovascular disease, cancer, diabetes, chronic respiratory disease, mental health
- **Double burden:** Many LMICs face simultaneous infectious disease and NCD epidemics
- **Drivers:** Tobacco, alcohol, unhealthy diets, physical inactivity, air pollution, rapid urbanization
- **Policy:** WHO Best Buys (cost-effective NCD interventions); HEARTS technical package
- **Gap:** Insufficient integration of NCD and infectious disease services

## AI Governance in Health

- **Opportunity:** Diagnostic support, drug discovery, operational efficiency, surveillance
- **Risks:** Bias, privacy violations, job displacement, over-reliance, accountability gaps
- **Regulatory landscape:** EU AI Act (2024); WHO guidance on AI ethics for health (2021); patchwork globally
- **LLMs in health:** Clinical decision support, medical education, patient communication — but hallucination risk, liability unclear
- **Need:** Regulatory frameworks specific to health AI; validation standards; post-deployment monitoring; equity safeguards
- **Workforce implications:** Training health professionals to work with AI; digital literacy

## Emerging and Cross-Cutting Themes

- **Genomic surveillance:** Expanding beyond COVID-19; pathogen genomics for AMR, TB, outbreak response
- **Universal Health Coverage:** Post-COVID momentum; integration of vertical programmes; primary healthcare strengthening
- **Conflict and health:** Record number of displaced people (>110M); health system destruction; attacks on health workers
- **Decolonizing global health:** Shifting power to LMICs; local research leadership; equitable partnerships
- **Planetary health:** Health of human civilization dependent on flourishing natural systems
- **Antimicrobial access:** Balancing AMR with need for antibiotic access in LMICs (access-excess paradox)

## Current Developments (2025-2026)

- **Pandemic preparedness is now in the implementation phase:** The **WHO Pandemic Agreement was adopted on 20 May 2025**, moving this issue from negotiation into ratification, governance, and operational follow-through.
- **Forced displacement remains historically high:** UNHCR reported **123.2 million forcibly displaced people at end-2024** in its **12 June 2025** Global Trends release, and **117.3 million at the end of June 2025** in its **4 November 2025** Mid-Year Trends report.
- **Climate-health pressures intensified:** The Lancet Countdown's **2025 global report** emphasized worsening heat, air-pollution, labour, and infectious-disease risks, reinforcing climate-sensitive surveillance as a mainstream public-health need.
- **Digital-health governance extended:** WHO announced on **23 May 2025** that the Global Strategy on Digital Health was extended to **2027**, explicitly tying digital systems and AI to health-system strengthening and preparedness.
- **AI regulation is operational in Europe:** The European Commission notes that the **EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024**, with staged implementation through **2 August 2027**. For European health researchers, AI governance is now a live compliance issue.
- **Conflict, climate, AI, and surveillance are converging:** These are no longer separable trends. They increasingly affect denominators, data quality, reporting infrastructure, funding stability, and ethics review in the same projects.

## Practical Examples

- **Pandemic governance:** The 20 May 2025 WHO Pandemic Agreement adoption is a concrete example of COVID-era lessons translating into institutional reform.
- **Conflict and mobility:** UNHCR's 2025 releases show why surveillance and elimination research must account for displaced and highly mobile populations, unstable denominators, and disrupted reporting chains.
- **Climate-sensitive infectious disease:** WHO's **26 December 2025** dengue update reported more than **14.4 million cases in 2024**, showing how climate-sensitive vector-borne disease has become a system-level challenge.
- **Digital and AI governance:** WHO's digital-health extension and the EU AI Act timeline show that AI in health is no longer mainly an ethics discussion; it is also a regulatory and implementation problem.
