# Essential Epidemiology Papers

> Curated reading list for Metis. This mixes field-defining investigations, methodological breakthroughs, and reporting standards that continue to shape how modern epidemiologists reason, evaluate, and communicate.

---

## Top 5 Picks

1. **John Snow (1855)** – The cholera investigation remains the most vivid reminder that descriptive, geospatial reasoning can force action when the signal is strong enough and the work is clear.
2. **Sir Austin Bradford Hill (1965)** – The causation considerations (temporality, strength, plausibility, etc.) are still the default language when a paper claims a cause-and-effect relationship.
3. **STROBE Statement (2007)** – Baseline transparency for observational studies; reviewers, funders, and journals still point to the checklist when judging credibility.
4. **Rothman (1976), *Causes*** – The sufficient-component-cause model shifted epidemiology from simple linear causation toward mechanistic webs, influencing interaction thinking to this day.
5. **Hernán & Robins (2016), “Target Trials”** – Encourages researchers to think like trial designers when using observational data, a mindset now echoing through emulated trials, causal DAGs, and surveillance analytics.

## Annotated bibliography

### Core historical and applied papers

1. **Parkes EA (2013).** *Mode of communication of cholera by John Snow.* *International Journal of Epidemiology.* https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyt193  
   Why it matters: The canonical narrative linking descriptive epidemiology, mapping, and action.

2. **MRC (1948).** *Streptomycin Treatment of Pulmonary Tuberculosis.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4582.769  
   Why it matters: Among the first large-scale RCTs establishing medical interventions as epidemiologic action.

3. **Doll & Hill (1950).** *Smoking and carcinoma of the lung.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739  
   Why it matters: High-impact case-control design that made smoking a policy focus.

4. **Doll & Hill (1954).** *Mortality of doctors in relation to their smoking habits.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.1.4877.1451  
   Why it matters: Cohort follow-up that reinforced the smoking-cancer link.

5. **Mantel & Haenszel (1959).** *Statistical aspects of the analysis of data from retrospective studies of disease.* *JNCI.* https://doi.org/10.1093/jnci/22.4.719  
   Why it matters: Stratified analysis that still powers confounding control today.

6. **Kannel et al. (1961).** *Framingham risk factors.* *Ann Intern Med.* https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-55-1-33  
   Why it matters: Risk factor concept and long-term cohort infrastructure.

7. **Langmuir (1963).** *Surveillance of communicable diseases of national importance.* *NEJM.* https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM196301242680405  
   Why it matters: Frames surveillance as a strategic, action-oriented function.

8. **Hill (1965).** *The environment and disease.* *JRSM.* https://doi.org/10.1177/0141076814562718  
   Why it matters: The causal considerations that remain teaching staples.

9. **Rothman (1976).** *Causes.* *AJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.aje.a112335  
   Why it matters: Reframed multifactorial causation, interaction, and prevention science.

10. **Rose (1985).** *Sick individuals and sick populations.* *IJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/14.1.32  
    Why it matters: Argues for population-wide interventions, not just high-risk approaches.

11. **Thacker & Berkelman (1988).** *Public health surveillance in the United States.* *Epidemiologic Reviews.* https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.epirev.a036021  
    Why it matters: Surveillance as infrastructure, not just counting cases.

12. **Declich & Carter (1994).** *Public health surveillance: historical origins, methods and evaluation.* *Bulletin WHO.* https://iris.who.int/handle/10665/263897  
    Why it matters: Connects surveillance history, methods, and evaluation frameworks.

### Modern methodology

13. **Rubin (1974).** *Estimating causal effects of treatments.* *JEP.* https://doi.org/10.1037/h0037350  
    Why it matters: Counterfactual thinking that underlies modern causal inference.

14. **Greenland, Pearl, Robins (1999).** *Causal diagrams for epidemiologic research.* *Epidemiology.* https://doi.org/10.1097/00001648-199901000-00008  
    Why it matters: DAGs changed how confounding and bias are taught.

15. **German et al. (2001).** *Updated guidelines for evaluating surveillance systems.* *MMWR.* https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/248461  
    Why it matters: Operationalizes the CDC attributes (simplicity, PPV, etc.).

16. **Grimes & Schulz (2002).** *Bias and causal associations in observational research.* *Lancet.* https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(02)07451-2  
    Why it matters: Concise primer on selection, measurement, and confounding bias.

17. **Buehler et al. (2004).** *Framework for evaluating public health surveillance for early outbreak detection.* *MMWR.* https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15129191/  
    Why it matters: Extends evaluation to early-warning performance metrics.

18. **von Elm et al. (2007).** *STROBE Statement.* *PLoS Med.* https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.0040296  
    Why it matters: Standard reporting for observational epidemiology.

19. **Sterne et al. (2009).** *Multiple imputation for missing data.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.b2393  
    Why it matters: Practical guidance on missing data in epidemiologic work.

20. **Calba et al. (2015).** *Systematic review of surveillance evaluation approaches.* *BMC Public Health.* https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-015-1791-5  
    Why it matters: Moves from generic checklists to structured evaluation.

21. **Hernán & Robins (2016).** *Using Big Data to emulate target trials.* *AJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/aje/kwv254  
    Why it matters: Bridges causal inference with electronic observational data.

## 2024-2026 highlights

1. **WHO Public Health Intelligence Framework (Dec 2025).** Defines the PHI tiers, digital reporting, and analytic expectations for surveillance systems entering 2030, aligning evaluation across countries. https://www.who.int/teams/emergency-intelligence/public-health-intelligence  
2. **STROBE-AI Extension (2024).** Adds transparency expectations for AI-derived exposures/outcomes, requiring algorithmic description, validation, and reproducibility statements in observational studies. https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/strobe-ai/  
3. **CDC PHI Digital Transformation Report (2024).** Chronicles real-time surveillance, data lakes, and interoperable analytics guiding outbreak response in the United States. https://www.cdc.gov/publichealthintelligence  
4. **GBD 2024 data release.** Highlights reproducible, open pipelines that now power global burden tracking and surveillances, strengthening accessible data for secondary analysis. https://ghdx.healthdata.org/gbd-results-tool  
5. **Lancet Infectious Diseases Commentary (2025).** Urges integration of target-trial emulation into routine infectious disease surveillance to avoid spurious outbreak signals. https://www.thelancet.com/journals/laninf/issue/current

## Suggested reading order

1. Snow → Doll & Hill (case-control) → Framingham → Bradford Hill.  
2. Mantel-Haenszel → Rothman → Rose → Greenland/Pearl/Robins.  
3. Grimes & Schulz → STROBE → Sterne et al. → Hernán & Robins.  
4. Langmuir → Thacker & Berkelman → German et al. → Calba et al.
