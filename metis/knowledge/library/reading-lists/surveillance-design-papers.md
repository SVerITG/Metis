# Surveillance Design Papers

> Readings on surveillance objectives, evaluation, digital innovation, outbreak detection, and the operational design choices that keep surveillance systems useful as disease burdens change.

---

## Top 5 Picks

1. **Langmuir (1963)** – Surveillance as decision support, not just case counting; foundational language for the CDC surveillance model.
2. **Thacker & Berkelman (1988)** – Positions infrastructure as the backbone of public health interventions, with an emphasis on completeness and timeliness.
3. **German et al. (2001)** – Introduces the CDC evaluation attributes, still the default vocabulary for system audits.
4. **Buehler et al. (2004)** – Extends evaluation to outbreak detection for early-warning systems, anticipating syndromic surveillance.
5. **Heymann & Rodier (2004)** – Frames public health intelligence (PHI), foreshadowing today’s data-integration and digital-alert hubs.

## Annotated bibliography

1. **Langmuir (1963).** *The surveillance of communicable diseases of national importance.* *NEJM.* https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM196301242680405  
   Why it matters: Frames surveillance as purposeful, national intelligence rather than passive counting.

2. **Thacker & Berkelman (1988).** *Public health surveillance in the United States.* *Epidemiologic Reviews.* https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.epirev.a036021  
   Why it matters: Defines the surveillance cycle, infrastructure, and responsibilities.

3. **Declich & Carter (1994).** *Public health surveillance: historical origins, methods and evaluation.* *Bulletin WHO.* https://iris.who.int/handle/10665/263897  
   Why it matters: Reconnects surveillance history to modern design choices.

4. **German et al. (2001).** *Updated guidelines for evaluating public health surveillance systems.* *MMWR.* https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/248461  
   Why it matters: Operationalizes the CDC attributes (utility, simplicity, PPV, etc.).

5. **Buehler et al. (2004).** *Framework for evaluating public health surveillance systems for early outbreak detection.* *MMWR.* https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15129191/  
   Why it matters: Inserts outbreak detection evaluation into the standard toolkit.

6. **Heymann & Rodier (2004).** *Global surveillance, national surveillance.* *Lancet.* https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(04)17668-9  
   Why it matters: Details how global collaboration and intelligence networks feed national action, a precursor to PHI frameworks.

7. **CDC Field Epidemiology Manual (2021 update).** *CDC.* https://www.cdc.gov/field-epi-manual/  
   Why it matters: Everyday reference for outbreak investigation, data collection, and surveillance practice.

8. **ECDC guide for designing EU communicable disease surveillance (2010).** *ECDC.* https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/en/publications-data/guide-designing-european-communicable-disease-surveillance  
   Why it matters: Provides architecture for cross-border data flows and joint risk assessment.

9. **WHO Field Epidemiology Handbook (2020).** *WHO.* https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240012159  
   Why it matters: Hands-on guidance linking surveillance, labs, and response coordination.

## 2024-2026 highlights

1. **ECDC Long-Term Surveillance Framework (Dec 2025 mid-term revision).** Recalibrates the 2021–2027 framework, emphasizing interoperability, digital transformation, and cross-border sentinel data. https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/en/publications-data/long-term-surveillance-framework-2021-2027-december-2025-mid-term-revision  
2. **WHO Public Health Intelligence Framework (Dec 2025).** Formalizes the PHI tiered model and notes expected spatial-temporal dashboards for outbreaks. https://www.who.int/teams/emergency-intelligence/public-health-intelligence  
3. **CDC PHI Digital Transformation Report (2024).** Chronicles how real-time surveillance, data lakes, and interoperable analytics now guide US outbreak response. https://www.cdc.gov/publichealthintelligence  
4. **WHO Genomic Surveillance Strategy (2024).** Updates how genomic data feed surveillance pipelines and informs variant detection. https://www.who.int/initiatives/genomic-surveillance  
5. **Lancet Digital Health Commission (2024).** Advocates layered digital detection stacks, spatial dashboards, and AI explainability as core surveillance components. https://www.thelancet.com/journals/landig/issue/current
