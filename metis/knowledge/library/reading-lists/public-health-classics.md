# Public Health Classics

> A compact canon of historical papers, reprints, and reports that still define how epidemiologists and public-health practitioners think about evidence, prevention, systems, and equity.

---

## Top 5 Picks

1. **John Snow (1855)** – Maps cholera as the original blend of descriptive epidemiology, action, and geography.
2. **Semmelweis (1847)** – The childbed fever story remains the go-to case study on evidence resistance and infection control.
3. **Doll & Hill (1950, 1954)** – The case-control and follow-up cohorts that launched the smoking-lung cancer link.
4. **Bradford Hill (1965)** – The famous checklist still frames causal inference for new risk factors.
5. **Alma-Ata Declaration (1978)** – Grounded primary health care and equity as global health priorities, a foundation for systems thinking.

## Annotated bibliography

1. **Parkes (2013 reprint).** *Mode of communication of cholera by John Snow.* *IJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyt193  
   Why it matters: The cholera investigation remains a blueprint for descriptive and spatial reasoning.

2. **Shorter (1984).** *Ignaz Semmelweis: The etiology, concept, and prophylaxis of childbed fever.* *BMJ.* https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1139454/  
   Why it matters: Early hygiene evidence that illustrates resistance to change in practice.

3. **Goldberger (1916).** *The transmissibility of pellagra.* *Public Health Reports.* https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nlmcatalog/101583536  
   Why it matters: Demonstrated that social conditions can masquerade as infectious phenomena.

4. **Frost (1939).** *The age selection of mortality from tuberculosis.* *AJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.aje.a117343  
   Why it matters: Early cohort thinking about changing risk over decades.

5. **MRC (1948).** *Streptomycin Treatment of Pulmonary Tuberculosis.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4582.769  
   Why it matters: One of the first modern RCTs.

6. **Doll & Hill (1950, 1954).** *Smoking and carcinoma of the lung; Mortality of doctors.* *BMJ.* https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739 / https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.1.4877.1451  
   Why it matters: Observational epidemiology with global policy impact.

7. **Kannel et al. (1961).** *Framingham risk factors.* *Ann Intern Med.* https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-55-1-33  
   Why it matters: Introduced “risk factors” that dominate chronic disease framing.

8. **Langmuir (1963).** *Surveillance of communicable diseases.* *NEJM.* https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM196301242680405  
   Why it matters: Surveillance as purposeful national intelligence.

9. **Hill (1965).** *The environment and disease: association or causation?* *JRSM.* https://doi.org/10.1177/0141076814562718  
   Why it matters: The go-to considerations for causal inference.

10. **Rothman (1976).** *Causes.* *AJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.aje.a112335  
    Why it matters: Component-cause model that reframed interaction and prevention.

11. **Rose (1985).** *Sick individuals and sick populations.* *IJE.* https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/14.1.32  
    Why it matters: Argues for population strategy, not just high-risk groups.

12. **Walsh & Warren (1979).** *Selective primary health care.* *NEJM.* https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM197911013011804  
    Why it matters: Influenced debates on vertical vs comprehensive approaches.

13. **Marmot, Shipley & Rose (1984).** *Inequalities in death.* *Lancet.* https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(84)92337-7  
    Why it matters: Launches modern social-epidemiology language about health gradients.

14. **Acheson (1988).** *Public Health in England.* https://archive.org/details/b32220513  
    Why it matters: National policy blueprint for public health functions.

15. **Declaration of Alma-Ata (1978).** *WHO.* https://www.who.int/teams/social-determinants-of-health/declaration-of-alma-ata  
    Why it matters: Grounded primary health care and equity as foundational goals.

16. **Ottawa Charter (1986).** *WHO.* https://www.who.int/health-promotion/conferences/previous/ottawa/en/  
    Why it matters: Established health promotion as public health strategy beyond clinical care.

17. **Commission on Social Determinants of Health (2008).** *Closing the gap in a generation.* https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-IER-CSDH-08.1  
    Why it matters: Modern evidence linking social systems to health inequality, with policy recommendations.

## 2024-2026 highlights

1. **WHO State of Inequality report (2024 update).** Tracks reproductive, maternal, newborn, child health, and service coverage inequality with interactive dashboards. https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/themes/health-equity  
2. **WHO Public Health Intelligence Framework (2025).** Reiterates the commitment to people-centered, equity-focused intelligence capacities and knowledge exchange. https://www.who.int/teams/emergency-intelligence/public-health-intelligence  
3. **WHO Global Strategy on Health Workforce 2025 mid-term report (2024).** Highlights the workforce equity imperative and surveillance of health workers themselves. https://www.who.int/activities/strengthening-health-workforce  
4. **Lancet Public Health Commission (2024) on reorienting public-health education** – Calls for renewing curricula around equity, resilience, and political determinants after COVID-19. https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/issue/current  
5. **WHO Health Promotion Global Strategy (2025).** Updates the 1986 Ottawa Charter with digital, climate, and equity lenses for modern public health action. https://www.who.int/teams/health-promotion/enhanced-wellbeing/global-strategy
