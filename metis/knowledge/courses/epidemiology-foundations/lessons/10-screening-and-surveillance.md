# Screening and Surveillance

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish screening from surveillance in purpose, unit of action, and evaluation.
- Explain why screening depends on prevalence, predictive value, and downstream care capacity.
- Describe the core functions of a surveillance system and why surveillance is continuous rather than episodic.
- Recognize why both screening and surveillance fail when detection is not linked to action.

## Prerequisites
- Disease frequency and study designs.
- Basic familiarity with diagnostic accuracy concepts.

## Content

### Section 1: Two related but different activities
Screening and surveillance are often mentioned together because both involve detecting health problems earlier than usual. But they are not the same thing.

**Screening** targets individuals who do not yet have recognized disease, or who are not currently presenting for diagnosis, in order to identify disease or risk earlier and improve outcomes.

**Surveillance** is the ongoing, systematic collection, analysis, interpretation, and dissemination of health data for public-health action at population level.

The difference in unit of action matters:

- screening acts on **individuals**
- surveillance acts on **populations and systems**

Confusing the two leads to bad design. A screening programme cannot be judged only by case counts, and a surveillance system cannot be judged only by whether it diagnoses one patient earlier.

### Section 2: What good screening is trying to achieve
Screening is not simply using a test in healthy people. A good screening programme should create a pathway from early detection to meaningful benefit. That usually requires:

- a condition that matters clinically or publicly
- a detectable early or asymptomatic phase
- a test with acceptable performance
- an effective confirmatory and treatment pathway
- evidence that benefits outweigh harms

This is why screening is a programme, not just a test. Even a technically accurate test can be harmful if it triggers false positives, anxiety, unnecessary referral, overtreatment, or costs without meaningful benefit.

### Section 3: Prevalence, predictive value, and why context matters
One of the most important beginner lessons is that test performance depends partly on context. Sensitivity and specificity are useful summaries, but the practical experience of screening often depends on **positive predictive value** and **negative predictive value**, which change with prevalence.

In low-prevalence settings, even a test with good sensitivity and specificity may generate many false positives relative to true positives. That means the programme can burden confirmatory services, waste resources, and erode trust.

This is especially relevant in elimination settings. As prevalence declines, the same screening algorithm may perform very differently from how it performed during higher-transmission phases. Programmes then need to reconsider thresholds, confirmatory testing, targeting, and the costs of maintaining sensitivity.

### Section 4: What surveillance is trying to achieve
Surveillance is broader than screening and diagnosis. Its purpose is information for action. A surveillance system collects data continuously or repeatedly, analyzes them, interprets them, and feeds them back to those who can act.

Core surveillance functions include:

- case detection
- reporting
- data compilation
- analysis
- interpretation
- dissemination
- response and feedback

A surveillance system may be designed for outbreak detection, burden monitoring, elimination verification, risk assessment, or program evaluation. It does not need to test everyone, and it does not usually operate as a one-time campaign. Continuity is one of its defining features.

### Section 5: Types of surveillance
Several surveillance types matter in practice.

**Indicator-based surveillance** uses structured reports from defined sources such as clinics, laboratories, or sentinel sites.

**Event-based surveillance** monitors unstructured information such as rumors, media reports, hotline alerts, or open-source intelligence.

**Passive surveillance** depends on routine reporting from facilities.

**Active surveillance** involves purposeful outreach to identify cases.

**Sentinel surveillance** uses selected sites to monitor patterns in a standardized way.

These approaches are complementary rather than mutually exclusive. A public-health system often needs more than one, especially when trying to balance timeliness, sensitivity, feasibility, and sustainability.

### Section 6: Worked example - screening in a low-prevalence setting
Imagine a district screens 10,000 asymptomatic people for a disease with 1% true prevalence. The test has 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity.

Expected results:

- true cases: 100
- true positives: 95
- false negatives: 5
- true non-cases: 9,900
- false positives: 495

So the programme identifies 590 positive screening results, but only 95 are true positives.

The positive predictive value is:

\[
\frac{95}{590} \approx 16\%
\]

Interpretation:
- Most positive screening results are false positives, even though the test looks "good" on paper.

This does not mean the test is useless. It means that in low-prevalence settings, confirmatory pathways, targeting strategies, and programme capacity become critical.

### Section 7: Worked example - surveillance for elimination
Imagine a sleeping sickness elimination programme shifts from periodic mobile screening to integrated passive surveillance in peripheral health facilities, supported by referral testing and case investigation.

This is not only a change in operational style. It is a change in surveillance design. The system now needs to answer questions such as:

- are suspect cases being detected at peripheral level?
- are referrals completed quickly enough?
- are reports complete and timely?
- are zero-case reports genuine or simply missing?
- is sensitivity being maintained as cases become rare?

A screening campaign may find individuals. A surveillance system must sustain awareness, reporting, verification, feedback, and response across the whole programme.

### Section 8: Evaluating screening and surveillance
Screening and surveillance need different evaluation logics.

For **screening**, important questions include:

- does the programme reduce morbidity or mortality?
- are benefits greater than harms?
- what is uptake?
- what proportion of positives complete confirmation and treatment?

For **surveillance**, questions often focus on system attributes such as:

- sensitivity
- timeliness
- acceptability
- flexibility
- representativeness
- data quality
- stability

CDC's surveillance evaluation framework remains useful because it makes clear that surveillance quality is not only about sensitivity. A system can be sensitive but too slow, too fragile, or too complicated to function well in routine practice.

### Section 9: Why action is the endpoint
Both screening and surveillance can fail by becoming data-collection rituals instead of action systems.

Screening without referral, confirmation, and treatment can create activity without benefit.

Surveillance without analysis, feedback, and response can generate reports without decisions.

This is why epidemiologists often say the endpoint is not detection alone. The endpoint is what detection allows you to do next.

## Key takeaways
- Screening and surveillance are related but distinct activities with different units of action and evaluation.
- Screening focuses on earlier identification in individuals and must be judged by net benefit, not test accuracy alone.
- Surveillance focuses on ongoing population-level information for action and depends on reporting, analysis, dissemination, and response.
- Predictive value depends strongly on prevalence, which is especially important in low-prevalence or elimination settings.
- Both screening and surveillance fail when downstream action is weak.

## Self-check questions
1. What is the main difference between screening and surveillance in terms of unit of action?
2. Why can a technically strong screening test still perform poorly in a low-prevalence setting?
3. What makes surveillance continuous rather than episodic?
4. Name two common attributes used to evaluate surveillance systems.
5. Why is screening best understood as a programme rather than just a test?
6. In elimination settings, why may surveillance design need to change as prevalence falls?

## Answer key
1. Screening acts primarily on individuals, while surveillance acts on populations and systems.
2. Because low prevalence reduces positive predictive value, so many positive results may be false positives despite good sensitivity and specificity.
3. It involves ongoing or repeated collection, analysis, interpretation, dissemination, and response rather than a one-time detection effort.
4. Examples include sensitivity, timeliness, representativeness, flexibility, acceptability, data quality, and stability.
5. Because benefit depends on the whole pathway from detection to confirmation, referral, treatment, quality assurance, and harm reduction.
6. Because low prevalence changes predictive value, resource efficiency, case-finding strategy, and the balance between broad screening and targeted or integrated surveillance.
