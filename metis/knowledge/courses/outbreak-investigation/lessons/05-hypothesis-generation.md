# Hypothesis Generation

## Learning objectives
- Generate plausible explanations for an outbreak using multiple data sources.
- Use interviews, observation, and contextual review to refine hypotheses.
- Avoid locking onto one explanation too early.

## Prerequisites
- Descriptive epidemiology in outbreaks.

## Content

### Section 1: Hypotheses should be plausible and revisable
An outbreak hypothesis usually links agent, source, exposure route, and affected population. Early hypotheses are working explanations, not final truths. Good teams revise them as new information arrives.

### Section 2: Sources of hypotheses
Useful inputs include line-list review, key informant interviews, site visits, food histories, attendance lists, supply-chain information, laboratory clues, and local knowledge. Outbreak reasoning improves when the epidemiology team listens closely to field context instead of relying only on forms.

### Section 3: Avoid tunnel vision
Teams often settle too early on the first compelling story. This can lead to missed mixed exposures, incorrect timing assumptions, or neglect of healthcare-associated spread. A better approach is to keep a short list of competing explanations and ask what evidence would separate them.

### Practical example
During a suspected typhoid cluster, interviews may suggest shared use of a market food vendor, but site inspection might reveal more important contamination in a neighborhood water system. Both hypotheses should remain live until evidence discriminates between them.

## Key takeaways
- Good hypotheses connect source, route, population, and timing.
- Interviews and site visits are often as important as formal datasets.
- Maintaining competing explanations protects against tunnel vision.

## Self-check questions
1. What makes an outbreak hypothesis useful?
2. Why are site visits important for hypothesis generation?
3. What is a risk of locking onto one theory too early?
4. Name three sources of evidence for generating hypotheses.
5. Why should hypotheses remain revisable?

## Answer key
1. It is plausible, specific, and testable with available data.
2. They reveal operational and environmental details not visible in records alone.
3. Investigators may ignore better explanations or miss multiple transmission pathways.
4. Interviews, line lists, site visits, laboratory clues, supply-chain review, or attendance rosters.
5. Because outbreak evidence evolves and early interpretations are often incomplete.
