# Reporting and After-Action Learning

## Learning objectives
- Explain what an outbreak report should document.
- Distinguish operational reporting from scientific publication.
- Use after-action review to strengthen systems after the event.

## Prerequisites
- All prior outbreak investigation lessons.

## Content

### Section 1: Reporting is part of the response
An outbreak report should document what happened, what was done, what was found, and what remains uncertain. Good reports are useful to decision-makers during the event and to future teams afterward.

### Section 2: Core report components
Most outbreak reports include background, event detection, case definition, methods, descriptive findings, analytic findings if relevant, laboratory and environmental evidence, control measures, limitations, and recommendations. The report should also record key dates and operational decisions.

### Section 3: After-action learning
After-action review asks what worked, what failed, and what needs to change in surveillance, logistics, laboratory support, staffing, communication, or governance. The aim is not blame. It is system improvement.

### Section 4: From response note to publication
Not every outbreak investigation becomes a journal paper, but many can produce valuable scientific learning. Publication usually requires clearer methods, stronger framing, and more explicit discussion of limitations than operational reporting alone.

### Practical example
After a district foodborne outbreak, an after-action review may reveal that the investigation itself was strong but laboratory transport delays prevented timely confirmation. That finding should feed into preparedness planning, not disappear once the outbreak ends.

## Key takeaways
- Reports preserve operational memory and support accountability.
- After-action review is a system-strengthening tool, not just a debrief.
- Scientific publication and operational reporting overlap but are not identical.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is outbreak reporting part of response rather than an administrative extra?
2. What should an after-action review focus on?
3. Name four core components of an outbreak report.
4. Why might a good operational report still need substantial work before publication?
5. What is one risk if lessons are not documented after the event?

## Answer key
1. Because decisions, evidence, and lessons need to be documented for action and accountability.
2. What worked, what failed, and what system changes are needed.
3. Background, case definition, methods, findings, control measures, limitations, or recommendations.
4. Journals usually require more formal methods, framing, and discussion of interpretation.
5. The same weaknesses may recur in the next outbreak.
