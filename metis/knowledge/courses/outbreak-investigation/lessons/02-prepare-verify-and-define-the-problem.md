# Prepare, Verify, and Define the Problem

## Learning objectives
- Describe the first operational steps in an outbreak investigation.
- Verify diagnosis, confirm excess cases, and define the initial problem clearly.
- Explain why preparation affects the quality of field investigation.

## Prerequisites
- What counts as an outbreak.

## Content

### Section 1: Preparation is part of epidemiology
Outbreak work starts before the first interview. Teams need roles, permissions, contacts, transport, data tools, and laboratory coordination. Weak preparation leads to missing data, poor biosafety, and delayed action.

### Section 2: Verify the diagnosis
Diagnosis verification means checking whether the reported syndrome or pathogen is what it appears to be. This may involve review of clinical records, discussion with clinicians, repeat testing, or sending samples to a reference laboratory. Misclassification early in an investigation can send the entire response in the wrong direction.

### Section 3: Confirm the excess
If baseline data exist, compare current counts with recent norms. If baseline data are poor, use expert judgment, nearby comparators, or historical knowledge. The point is not statistical perfection. The point is deciding whether unusual transmission or exposure is plausible.

### Section 4: Build an initial working problem statement
A useful working definition names the event, place, period, and affected population. It should be specific enough to guide action but flexible enough to change as evidence improves.

### Practical example
During a suspected foodborne outbreak after a wedding, the team should confirm the diagnosis, secure attendee lists, identify laboratories, prepare interview tools, and define the event window before attempting an analytic study.

## Key takeaways
- Logistics, permissions, and team organization shape the quality of the investigation.
- Verification covers both diagnosis and whether there is a true excess of cases.
- A working problem statement helps align the team and the response.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is diagnosis verification different from confirming an outbreak?
2. What should be in an initial working problem statement?
3. Why can poor preparation bias the investigation?
4. What can teams do when baseline data are weak?
5. Why should the first case definition remain revisable?

## Answer key
1. Diagnosis verification checks what the condition is, while outbreak confirmation checks whether cases exceed expectation.
2. Event, place, time period, and affected population.
3. Because missing tools, unclear roles, or delayed sampling can distort data and timing.
4. Use expert judgment, nearby comparisons, and contextual evidence.
5. Because early information is incomplete and definitions often improve as evidence accumulates.
