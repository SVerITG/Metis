# Descriptive Epidemiology in Outbreaks

## Learning objectives
- Use time, place, and person to characterize an outbreak.
- Explain what epidemic curves and spot maps contribute.
- Identify how descriptive findings shape hypotheses and control measures.

## Prerequisites
- Case definitions and line listing.

## Content

### Section 1: Description comes before explanation
Descriptive epidemiology is often the fastest way to understand an outbreak. Before complex analysis, investigators should ask who is affected, where transmission appears concentrated, and when cases rose and fell.

### Section 2: Time
Onset dates help build an epidemic curve. The shape of that curve can suggest a point-source event, continuous exposure, or propagated person-to-person transmission. The curve also helps define the likely exposure window.

### Section 3: Place
Mapping cases by residence, worksite, facility, or water source can reveal clusters, directional spread, or operational gaps in service access. Even simple hand-drawn maps can be informative in field conditions.

### Section 4: Person
Age, sex, occupation, risk group, vaccination status, and other characteristics help clarify who is at greatest risk. These patterns may point to specific exposures or inequities in prevention.

### Practical example
If a line list shows that most cases are among school-aged children from neighborhoods using the same water point and that onset peaks within three days, the field team already has a plausible exposure hypothesis before formal analysis begins.

## Key takeaways
- Descriptive epidemiology is the backbone of early outbreak reasoning.
- Epidemic curves, maps, and simple tables often generate the best first hypotheses.
- Time, place, and person patterns can guide targeted control rapidly.

## Self-check questions
1. Why should descriptive work usually precede analytic studies?
2. What can the shape of an epidemic curve suggest?
3. Why are simple maps useful during response?
4. Give two person-level characteristics that can help generate hypotheses.
5. How can descriptive findings guide control before certainty is complete?

## Answer key
1. Because it clarifies the event, exposure window, and high-risk groups before formal testing.
2. Whether transmission resembles point source, continuous exposure, or propagated spread.
3. They can reveal clustering, spread, and operational priorities quickly.
4. Age, occupation, sex, vaccination status, school attendance, or residence.
5. They identify likely exposures and affected groups that can be targeted immediately.
