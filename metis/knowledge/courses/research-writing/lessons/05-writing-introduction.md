# Writing the Introduction

## Learning objectives
- Structure an introduction from problem to gap to aim.
- Avoid overlong background sections that weaken the paper.
- End with a precise study objective or question.
- Understand how the introduction prepares the reader for the methods, results, and discussion.

## Prerequisites
- Research question and literature review strategy.

## Content

### Section 1: What the introduction is for
The introduction does not exist to prove that the author has read widely. Its job is to justify the study. A strong introduction tells the reader:

- why the problem matters
- what is already known
- what is still uncertain or inadequate
- what this study will do about that gap

If the reader reaches the methods section still unsure why the study exists, the introduction has failed.

### Section 2: The funnel structure
A useful structure is a narrowing funnel:

1. establish the broader problem
2. summarize the most relevant existing evidence
3. identify the specific gap or unresolved issue
4. state the study aim or objective

This structure works because it takes the reader from general importance to specific necessity. It also helps prevent introductions from turning into aimless literature reviews.

### Section 3: Problem, gap, aim
Most strong introductions can be understood through three core elements:

- **problem:** why the topic matters
- **gap:** what is missing, weak, or unresolved in current evidence
- **aim:** what this study specifically does

Many weak introductions have only the first element. They describe a problem at length but never identify a clear gap. Others state a gap but fail to connect it to the actual study objective. The aim paragraph should feel like the natural consequence of the problem and gap that came before it.

### Section 4: Use literature selectively
An introduction is not the place to summarize every paper. The literature included should be chosen because it supports the argument leading to the study question.

This means the author should prefer:

- pivotal evidence
- methodologically relevant evidence
- directly comparable settings
- papers that clarify the gap

Long exhaustive background sections often weaken introductions because they bury the central argument. Brevity and relevance usually make the logic stronger.

### Section 5: Worked example - surveillance paper introduction
Imagine a paper on sustainable post-elimination HAT surveillance. A strong introduction might move through four steps:

1. elimination success creates a new surveillance challenge
2. rare-event detection is operationally fragile once cases decline
3. existing evidence says relatively little about how to maintain sensitivity sustainably in peripheral systems
4. this study evaluates whether a defined surveillance support strategy improves referral and verification performance in former HAT foci

That structure gives the reader a reason to care and a clear understanding of what the paper will contribute.

### Section 6: The final paragraph matters most
The last paragraph of the introduction is often the most important. It should state clearly:

- the study objective
- the population or setting
- the broad design where relevant
- sometimes the main hypothesis

This paragraph prepares the reader for the methods and later gives the discussion a clear target. If the objective is vague here, the rest of the paper often becomes vague as well.

### Section 7: How the introduction supports the discussion
A good introduction sets up the Discussion by defining the question the Discussion must answer. When the discussion later interprets the results, it should return to the gap and objective established in the introduction.

This is one reason introductions should not overpromise. If the introduction implies a broad causal or policy claim that the methods cannot support, the Discussion becomes strained or overstated.

The introduction should therefore be ambitious enough to show importance, but disciplined enough to match the actual study.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- writing a literature dump without a clear argument
- spending too long on global background and too little on the actual gap
- ending with a vague aim such as "to explore" or "to understand" without precision
- introducing claims that the methods cannot really answer
- using too many citations where a few strong references would do

The practical lesson is that an introduction should be structured persuasion, not accumulated background.

## Key takeaways
- The introduction should justify the study, not showcase everything the author has read.
- Problem, gap, and aim are the core elements of a strong introduction.
- The funnel structure helps move the reader from broad context to specific study objective.
- Selective use of literature usually strengthens rather than weakens the argument.
- The final paragraph should make the study objective explicit and concrete.

## Self-check questions
1. What is the main job of the introduction?
2. Why is the final paragraph especially important?
3. What is the difference between a background section and a literature dump?
4. Why should the introduction identify a specific gap rather than only a broad problem?
5. How does the introduction prepare the Discussion?
6. What is one sign that an introduction is too broad?

## Answer key
1. To justify the study by showing why the problem matters, what gap exists, and what the study will do.
2. Because it states the objective clearly and prepares the reader for the methods and later interpretation.
3. A background section supports the study argument selectively, while a literature dump lists many papers without a clear argumentative function.
4. Because the gap is what makes the specific study necessary rather than merely relevant.
5. It defines the question and contribution that the Discussion must later interpret in light of the results.
6. The reader finishes the introduction still uncertain about the exact study objective or the specific evidence gap being addressed.
