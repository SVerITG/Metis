# Reporting Guidelines and EQUATOR

## Learning objectives
- Navigate the EQUATOR ecosystem and match major guidelines to study design.
- Use reporting checklists during planning and drafting, not only at submission.
- Distinguish reporting quality from methodological quality.
- Recognize how guidelines improve completeness, transparency, and peer-review readiness.

## Prerequisites
- Study protocol development.

## Content

### Section 1: Why reporting guidelines exist
Poor reporting wastes research value. Even a well-designed study becomes hard to assess, reproduce, or use if key details are missing. Readers need to know what was done, how participants were selected, how variables were defined, how missing data were handled, and what limitations remain.

Reporting guidelines were developed to reduce this problem. They do not guarantee good science, but they improve the chance that the science can be understood and evaluated properly.

### Section 2: What EQUATOR provides
The **EQUATOR Network** is the main hub for reporting guidance across study types. It helps researchers identify which checklist matches their design and provides access to supporting materials.

Common examples include:

- **STROBE** for observational studies
- **CONSORT** for randomized trials
- **STARD** for diagnostic accuracy studies
- **PRISMA** for systematic reviews
- **SPIRIT** for clinical trial protocols

The practical value of EQUATOR is not only listing checklists. It helps researchers think design-specifically about what readers and reviewers will need to see.

### Section 3: Reporting quality is not the same as study quality
One of the most important distinctions is this: a reporting guideline improves **completeness of reporting**, not the underlying design by itself.

A badly designed study can still be reported transparently. A strong study can still be reported poorly. The guideline helps the second problem more directly than the first.

This is why checklists should not be treated as a substitute for epidemiologic judgment. They are tools for completeness, not automatic quality stamps.

### Section 4: Why use guidelines early
Many researchers encounter guidelines only when a journal asks for a checklist at submission. That is too late to get the full value.

Used early, checklists help reveal missing design elements before the manuscript is finished. For example, STROBE may expose that participant flow, missing data handling, or confounder definitions are still vague. STARD may reveal that the diagnostic reference standard or indeterminate results have not been thought through clearly.

In that sense, reporting guidelines are also planning tools.

### Section 5: Worked example - STROBE during drafting
Imagine you are writing an observational cohort study on post-elimination surveillance performance. If you consult STROBE only at submission, you may discover late that key details are missing:

- how participants or facilities were selected
- how missing data were handled
- which confounders were adjusted for and why
- how follow-up or reporting flow was managed

If you use STROBE earlier, those omissions can shape the writing and even the analysis plan before peer review exposes them.

That is why experienced researchers often keep the relevant checklist nearby while drafting rather than treating it as an afterthought.

### Section 6: Matching guideline to design
The right guideline depends on the design actually used, not the label you prefer to apply.

For example:

- diagnostic test studies should usually start with STARD
- observational epidemiology should usually consider STROBE
- systematic reviews should usually use PRISMA
- randomized trials should usually use CONSORT

Choosing the wrong checklist can leave important reporting areas uncovered. Design clarity therefore comes before checklist selection.

### Section 7: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- completing the checklist only after the manuscript is essentially finished
- using a guideline as a performative journal requirement rather than a real drafting aid
- assuming checklist completion proves methodological quality
- selecting a checklist that does not fit the actual design
- ignoring extensions that better fit specialized designs

The practical lesson is that guidelines work best when they are integrated into workflow, not appended at the end.

## Key takeaways
- Reporting guidelines exist to improve completeness and transparency of research reports.
- EQUATOR helps researchers identify the right guideline for the study design.
- Checklists improve reporting quality, not methodological quality by themselves.
- The strongest use of reporting guidelines is early in planning and drafting, not only at submission.
- Matching the right checklist to the actual design is part of good writing discipline.

## Self-check questions
1. Which major guideline fits a diagnostic accuracy study?
2. Why use reporting checklists before submission rather than only at the end?
3. What is the difference between reporting quality and methodological quality?
4. Why can choosing the wrong guideline be a problem?
5. What does EQUATOR provide that is practically useful?
6. Why is checklist completion not a guarantee of a strong study?

## Answer key
1. STARD.
2. Because they can reveal missing design, analysis, and reporting elements while there is still time to fix them.
3. Reporting quality concerns how clearly and completely the study is described, while methodological quality concerns how well the study was designed and conducted.
4. Because key items relevant to the actual design may be missed, leaving important reporting gaps.
5. A central hub linking reporting guidelines to study designs and providing supporting materials for their use.
6. Because a study can be reported completely and still be poorly designed or biased.
