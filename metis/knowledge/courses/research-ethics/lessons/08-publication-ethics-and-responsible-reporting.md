# Publication Ethics and Responsible Reporting

## Learning objectives
- Describe major publication-ethics issues relevant to researchers.
- Explain why transparency and accurate reporting are ethical obligations.
- Recognize common integrity problems in authorship and dissemination.

## Prerequisites
- Emergency and outbreak research ethics.

## Content
Publication ethics covers authorship, plagiarism, duplicate publication, selective reporting, conflicts of interest, image or data manipulation, and the obligation to describe methods and limitations honestly. These are not merely journal rules. They affect whether the evidence base can be trusted.

Responsible reporting also includes acknowledging uncertainty, following reporting guidelines, and avoiding exaggerated claims that can distort policy or practice. In public health, poor reporting can misdirect programmes and waste scarce resources.

## Key takeaways
- Publication ethics is part of research ethics, not a separate problem.
- Honest reporting of methods, limits, and conflicts is essential.
- Misconduct can damage both the literature and downstream public health action.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is selective reporting ethically problematic?
2. What is one authorship-related ethical issue?
3. Why are conflicts of interest relevant to readers?
4. How can exaggerated conclusions cause harm?
5. What role do reporting guidelines play in research integrity?

## Answer key
1. Because it distorts the evidence base and misleads decisions.
2. Gift authorship or exclusion of deserving contributors.
3. They may influence study design, interpretation, or credibility.
4. They can lead to poor policy or clinical decisions.
5. They improve transparency and completeness of reporting.
