# Justice, Equity, and Community Engagement

## Learning objectives
- Explain what justice means in research participation and benefit.
- Describe why community engagement matters in public health studies.
- Recognize unfair distributions of burden and benefit.

## Prerequisites
- Privacy, confidentiality, and data governance.

## Content
Justice in research asks who is recruited, who bears inconvenience or risk, and who benefits from the knowledge produced. Studies can be unfair when marginalized populations are overused as data sources but under-served by the resulting interventions or publications.

Community engagement improves ethics and science. It helps researchers understand local priorities, refine methods, avoid harmful assumptions, and make participation more respectful. Engagement should be substantive rather than symbolic.

## Key takeaways
- Justice concerns fair burden, benefit, and representation.
- Community engagement improves both ethics and study quality.
- Extractive research often reflects failures of equity and partnership.

## Self-check questions
1. What does justice ask in research?
2. Why is community engagement scientifically useful?
3. What is one sign of extractive research?
4. Why can fairness not be judged by participant numbers alone?
5. How can engagement improve protocol design?

## Answer key
1. Who bears burdens, who benefits, and whether participation is fair.
2. Because it improves relevance, feasibility, and contextual accuracy.
3. Communities contribute data but see little benefit or recognition.
4. Because fairness also concerns power, benefit sharing, and access to findings.
5. By identifying local risks, barriers, and acceptable procedures.
