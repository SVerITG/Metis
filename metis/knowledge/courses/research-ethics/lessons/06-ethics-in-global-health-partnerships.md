# Ethics in Global Health Partnerships

## Learning objectives
- Identify ethical issues specific to cross-border and North-South research partnerships.
- Explain why authorship, ownership, and benefit sharing are ethical issues.
- Recognize how power imbalances shape collaboration.

## Prerequisites
- Justice, equity, and community engagement.

## Content
Global health partnerships often involve unequal access to funding, infrastructure, publication channels, and agenda-setting power. Ethics in these settings goes beyond participant protection. It includes fair authorship, data ownership, capacity strengthening, local leadership, and avoidance of extractive project design.

Partnerships become ethically stronger when priorities are co-developed, budgets support local institutions meaningfully, local investigators lead interpretation where appropriate, and outputs return value to the settings where data were generated.

## Key takeaways
- Partnership ethics includes power, authorship, ownership, and capacity.
- Ethical collaboration requires more than local data collection permissions.
- Benefit sharing and local leadership are central issues in global health research.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is authorship an ethical issue in global health?
2. What is one sign of an extractive partnership?
3. Why does agenda-setting power matter?
4. What is one way to improve fairness in collaboration?
5. Why is local leadership important beyond symbolism?

## Answer key
1. Because it reflects recognition, accountability, and distribution of academic benefit.
2. Data are collected locally but analysis and recognition remain elsewhere.
3. Because it determines whose questions and priorities the research serves.
4. Shared agenda setting, equitable budgeting, or capacity strengthening.
5. Because local expertise improves relevance, legitimacy, and sustainability.
