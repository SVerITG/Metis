# Why Health Economics Matters in Public Health

## Learning objectives
- Explain why scarcity and opportunity cost matter in public health.
- Describe how epidemiology and economics answer different decision questions.
- Recognize when health economics should enter programme planning.

## Prerequisites
- Basic epidemiology.

## Content
Public health decisions are made under scarcity. Money, staff time, laboratory capacity, vehicles, medicines, and political attention are all limited. Health economics studies how choices should be made when not every valuable action can be funded at once.

Epidemiology tells us who is affected, how much disease exists, and what factors shape risk. Economics adds a different question: given limited resources, which option gives the best value, and what are we giving up by choosing it? That idea is opportunity cost.

For example, a very effective intervention may still be a poor policy choice if it consumes resources that would prevent more deaths elsewhere. Health economics therefore matters when decisions involve trade-offs across programmes, target groups, or implementation strategies.

## Key takeaways
- Scarcity makes prioritization unavoidable.
- Epidemiology identifies problems; economics helps compare choices.
- Opportunity cost is central to policy thinking.

## Self-check questions
1. What does opportunity cost mean?
2. Why is epidemiologic effectiveness alone not enough for policy choice?
3. Name three scarce resources in public health.
4. When should economics enter programme planning?
5. Why can a good intervention still be a poor allocation choice?

## Answer key
1. The value of what is given up by choosing one option over another.
2. Because policy also depends on costs, affordability, and trade-offs.
3. Money, workforce time, laboratory capacity, transport, or political attention.
4. Whenever decisions involve competing uses of limited resources.
5. Because the same resources might generate larger health gains elsewhere.
