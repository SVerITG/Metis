# Measuring Health Outcomes for Economic Evaluation

## Learning objectives
- Compare cases averted, life-years, DALYs, and QALYs as outcome measures.
- Explain why outcome choice affects comparability across studies.
- Recognize trade-offs between simplicity and comparability.

## Prerequisites
- Costs, perspectives, and time horizons.

## Content
Economic evaluation needs an outcome metric. Some studies use programme-specific outcomes such as cases detected or deaths averted. Others use broader measures such as life-years gained, disability-adjusted life years averted, or quality-adjusted life years gained.

Specific outcomes are often easier for programme teams to understand, but they are hard to compare across diseases. Broader summary measures improve comparability but require stronger assumptions about disability, quality of life, or life expectancy. The right choice depends on the decision context and the audience.

## Key takeaways
- Outcome measures shape what comparisons are possible.
- Disease-specific outcomes are intuitive but narrow.
- DALYs and QALYs broaden comparison but add assumptions.

## Self-check questions
1. Why might a study use cases averted instead of DALYs?
2. What is one benefit of DALYs or QALYs?
3. Why does outcome choice affect comparability?
4. What is one drawback of broad summary measures?
5. When is a programme-specific outcome still useful?

## Answer key
1. Because it may be more intuitive for the local decision problem.
2. They allow comparison across different diseases and interventions.
3. Because not all metrics can be compared directly across studies.
4. They depend on additional assumptions and modeling choices.
5. When the decision is narrow and specific to one programme.
