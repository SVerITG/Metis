# Costs, Perspectives, and Time Horizons

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish financial from economic costs.
- Explain why perspective changes which costs matter.
- Describe why time horizon affects conclusions.

## Prerequisites
- Why health economics matters in public health.

## Content
Costs are not just invoices. Financial costs are direct expenditures, while economic costs also include the value of donated goods, volunteer time, and resources used in alternative ways. Perspective determines which costs are counted. A provider perspective focuses on the health system; a societal perspective includes patient time, transport, and productivity effects.

Time horizon also matters. A vaccination programme may look expensive in year one but favorable over ten years if it prevents later treatment costs and deaths. Short horizons can understate long-term value, while very long horizons may rely on assumptions that require careful justification.

## Key takeaways
- Perspective determines whose costs are counted.
- Economic costs are broader than financial costs.
- Time horizon can change the apparent value of an intervention.

## Self-check questions
1. What is one difference between financial and economic cost?
2. Why does perspective matter?
3. What might a societal perspective include that a provider perspective omits?
4. Why can short time horizons mislead?
5. What should authors justify when choosing a time horizon?

## Answer key
1. Economic cost includes opportunity cost and non-purchased resources.
2. Because different stakeholders bear different costs.
3. Patient travel time, lost wages, or household expenditure.
4. They may miss downstream savings or health gains.
5. Why that period matches the intervention and decision problem.
