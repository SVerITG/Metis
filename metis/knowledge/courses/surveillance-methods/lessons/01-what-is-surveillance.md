# What Is Surveillance?

## Learning objectives
- Define surveillance in operational public-health terms.
- Distinguish surveillance from research, service statistics, and screening.
- Explain why dissemination and response are part of the surveillance definition.
- Recognize why system purpose must be explicit before indicators or dashboards are designed.

## Prerequisites
- Basic epidemiology.

## Content

### Section 1: The core definition
Public-health surveillance is the **ongoing, systematic collection, analysis, interpretation, and dissemination of health data for public-health action**. That final phrase matters most. Surveillance is not only about gathering numbers. It is a decision system designed to support action.

This is why surveillance is often described as "information for action." If data are collected but never interpreted, disseminated, or used, the system may produce reports, but it is not functioning well as surveillance.

### Section 2: Why continuity matters
Surveillance is different from one-time data collection because it is continuous or recurrent. It tracks signals over time, allowing public-health teams to notice change, detect unusual events, monitor burden, and assess whether interventions are working.

Continuity makes surveillance useful for:

- early warning
- trend monitoring
- elimination follow-up
- programme management
- outbreak detection and response

A one-off survey can be informative, but it is not the same as a surveillance system.

### Section 3: What surveillance is not
Surveillance is often confused with several related activities.

**Research** aims to generate generalizable knowledge. It often uses formal study designs and may not be embedded in routine response.

**Service statistics** record programme activity, but they become surveillance only if they are analyzed and used systematically for action.

**Screening** targets individuals to identify disease or risk early. Surveillance targets populations and systems.

These distinctions matter because the design, evaluation, and ethical logic of each activity differ.

### Section 4: Why dissemination is part of the definition
Many beginners remember the words collection and analysis but forget dissemination. This is a major mistake.

Surveillance without dissemination is incomplete because the information never reaches the people who can:

- verify a signal
- change a protocol
- deploy resources
- supervise facilities
- communicate risk

In operational settings, dissemination may take the form of weekly bulletins, district dashboards, alert messages, outbreak reports, feedback calls, or supervisory meetings. The format matters less than the function: the information must move to where decisions are made.

### Section 5: Worked example - weekly reporting with action
Imagine health facilities submit weekly reports on suspected cholera cases. The district team compiles the reports, monitors thresholds, contacts facilities with unusual increases, verifies suspect clusters, and sends targeted supervision to affected areas.

That is surveillance because:

- data are collected continuously
- the information is analyzed
- findings are disseminated
- action follows from the signal

If the same forms were collected and stored without review or follow-up, the system would be much weaker operationally, even if the database looked complete.

### Section 6: Purpose must come before indicators
One of the most common design errors is to start with indicators rather than with purpose.

A surveillance system should begin by asking:

- what decision is the system meant to support?
- what event or burden is being monitored?
- who needs the information?
- how quickly must they receive it?

Only after those questions are answered should the team decide on case definitions, reporting intervals, data elements, or dashboard structure.

Without a clear purpose, systems tend to accumulate indicators that are expensive to report and hard to use.

### Section 7: Surveillance as a system, not a form
A surveillance system includes more than forms or software. It includes:

- case detection
- reporting channels
- data management
- analysis
- interpretation
- feedback
- response processes
- workforce capacity

This is why the same reporting template can function very differently across settings. In one district, it may trigger rapid verification and action. In another, it may simply be archived. The difference is the system around the form.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- assuming data collection alone equals surveillance
- forgetting dissemination and feedback
- designing indicators before defining the decision problem
- confusing screening programmes with surveillance systems
- treating a dashboard as the system rather than one output of the system

The practical lesson is that surveillance is operational infrastructure, not just information storage.

## Key takeaways
- Surveillance is the ongoing, systematic collection, analysis, interpretation, and dissemination of data for public-health action.
- Continuity and action orientation distinguish surveillance from one-time surveys or passive data accumulation.
- Dissemination and feedback are core components, not optional extras.
- Surveillance differs from research, screening, and routine service statistics in purpose and use.
- System purpose should be explicit before indicators and reporting structures are designed.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is dissemination part of the definition of surveillance?
2. How does surveillance differ from a prevalence survey?
3. Why is a reporting database not automatically a surveillance system?
4. Why should surveillance purpose be defined before choosing indicators?
5. How is surveillance different from screening?
6. What makes a reporting system operationally useful?

## Answer key
1. Because the information must reach decision-makers and responders; otherwise the system does not complete the information-for-action loop.
2. Surveillance is ongoing and action-oriented, while a prevalence survey is usually a one-time or periodic measurement exercise.
3. Because storage alone does not ensure analysis, interpretation, dissemination, feedback, or response.
4. Because indicators should be chosen to support specific decisions rather than collected simply because they are available.
5. Surveillance targets population-level monitoring and action, while screening targets early detection in individuals.
6. Regular analysis, timely dissemination, clear thresholds or triggers, and real follow-up actions.
