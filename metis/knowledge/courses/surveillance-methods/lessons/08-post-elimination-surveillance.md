# Post-Elimination Surveillance

## Learning objectives
- Explain why surveillance often becomes harder rather than easier after elimination targets are reached.
- Identify the design features needed when cases are rare and predictive value declines.
- Link elimination verification to system sensitivity, confirmation, and response capacity.
- Recognize why expertise decay and falling political attention are themselves surveillance risks.

## Prerequisites
- All previous surveillance lessons.

## Content

### Section 1: The paradox of rarity
Post-elimination surveillance is difficult because success changes the surveillance problem. When cases become rare:

- health workers see fewer real cases
- suspicion declines
- positive predictive value often worsens
- funding pressure increases
- the political system may assume the problem is solved

Yet the cost of missing a true case can remain high, especially if transmission can re-establish or importations can spark local resurgence.

This is the paradox of rarity: fewer cases can make the system harder to interpret and easier to weaken.

### Section 2: Why low prevalence changes interpretation
As prevalence drops, many signals that once had clear meaning become harder to interpret. False positives become more operationally costly, and positive screening or alert signals need stronger verification.

This is why post-elimination systems often require:

- stronger laboratory confirmation pathways
- careful case investigation
- case-based records
- explicit response triggers

The system cannot rely only on crude counts or broad screening signals. It needs methods that preserve sensitivity while keeping enough specificity to avoid constant false alarms.

### Section 3: Declining vigilance is a system risk
One of the biggest threats after elimination is not statistical. It is organizational.

Health workers may lose familiarity with the disease. Supervisors may stop emphasizing it. Referral pathways may weaken. Laboratories may no longer maintain routine competence. Communities may stop recognizing symptoms or relevance.

This means surveillance sensitivity can decline even without any formal policy change. Expertise decay is therefore part of the surveillance design problem.

### Section 4: Worked example - sleeping sickness after elimination
Sleeping sickness is a good example because the system must transition from higher-intensity case finding to lower-incidence vigilance without losing the ability to detect residual or imported cases.

A realistic post-elimination design may include:

- passive detection in peripheral facilities
- referral-linked confirmation
- case-based investigation of every suspected or confirmed case
- geographic targeting of known higher-risk foci
- periodic supervision and retraining

This is different from mass screening, but it is not the same as ordinary neglectful passive surveillance. It is a deliberately designed low-incidence vigilance system.

### Section 5: Sensitivity must remain linked to response
In post-elimination settings, sensitivity only matters if it is linked to response capacity.

If a system can detect a suspect case but cannot:

- confirm it
- investigate contacts or setting
- review local surveillance sensitivity
- respond geographically or operationally

then the system may detect signals without controlling risk.

That is why post-elimination surveillance should be judged not only by detection metrics, but also by the completeness and speed of the response chain.

### Section 6: Risk-based and trigger-based design
Because broad high-intensity surveillance is often unsustainable after elimination, many systems use:

- risk-based targeting of known foci or border areas
- trigger-based investigation after suspect or confirmed cases
- periodic external review
- sentinel strengthening in selected locations

This can be more sustainable than trying to maintain maximal intensity everywhere. But it depends on good understanding of residual risk and clear operational triggers.

### Section 7: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- assuming rarity means the system can simply be downgraded
- interpreting low case counts as proof the system remains sensitive
- neglecting retraining and diagnostic readiness
- using broad screening tools without adapting confirmation pathways
- separating surveillance design from response planning

The practical lesson is that post-elimination surveillance needs deliberate design. It does not maintain itself automatically after success.

## Key takeaways
- Post-elimination surveillance is difficult because rarity reduces vigilance, lowers predictive value, and increases the impact of missed cases.
- Low-incidence settings need strong confirmation pathways, case-based investigation, and clear response rules.
- Expertise decay and declining political attention are major surveillance risks.
- Risk-based targeting and trigger-based response are often more sustainable than uniform high-intensity surveillance.
- System sensitivity must remain connected to concrete operational response capacity.

## Self-check questions
1. Why does rarity make surveillance interpretation harder?
2. Why can expertise decay become a surveillance risk after elimination?
3. What design features often support post-elimination surveillance?
4. Why is a low case count not enough to prove the system is working well?
5. Why are confirmation pathways especially important in low-prevalence settings?
6. Why should response planning be considered part of post-elimination surveillance design?

## Answer key
1. Because false positives become more common relative to true cases, while true events become harder to detect and verify.
2. Because health workers, laboratories, and supervisors may lose familiarity with the disease, reducing detection sensitivity even without formal system change.
3. Case-based investigation, strong confirmation, risk-based targeting, trigger-based response, retraining, and periodic review.
4. Because low counts may reflect weak sensitivity, missed cases, or declining vigilance rather than true interruption of transmission.
5. Because the predictive value of preliminary signals is lower, so stronger verification is needed before major decisions.
6. Because detection without confirmation, investigation, and operational action does not adequately protect elimination gains.
