# Evaluation Using CDC Attributes

## Learning objectives
- Use the CDC surveillance-attribute framework to evaluate a surveillance system.
- Distinguish usefulness, simplicity, flexibility, data quality, acceptability, sensitivity, positive predictive value, representativeness, timeliness, and stability.
- Recognize how improvements in one attribute can weaken another.
- Translate evaluation findings into realistic redesign priorities.

## Prerequisites
- Data flow and system types.

## Content

### Section 1: Why evaluate a surveillance system?
Surveillance systems consume money, time, staff attention, and political capital. Evaluation asks whether the system is actually useful and whether its design fits its stated purpose.

This matters because a system can be busy without being effective. It may collect many data elements, burden frontline staff, and produce attractive dashboards while still failing to detect events early, guide action, or maintain acceptable data quality.

Evaluation is therefore not an academic add-on. It is part of responsible system stewardship.

### Section 2: Usefulness comes first
In the CDC framework, **usefulness** is central. Before discussing technical attributes, ask whether the system actually contributes to prevention, response, or programme management.

A system may be simple and stable yet not useful if it never changes decisions. Conversely, a somewhat messy system may still be valuable if it reliably supports timely response in a high-risk setting.

This is why usefulness should frame the rest of the evaluation.

### Section 3: Core CDC attributes
Common CDC attributes include:

- **simplicity**
- **flexibility**
- **data quality**
- **acceptability**
- **sensitivity**
- **positive predictive value**
- **representativeness**
- **timeliness**
- **stability**

These attributes help evaluators break a complex system into interpretable dimensions. No single attribute is enough on its own, and not all matter equally in every context.

### Section 4: What the attributes mean in practice
**Simplicity** refers to how easy the system is to operate and understand.

**Flexibility** refers to how well it adapts to changing needs.

**Data quality** concerns completeness and validity.

**Acceptability** refers to whether participants are willing to report and use the system.

**Sensitivity** concerns the proportion of relevant events the system detects.

**Positive predictive value** concerns how many reported cases or signals are true events.

**Representativeness** asks whether the system reflects occurrence by person, place, and time accurately.

**Timeliness** asks how quickly information moves through the system.

**Stability** concerns reliability and continuity of operation.

These attributes are practical, not abstract. They can often be observed through routine records, interviews, and operational timelines.

### Section 5: Worked example - active versus passive system trade-off
Imagine a high-sensitivity active surveillance system for an elimination programme. It detects more cases than the routine passive system, but it is expensive, logistically fragile, and difficult to sustain nationally.

Evaluation may find:

- strong sensitivity
- weak stability
- limited simplicity
- poor long-term feasibility

This does not mean the system is a failure. It means the system may be well suited to a targeted elimination phase but poorly suited to indefinite nationwide scale-up.

The CDC framework helps make those trade-offs explicit.

### Section 6: Attributes interact
One of the most important lessons is that surveillance attributes interact.

For example:

- increasing sensitivity may reduce positive predictive value
- increasing data detail may reduce simplicity and acceptability
- improving timeliness may reduce completeness if deadlines become unrealistic
- adding multiple verification steps may improve quality but slow response

This means evaluation should not ask, "How do we maximize every attribute?" That is usually impossible. It should ask, "Which balance of attributes best serves the system's purpose?"

### Section 7: How evaluation should be done
Good evaluation usually includes:

- stakeholder engagement
- clear description of the system
- a focused evaluation question
- evidence from records, timelines, observations, and interviews
- justified recommendations

Not every evaluation needs every metric. A district cholera alert system and a post-elimination case-based surveillance system may need different emphases. The framework should support the question, not replace it.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- evaluating metrics without asking whether the system is useful
- treating all attributes as equally important in every context
- giving recommendations that are technically elegant but operationally impossible
- confusing completeness of data with value of the system
- ignoring reporter burden and frontline acceptability

The practical lesson is that surveillance evaluation is about redesigning a working system, not scoring it from a distance.

## Key takeaways
- Surveillance evaluation asks whether a system is useful and whether its design matches its purpose.
- The CDC framework offers a practical set of attributes for structured evaluation.
- Attributes such as sensitivity, timeliness, stability, and acceptability often involve trade-offs.
- Usefulness should anchor interpretation, because technically tidy systems may still be operationally weak.
- Recommendations should be realistic, context-specific, and linked to system purpose.

## Self-check questions
1. Why should usefulness be considered before other surveillance attributes?
2. What does acceptability mean in surveillance evaluation?
3. Why can timeliness and completeness move in opposite directions?
4. Why is sensitivity not the only attribute that matters?
5. Why might a highly sensitive system still be a poor national design?
6. What makes a surveillance evaluation recommendation useful?

## Answer key
1. Because a system that does not support action is weak even if some technical attributes look good.
2. The willingness of participants and institutions to engage with and contribute to the system.
3. Because very fast deadlines may reduce the proportion of reports submitted fully or correctly.
4. Because a system must also be stable, acceptable, timely, and operationally feasible to function well.
5. Because it may be too expensive, too complex, or too fragile to sustain at scale.
6. It must be feasible, linked to evidence, and relevant to the actual purpose of the system.
