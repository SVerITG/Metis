# Case Definitions

## Learning objectives
- Explain the operational role of suspected, probable, and confirmed case definitions.
- Balance sensitivity and specificity according to surveillance purpose.
- Understand how case definitions interact with laboratory confirmation and response rules.
- Recognize why definition changes must be documented to preserve comparability.

## Prerequisites
- Indicator-based vs event-based surveillance.

## Content

### Section 1: Why case definitions matter
Case definitions are one of the central design tools of surveillance. They determine who counts as a case for reporting, analysis, and response. Without a shared definition, apparent trends may reflect changing interpretation rather than changing disease occurrence.

This is why case definitions are not merely technical wording. They shape sensitivity, specificity, workload, and comparability.

### Section 2: Case definitions are surveillance tools, not clinical truths
A surveillance case definition is designed for public-health use, not for perfect bedside diagnosis. It often aims to standardize reporting across facilities and time periods rather than to capture every clinical nuance.

That means a case definition may differ from the way clinicians think about diagnosis in individual patients. This is not a flaw. It reflects the fact that surveillance needs consistency and operational clarity.

### Section 3: Suspected, probable, and confirmed
Many systems use a tiered structure:

- **suspected case:** broad criteria, often sensitive and useful for alerting
- **probable case:** additional epidemiologic or clinical evidence, but not full confirmation
- **confirmed case:** laboratory or otherwise definitive confirmation according to the system's rules

This structure allows the system to separate early detection from final classification. Broad early definitions can trigger verification and response, while confirmed definitions support more specific reporting and burden estimation.

### Section 4: Fit-for-purpose design
The right balance between sensitivity and specificity depends on purpose.

For early warning, outbreak alerting, or rare high-consequence diseases, a broader definition may be appropriate because missing a true case is costly.

For elimination verification, burden reporting, or resource allocation where false positives are especially problematic, a narrower and more specific definition may be necessary.

This means there is no single universally "best" case definition. The best definition is the one that fits the surveillance objective.

### Section 5: Worked example - cholera alerting versus final reporting
Imagine a district wants rapid cholera detection during the rainy season. The suspected case definition may be intentionally broad, using acute watery diarrhea in a relevant age group plus geographic context.

That definition supports sensitivity and fast alerting.

For final reporting, however, confirmed cases may require laboratory evidence or stricter epidemiologic linkage. The confirmed definition is more specific and more appropriate for final burden summaries.

Both definitions serve the system, but they serve different moments in the workflow.

### Section 6: Why definition changes are risky
Case definitions sometimes need to change because diagnostics improve, epidemiology shifts, or the programme enters a different phase such as elimination. But each change risks breaking comparability over time.

If a system silently tightens or broadens its definition, a rise or fall in reported cases may reflect the definitional change rather than a real epidemiologic shift.

That is why all case-definition changes should be:

- documented clearly
- time-stamped
- communicated to reporters
- considered when interpreting trends

### Section 7: Case definitions and laboratory confirmation
It is important to separate the case definition from the confirmation pathway. A system may use a broad suspected definition but require confirmatory laboratory testing before escalating certain actions or finalizing counts.

This distinction matters in low-prevalence and post-elimination settings, where the predictive value of preliminary classifications may be poor. A programme may need a sensitive detection rule but a very specific confirmation algorithm.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- treating surveillance case definitions as if they were perfect clinical diagnoses
- using one definition for all surveillance purposes without considering trade-offs
- changing definitions without documenting the shift
- failing to separate case classification from response thresholds
- assuming more specificity is always better

The practical lesson is that case definitions should be judged by how well they support the system's purpose, not by whether they look maximally rigorous in the abstract.

## Key takeaways
- Case definitions standardize who counts in surveillance and strongly influence the data the system produces.
- Suspected, probable, and confirmed categories serve different operational purposes.
- The balance between sensitivity and specificity should follow the surveillance goal.
- Changes in case definitions must be documented because they affect comparability over time.
- Laboratory confirmation and surveillance classification are related but not identical concepts.

## Self-check questions
1. Why might early alerts need broad case definitions?
2. What is the operational value of a probable case category?
3. Why can changing a case definition distort time trends?
4. Why is a surveillance case definition not the same as a clinical diagnosis?
5. Why might elimination settings require stricter confirmation rules?
6. What should be documented whenever a case definition changes?

## Answer key
1. Because surveillance may prioritize sensitivity and rapid detection when the cost of missing a true case is high.
2. It allows cases with substantial evidence but not full confirmation to be tracked and managed separately.
3. Because reported counts may change due to the definition rather than due to real epidemiologic change.
4. Because surveillance definitions are built for standardization and operational reporting, not to capture every diagnostic nuance of bedside care.
5. Because prevalence is low, false positives become more operationally costly, and verification standards may need to be stricter.
6. The exact wording, date of change, rationale, and implications for interpretation and reporting.
