# Reproducible Scripts and Quarto Reports

## Learning objectives
- Explain why analysis and reporting should be linked reproducibly.
- Describe what Quarto adds to an R workflow.
- Recognize the value of scripted outputs for manuscripts and programme work.

## Prerequisites
- Statistical testing and simple models.

## Content
Reproducible reporting means the same code that cleans and analyzes data can also generate tables, figures, and narrative summaries. Quarto supports this by combining text, code, and output in one document. That makes analytical decisions easier to audit and updates easier to deliver.

This matters for regular surveillance reports, manuscript tables, technical appendices, and exploratory notebooks. The main benefit is not elegance. It is reducing manual transfer errors between analysis and reporting.

## Key takeaways
- Reproducible reports reduce manual reporting drift.
- Quarto connects code, output, and explanation.
- Linked analysis-reporting workflows improve auditability.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is manual transfer from analysis to report risky?
2. What does Quarto combine?
3. Why is reproducible reporting useful in surveillance?
4. What is one benefit for manuscript work?
5. Why is this better than scattered scripts and copied tables?

## Answer key
1. Because copy-paste can introduce errors and inconsistency.
2. Narrative text, code, and computed output.
3. Reports can be regenerated consistently after data updates.
4. Tables and figures can be refreshed without rebuilding them manually.
5. The workflow becomes easier to trace and update.
