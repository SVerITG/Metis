# What Elimination Means

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish control, elimination as a public health problem, interruption of transmission, and eradication.
- Explain why elimination language matters for strategy and evaluation.
- Recognize that technical success and programmatic success are not always identical.

## Prerequisites
- Basic epidemiology and surveillance.

## Content
Elimination language shapes programme design. Control generally means reducing disease burden to an acceptable level. Elimination as a public health problem often refers to reaching a defined threshold of low burden. Interruption of transmission is stronger: it implies no continuing endemic transmission in the defined area. Eradication goes further and means permanent global disappearance.

These distinctions matter because each goal implies different indicators, interventions, and surveillance obligations. A programme that achieves low case numbers may still need substantial investment if interruption has not been demonstrated. Likewise, apparent interruption still requires vigilance against reintroduction or hidden transmission.

## Key takeaways
- Elimination terms are not interchangeable.
- Programme goals determine evidence needs and surveillance design.
- Low burden is not the same as interrupted transmission.

## Self-check questions
1. How does elimination differ from eradication?
2. Why does terminology matter operationally?
3. What is one risk of calling low burden elimination too early?
4. Why might surveillance continue after success is declared?
5. What is one difference between control and interruption of transmission?

## Answer key
1. Elimination is usually area-specific; eradication is global and permanent.
2. Because goals determine interventions, evidence standards, and oversight.
3. Programmes may relax too soon while transmission persists.
4. To detect hidden transmission or reintroduction.
5. Control tolerates some ongoing transmission; interruption aims to stop endemic transmission entirely.
