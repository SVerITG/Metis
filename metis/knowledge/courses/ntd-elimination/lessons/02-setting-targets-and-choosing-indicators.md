# Setting Targets and Choosing Indicators

## Learning objectives
- Describe how elimination targets are translated into operational indicators.
- Explain why indicator choice affects programme behavior.
- Recognize the difference between monitoring indicators and verification evidence.

## Prerequisites
- What elimination means.

## Content
Elimination programmes need indicators that are meaningful, measurable, and aligned with the strategic goal. Some indicators monitor service delivery or case detection. Others contribute to verification of elimination claims. These are not always the same.

Poor indicator choice can distort priorities. If programmes focus only on reported case counts, they may underinvest in diagnostic quality, equity, or surveillance sensitivity. Strong elimination monitoring therefore usually combines burden, surveillance performance, and system-readiness indicators.

## Key takeaways
- Indicator choice shapes programme behavior.
- Monitoring indicators and verification evidence are related but distinct.
- Elimination metrics should capture both disease and system performance.

## Self-check questions
1. Why are reported case counts alone often insufficient?
2. What is one difference between monitoring and verification indicators?
3. Why can poor indicators distort a programme?
4. What should elimination indicators usually capture besides burden?
5. Why must indicators be feasible to measure?

## Answer key
1. Because low counts may reflect weak detection rather than true success.
2. Monitoring supports routine management; verification supports credibility of the claim.
3. They can shift effort toward visible metrics rather than actual elimination readiness.
4. Surveillance quality, diagnostic capacity, or equity of coverage.
5. Because unusable indicators do not support real programme decisions.
