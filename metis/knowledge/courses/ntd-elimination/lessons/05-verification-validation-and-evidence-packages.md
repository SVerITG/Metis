# Verification, Validation, and Evidence Packages

## Learning objectives
- Explain how programmes demonstrate elimination credibly.
- Distinguish routine monitoring from formal verification or validation.
- Describe what an evidence package usually needs to contain.

## Prerequisites
- Surveillance in the endgame.

## Content
Formal elimination claims require more than encouraging trends. Programmes generally need a structured evidence package showing burden decline, surveillance performance, intervention history, diagnostic quality, and governance arrangements. Depending on the disease, this may be called verification, validation, or dossier review.

The central idea is credibility. External reviewers need enough evidence to judge whether the claim is defensible, whether surveillance is adequate, and whether the risk of hidden transmission is acceptably low.

## Key takeaways
- Verification requires structured evidence, not just optimism.
- Evidence packages usually combine burden, surveillance, and systems data.
- External credibility is part of elimination governance.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is routine monitoring alone not enough for verification?
2. What is one component of an elimination evidence package?
3. Why do external reviewers matter?
4. What is one risk of weak evidence packages?
5. Why is governance information relevant?

## Answer key
1. Because verification requires stronger evidence and review than routine management.
2. Surveillance performance, intervention history, or diagnostic quality evidence.
3. They help ensure the claim is credible and not self-validated.
4. Programmes may declare success without defensible evidence.
5. Because sustainability and accountability affect confidence in continued success.
