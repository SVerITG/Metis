# Surveillance in the Endgame

## Learning objectives
- Explain why surveillance gets harder as disease becomes rarer.
- Describe key endgame surveillance challenges.
- Recognize why zero reported cases are not enough.

## Prerequisites
- Intervention packages for elimination.

## Content
As disease declines, surveillance becomes more difficult and more important. Rare cases are easier to miss, clinical suspicion drops, diagnostic quality may degrade, and reporting systems can atrophy. This creates a paradox: programmes need stronger surveillance precisely when the disease appears to be disappearing.

Endgame surveillance therefore focuses on sensitivity, diagnostic readiness, timeliness, and strategic placement of detection capacity. Apparent absence of cases must be interpreted alongside evidence about how hard the system is looking.

## Key takeaways
- Rarity makes detection harder, not easier.
- Endgame surveillance is a core elimination function.
- Zero cases mean little without evidence of detection capacity.

## Self-check questions
1. Why can declining incidence weaken surveillance?
2. What is one reason zero cases are hard to interpret?
3. Why does clinical suspicion matter in the endgame?
4. What should programmes assess besides case counts?
5. Why is this called a paradox?

## Answer key
1. Staff may lose familiarity, readiness, and urgency.
2. Because cases may be missed by weak systems.
3. Rare diseases are less likely to be considered and tested.
4. Sensitivity, timeliness, diagnostic readiness, and coverage.
5. Because surveillance must strengthen even as visible burden falls.
