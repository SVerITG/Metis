# Integration, Health Systems, and Sustainability

## Learning objectives
- Explain why elimination gains can erode when programmes are not embedded in systems.
- Describe the role of integration and routine service delivery in sustainability.
- Recognize the risks of vertical success without durable system support.

## Prerequisites
- Equity, geography, and the last mile.

## Content
Elimination programmes often begin with vertical urgency, targeted funding, and exceptional operational effort. But long-term success depends on whether key functions become part of routine systems: diagnostics, reporting, referral, supervision, supply, and governance.

Integration does not mean abandoning disease-specific expertise. It means ensuring that essential functions survive beyond special campaigns and donor cycles. Without this, gains can erode quickly once burden falls and attention shifts elsewhere.

## Key takeaways
- Sustainability depends on systems, not only campaigns.
- Integration helps preserve elimination gains after visibility declines.
- Vertical success can be fragile if routine systems remain weak.

## Self-check questions
1. Why can elimination gains erode after apparent success?
2. What does integration mean here?
3. Why is routine service capacity important?
4. What is one risk of overreliance on vertical structures?
5. Why should governance be part of sustainability thinking?

## Answer key
1. Because surveillance and response capacity may weaken when attention and funding decline.
2. Embedding key functions into routine health-system processes.
3. Because endgame and post-elimination work still require detection and response.
4. Capacity may disappear when special funding or campaigns end.
5. Because accountability and coordination shape whether systems stay functional.
