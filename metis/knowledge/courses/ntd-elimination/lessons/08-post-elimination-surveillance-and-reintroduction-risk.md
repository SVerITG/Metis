# Post-Elimination Surveillance and Reintroduction Risk

## Learning objectives
- Describe why surveillance remains necessary after elimination claims.
- Explain the main sources of reintroduction or recrudescence risk.
- Recognize what post-elimination stewardship requires.

## Prerequisites
- Integration, health systems, and sustainability.

## Content
Post-elimination surveillance exists because success is not self-sustaining. Reintroduction can occur through cross-border movement, undetected residual foci, vector persistence, ecological change, waning diagnostic readiness, or programmatic neglect. The absence of recent cases therefore does not justify dismantling all vigilance.

Post-elimination strategy usually requires targeted surveillance, clear response triggers, ongoing diagnostic access, reporting pathways, and governance that prevents the disease from becoming invisible again. The challenge is maintaining enough readiness without reverting to unsustainable intensity.

## Key takeaways
- Elimination does not end surveillance responsibilities.
- Reintroduction risk can come from both biological and system failures.
- Post-elimination stewardship is about calibrated vigilance, not permanent emergency mode.

## Self-check questions
1. Why does surveillance continue after elimination?
2. Name two sources of reintroduction risk.
3. What is one element of post-elimination stewardship?
4. Why is permanent high-intensity response not always appropriate?
5. What happens if a disease becomes invisible to routine systems?

## Answer key
1. Because hidden transmission or reintroduction can still occur.
2. Cross-border movement, residual foci, weak diagnostics, or vector persistence.
3. Targeted surveillance, response triggers, or ongoing diagnostic capacity.
4. Because it may be operationally and financially unsustainable.
5. Cases may be missed until transmission is re-established.
