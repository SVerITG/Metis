# Equity, Geography, and the Last Mile

## Learning objectives
- Describe why elimination often stalls in specific populations or places.
- Explain the relevance of equity and geography in residual transmission.
- Recognize why averages can hide last-mile risk.

## Prerequisites
- Verification, validation, and evidence packages.

## Content
Elimination often becomes a last-mile problem. Residual transmission may persist in remote areas, mobile populations, politically neglected groups, or places where services are weakest. National averages can look encouraging while local risk remains concentrated.

This is why equity and geography are not side issues. They are central to elimination success. The last mile is frequently where surveillance is weakest and programme reach is most fragile.

## Key takeaways
- Residual transmission is often geographically and socially uneven.
- National averages can conceal elimination failure in specific places.
- Equity is a practical elimination issue, not only a moral one.

## Self-check questions
1. What is meant by the last mile in elimination?
2. Why can national averages mislead?
3. Which groups are often missed in endgame strategies?
4. Why does geography matter operationally?
5. How is equity linked to surveillance quality?

## Answer key
1. The final phase where remaining transmission is concentrated and harder to reach.
2. Because they can hide persistent hotspots or underserved groups.
3. Remote, mobile, marginalized, or politically neglected populations.
4. Because access, infrastructure, and ecology affect intervention reach and detection.
5. Underserved groups often have weaker access to diagnosis and reporting.
