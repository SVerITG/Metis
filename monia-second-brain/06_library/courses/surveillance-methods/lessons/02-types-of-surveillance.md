# Types of Surveillance

## Learning objectives
- Compare passive, active, sentinel, syndromic, and case-based surveillance.
- Match surveillance type to programme goals, disease ecology, and resource constraints.
- Explain trade-offs between sensitivity, timeliness, representativeness, and cost.
- Recognize why hybrid surveillance systems are often necessary in practice.

## Prerequisites
- What is surveillance.

## Content

### Section 1: No single surveillance type fits every purpose
Surveillance systems differ because public-health questions differ. A system designed for routine burden monitoring is not identical to one designed for elimination verification or rapid outbreak detection. That is why surveillance types should be understood as strategic choices rather than as administrative labels.

The right type of surveillance depends on the disease, the programme stage, available resources, and how quickly action is needed.

### Section 2: Passive surveillance
**Passive surveillance** depends on routine reporting from health facilities, laboratories, or other established sources. Cases are detected through ordinary service delivery and then reported through the surveillance system.

Its strengths are:

- scalability
- relatively low cost
- broad geographic coverage
- suitability for routine monitoring

Its limitations are:

- underreporting
- delayed reporting
- dependence on health-seeking behavior and diagnostic access
- variable data quality across facilities

Passive surveillance is often the backbone of national surveillance systems, but it may miss many cases when awareness, access, or diagnostic capacity are weak.

### Section 3: Active surveillance
**Active surveillance** involves deliberate effort to seek cases rather than waiting for them to be reported routinely. This may include outreach visits, household screening, regular facility contact, or review of records by dedicated teams.

Its strengths are:

- higher sensitivity
- more complete case finding
- usefulness in outbreaks, elimination programmes, and high-priority threats

Its limitations are:

- cost
- labour intensity
- limited sustainability at large scale

Active surveillance is often justified when missing cases would be especially costly, such as in elimination settings or during acute response.

### Section 4: Sentinel surveillance
**Sentinel surveillance** uses selected reporting sites that collect high-quality, systematic data on a defined set of conditions.

Its strengths are:

- deeper, often more standardized data
- feasibility when universal reporting is unrealistic
- usefulness for trend monitoring and early warning in selected populations

Its limitations are:

- limited representativeness
- dependence on site selection
- potential bias if sentinel sites differ systematically from non-sentinel areas

Sentinel systems are especially useful when the goal is monitoring trends or pathogen patterns rather than estimating full national burden directly.

### Section 5: Syndromic and case-based surveillance
**Syndromic surveillance** focuses on symptom patterns, chief complaints, or other early signals before full diagnostic confirmation. It emphasizes timeliness and can be useful for outbreak detection or mass-gathering monitoring.

**Case-based surveillance** records detailed information for each individual case, often including demographics, laboratory results, location, and follow-up status. It is especially useful in elimination settings, outbreak response, and diseases where each case requires investigation.

Case-based systems are powerful because they support both epidemiologic tracking and operational response, but they can become resource-intensive when case volume is high.

### Section 6: Worked example - HAT in low-incidence settings
In low-incidence HAT settings, a purely passive system may miss cases because health workers see the disease rarely and patients may present late. A purely active system may be too costly to sustain indefinitely.

A more realistic approach may combine:

- passive facility-based detection
- targeted active case finding in higher-risk areas
- case-based investigation of confirmed infections
- referral-linked follow-up around identified cases

This illustrates a core surveillance principle: hybrid systems are often more practical than purist ones.

### Section 7: Choosing the right type
When choosing a surveillance type, ask:

- is sensitivity the top priority?
- is national representativeness essential?
- how quickly must the system detect a signal?
- how much ongoing field effort is affordable?
- is each case important enough to justify case-based follow-up?

The answers may differ for the same disease at different points in its control trajectory. Early burden estimation, outbreak response, and post-elimination vigilance often require different surveillance designs.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- assuming passive surveillance is "enough" simply because it exists
- using sentinel data as if they were fully representative
- choosing active surveillance without planning for sustainability
- confusing syndromic and case-based purposes
- treating surveillance types as mutually exclusive when combination is more realistic

The practical lesson is that surveillance design should reflect the decision problem, not only institutional habit.

## Key takeaways
- Passive, active, sentinel, syndromic, and case-based surveillance each solve different operational problems.
- Passive surveillance is scalable but often incomplete; active surveillance is more sensitive but costly.
- Sentinel surveillance trades representativeness for depth and feasibility.
- Syndromic systems emphasize speed, while case-based systems support detailed follow-up and control.
- Hybrid surveillance systems are often the most realistic solution in applied public health.

## Self-check questions
1. Why might passive surveillance undercount cases?
2. When is active surveillance especially valuable?
3. What is the main trade-off in sentinel surveillance?
4. How is syndromic surveillance different from case-based surveillance?
5. Why are hybrid systems common in elimination settings?
6. What question should guide the choice of surveillance type?

## Answer key
1. Because it depends on routine detection, health-seeking behavior, diagnostic access, and reporting completeness.
2. When sensitivity is crucial, such as during outbreaks, elimination efforts, or high-priority disease control.
3. It gains depth and feasibility while sacrificing full representativeness.
4. Syndromic surveillance focuses on early symptom-based signals, while case-based surveillance tracks detailed information on each identified case.
5. Because no single type usually provides the necessary balance of sensitivity, feasibility, and follow-up capacity.
6. The public-health decision or function the system is meant to support, such as routine monitoring, rapid detection, or case-by-case response.
