# Intervention Packages for Elimination

## Learning objectives
- Explain why elimination usually depends on combined interventions.
- Compare case finding, vector control, MDA, and service-based strategies.
- Recognize how intervention packages vary across NTDs.

## Prerequisites
- Setting targets and choosing indicators.

## Content
Elimination is rarely achieved through one intervention alone. Programmes often combine case detection, treatment access, vector control, preventive chemotherapy, behavior change, environmental action, and targeted surveillance. The right package depends on the biology of the disease and the health-system context.

Operationally, the challenge is not only choosing the right interventions but sequencing and integrating them so that coverage is high enough and surveillance remains credible as incidence falls.

## Key takeaways
- Elimination strategies are usually multi-component.
- Disease biology and health-system context determine which package is plausible.
- Strategy design includes timing, sequencing, and operational fit.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is one intervention often not enough for elimination?
2. What can determine which intervention package is appropriate?
3. Why does sequencing matter?
4. What is one common operational challenge in combined strategies?
5. Why should surveillance remain linked to intervention strategy?

## Answer key
1. Because transmission often depends on multiple pathways and system constraints.
2. Disease biology, transmission route, feasibility, and health-system capacity.
3. Because the timing of interventions can affect coverage and impact.
4. Maintaining consistent implementation across places and time.
5. Because declining incidence changes how programmes judge whether interventions are working.
