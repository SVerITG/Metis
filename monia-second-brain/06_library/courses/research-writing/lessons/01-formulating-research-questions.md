# Formulating Research Questions

## Learning objectives
- Turn broad interests into answerable research questions.
- Match different question types to suitable study designs and outcomes.
- Use frameworks such as PICOT pragmatically rather than mechanically.
- Recognize when a question is interesting but still too vague, too broad, or too infeasible to study well.

## Prerequisites
- None.

## Content

### Section 1: Why the question comes first
Many weak studies start with a method, dataset, or software plan rather than with a clear question. Strong research usually works in the opposite direction. The question should drive the design, variables, analysis, and interpretation.

A good research question is not just a sentence that sounds academic. It should clarify:

- what phenomenon matters
- in whom or where it matters
- what comparison or contrast is relevant
- what outcome will be judged
- why answering it would change understanding or decisions

If those elements are unclear, the study usually becomes unclear as well.

### Section 2: Different question types need different designs
Not all research questions are the same. Common types include:

- **etiologic** questions about causes or risk factors
- **diagnostic** questions about test performance
- **prognostic** questions about likely outcomes over time
- **implementation** questions about delivery, uptake, or feasibility
- **policy** questions about system effects or programme consequences

These distinctions matter because a well-formed diagnostic question does not require the same design as a policy-evaluation question. A good question therefore already points toward a design family before any methods section is written.

### Section 3: What makes a question answerable
An answerable research question is usually:

- specific enough to define variables and population
- important enough to matter scientifically or operationally
- feasible with available data, time, and expertise
- narrow enough to analyze rigorously

A question can be important and still unusable. "How can health systems improve?" is relevant but too broad to study directly. It needs narrowing into a tractable question with a definable outcome and setting.

### Section 4: PICOT and related frameworks
Frameworks such as **PICOT** can help sharpen a question:

- **P**opulation
- **I**ntervention or exposure
- **C**omparison
- **O**utcome
- **T**ime

These frameworks are useful because they force clarity. But they should not be used mechanically. Not every question needs every element equally, and qualitative, diagnostic, or implementation questions may need adaptation rather than rigid application.

The value of a framework is not the acronym itself. It is the discipline of specifying the contrast and outcome clearly.

### Section 5: Worked example - from broad interest to researchable question
Suppose a researcher starts with the broad interest:

"How can post-elimination HAT surveillance remain sensitive and feasible?"

That is important, but too broad for a single study. It can be sharpened into something more answerable, such as:

"Among peripheral health facilities in former HAT foci, does quarterly supervisory support compared with standard practice improve suspect-case referral completion over 12 months?"

This version is stronger because:

- the population is defined
- the intervention and comparison are clearer
- the outcome is measurable
- the time horizon is explicit

It may still need refinement, but it is now close to a studyable question.

### Section 6: Relevance and feasibility must coexist
A common mistake is to choose only on importance or only on feasibility.

Questions chosen only for importance may be too large, too complex, or impossible to answer well with available resources.

Questions chosen only for feasibility may be technically convenient but scientifically trivial.

Good question formulation balances both. The ideal question matters enough to justify the work and is focused enough to answer credibly.

### Section 7: How question wording shapes outcomes and analysis
Question wording affects everything downstream.

For example, asking "what factors are associated with delayed diagnosis?" implies observational analysis and careful confounding thinking.

Asking "does supervision reduce reporting delay?" suggests an evaluative comparison and perhaps a trial, quasi-experiment, or before-after design.

Asking "how do clinicians experience surveillance burden?" suggests qualitative or mixed-methods work rather than a pure regression problem.

That is why the question is not a preface to methods. It is the core design decision.

### Section 8: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- asking a question that is too broad
- confusing a topic area with a research question
- choosing a question because data already exist rather than because the question is strong
- failing to define the comparison
- leaving the outcome too vague

A practical test is simple: if the team cannot explain the question in one or two precise sentences, the study probably is not ready.

## Key takeaways
- A strong study starts with a sharp research question rather than with a preferred method or available dataset.
- Different question types imply different study designs and analytic strategies.
- Good questions balance importance, specificity, and feasibility.
- Frameworks such as PICOT are useful for sharpening questions, but should be used pragmatically.
- Clear question wording improves every later part of the study, from protocol to analysis to writing.

## Self-check questions
1. What makes a research question answerable rather than merely interesting?
2. Why does design choice depend on question type?
3. How can a question be important but still unusable?
4. What does PICOT help clarify?
5. Why is "we already have the data" a weak reason to define a question?
6. What is one sign that a research question is still too vague?

## Answer key
1. It is specific enough to define population, comparison, outcome, and feasibility while still addressing an important issue.
2. Because etiologic, diagnostic, prognostic, implementation, and policy questions require different evidence structures and comparisons.
3. Because it may be too broad, too poorly specified, or too infeasible to answer rigorously with available resources.
4. Population, intervention or exposure, comparison, outcome, and time.
5. Because a good research question should be driven by scientific or operational importance, not only by dataset convenience.
6. The team cannot explain clearly what is being compared, in whom, over what period, and with what outcome.
