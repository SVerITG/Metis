# Systematic Reviews and PRISMA

## Learning objectives
- Explain how a systematic review differs from a narrative review.
- Describe the role of protocol registration, reproducible searching, screening, and structured synthesis.
- Use PRISMA 2020 as a reporting scaffold rather than a post hoc checklist.
- Recognize common threats to transparency in review-based research.

## Prerequisites
- Literature review strategy.
- Reporting guidelines and EQUATOR.

## Content

### Section 1: Systematic reviews are methods projects
A systematic review is not simply a long background section. It is a research project with its own question, eligibility criteria, search strategy, screening process, risk-of-bias assessment, and synthesis plan. Its credibility depends on whether these steps are explicit and reproducible.

Narrative reviews can be useful for orientation, but they often rely on selective searching and unsystematic inclusion. Systematic reviews aim to reduce that discretion. They do not eliminate judgment, but they force judgment to be documented.

### Section 2: Start with a clear review question
As with primary studies, the review question determines the rest of the design. A broad question such as "What is known about digital surveillance?" is too loose for a rigorous review unless the scope is carefully bounded. A stronger question defines:

- population or setting
- intervention, exposure, or phenomenon
- comparator when relevant
- outcomes or review focus
- eligible study designs

This structure does not need to be mechanically PICOT in every case, but the boundaries must be explicit. Otherwise the search, screening, and synthesis become unstable.

### Section 3: Protocols and registration
Review methods should be specified before screening begins. A protocol clarifies the objective, databases, search terms, inclusion criteria, extraction items, risk-of-bias approach, and synthesis plan. Registration in places such as PROSPERO increases transparency by showing what was intended before the evidence landscape was fully known.

Pre-specification matters because systematic reviews are vulnerable to quiet post hoc decisions. If inclusion criteria shift after the authors see the studies, the final review may reflect convenience more than method.

### Section 4: Reproducible searching and screening
Search strategy is central, not clerical. A defensible review reports:

- which databases were searched
- the date of the last search
- the search strings or enough detail to reconstruct them
- language or date restrictions
- supplementary searching, such as reference lists or grey literature

Screening should also be transparent. Readers need to know how duplicates were handled, whether screening was done by one or two reviewers, how disagreements were resolved, and why full texts were excluded.

These details matter because the review's evidence base is only as credible as its selection process.

### Section 5: Worked example
Imagine a systematic review of digital tools used for event-based surveillance in low-resource settings.

A weak review might say that the authors searched PubMed and Google Scholar for relevant articles and included studies that seemed applicable.

A strong review would define event-based surveillance tools clearly, specify the databases searched, provide the search strings, state the search dates, define inclusion and exclusion criteria, document the screening workflow, describe data extraction items, and explain whether a narrative synthesis or meta-analysis was planned. If study designs are heterogeneous, the review should justify why narrative synthesis is more appropriate than statistical pooling.

Again, the difference is not academic formality. It is the difference between a review that can be trusted and one that cannot be audited.

### Section 6: PRISMA 2020 as a scaffold
PRISMA 2020 helps authors report the key parts of the review process: title, rationale, objectives, eligibility criteria, information sources, search strategy, selection methods, data collection, risk-of-bias assessment, synthesis methods, results, and discussion. The PRISMA flow diagram is particularly useful because it shows how the evidence base was constructed from search results to included studies.

PRISMA should not be treated as something added at the end. If authors wait until submission to look at the checklist, they often discover that key steps were never documented properly.

### Section 7: Risk of bias and synthesis
A systematic review is not complete when studies are merely listed. The review also needs an appraisal strategy. That may involve design-specific tools, structured judgments about internal validity, and careful consideration of heterogeneity.

For synthesis, authors should explain whether they used:

- narrative synthesis
- meta-analysis
- subgroup analysis
- sensitivity analysis
- certainty assessment frameworks

Not every review needs meta-analysis. In many public health topics, interventions, settings, and outcomes are too heterogeneous for meaningful pooling. In those cases, a transparent narrative synthesis is better than a forced quantitative summary.

### Section 8: Frequent problems
Common weaknesses in review manuscripts include:

- vague eligibility criteria
- poorly documented searches
- single-reviewer screening without explanation
- no risk-of-bias assessment
- narrative synthesis with no organizing logic
- PRISMA used superficially after the fact

These are not minor reporting issues. They affect whether the review's conclusions reflect the evidence base or the authors' undocumented preferences.

## Key takeaways
- Systematic reviews are research projects with explicit methods, not extended literature summaries.
- Clear review questions, pre-specified protocols, and reproducible searches are foundational.
- PRISMA 2020 works best when used to shape the review from the start rather than to decorate the final manuscript.
- Screening, exclusion, and synthesis decisions must be documented transparently.
- Narrative synthesis is acceptable when heterogeneity is high, but it still requires structure and methodological discipline.

## Self-check questions
1. What is the main methodological difference between a narrative review and a systematic review?
2. Why is protocol registration useful before screening begins?
3. What does the PRISMA flow diagram contribute to a manuscript?
4. Why might a narrative synthesis be preferable to meta-analysis in some public health reviews?
5. What makes a search strategy reproducible?
6. Why is risk-of-bias assessment important even when the review is not doing a meta-analysis?

## Answer key
1. A systematic review uses explicit, reproducible methods for identifying, selecting, and synthesizing evidence, while a narrative review is usually more discretionary.
2. It makes the planned methods visible before the evidence is fully known, reducing the risk of selective decisions later.
3. It documents how the set of included studies was built from search results through screening and exclusion.
4. Because heterogeneity in interventions, settings, or outcomes can make statistical pooling misleading.
5. Clear reporting of databases, dates, search strings, limits, and supplementary search procedures.
6. Because readers still need to know how trustworthy the included evidence is before accepting the review's conclusions.
