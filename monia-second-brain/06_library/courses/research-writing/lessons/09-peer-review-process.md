# Peer Review and Revision

## Learning objectives
- Describe the main stages of journal peer review and editorial decision-making.
- Build a practical strategy for responding to reviewer comments.
- Distinguish comments that need manuscript changes from comments that need clearer explanation.
- Revise with professionalism while defending justified choices when necessary.

## Prerequisites
- IMRaD writing basics.
- Reporting guidelines and writing sections.

## Content

### Section 1: What peer review is actually testing
Peer review is often described as quality control, but that phrase is too vague to be useful. In practice, reviewers and editors are testing several things at once:

- whether the question matters to the journal audience
- whether the methods support the claims
- whether the writing is clear enough to evaluate
- whether the findings add something beyond what is already known
- whether the manuscript follows the journal's reporting expectations

This means reviewer criticism does not always indicate that the science is wrong. Sometimes it shows that the manuscript failed to make the science legible.

### Section 2: Understand the decision categories
Most papers receive one of a small number of decisions: reject, major revision, minor revision, or accept with small edits. Major revision can feel discouraging, but in practice it often means the editor sees a possible path to publication if the core issues are addressed.

The first task after receiving reviews is not to start typing. It is to classify the comments:

- fatal design or validity concerns
- important but fixable analytic concerns
- requests for clarification
- stylistic or formatting requests

This triage prevents authors from spending hours polishing wording before dealing with the one point that might actually determine the editorial outcome.

### Section 3: Build a response matrix
A strong revision process starts with a response matrix. For each comment, record:

- reviewer number
- exact comment
- your response
- whether the manuscript changed
- where the change appears

This structure forces specificity. "Done" is not a sufficient response. Better responses explain the action taken and where the editor can find it. If no change was made, explain why respectfully and directly.

### Section 4: When to revise the manuscript versus the explanation
Some reviewer comments reveal a real problem in the analysis or reporting. Others reveal that the paper was unclear. The revision strategy depends on which problem you are facing.

If a reviewer says the confounder strategy is weak, you may need either:

- a real analytic change, such as an additional sensitivity analysis or clearer model specification
- or clearer explanation of the rationale already used

The distinction matters. Cosmetic wording changes do not solve analytic problems, and unnecessary re-analysis can waste time when the real issue is presentation.

### Section 5: Worked example
Imagine reviewers question whether a post-elimination HAT surveillance evaluation overstates system sensitivity because the study included mostly accessible facilities.

A weak response would be:

"Thank you for this important comment. We have revised the manuscript accordingly."

A stronger response would say that the reviewer is correct that the sampling frame may overrepresent better-supported facilities; the revised manuscript now makes the purposive sampling strategy explicit in the Methods, adds a limitation in the Discussion, and softens the conclusion from system-wide performance to performance in assessed facilities. If a sensitivity analysis is possible, note that too.

That response is persuasive because it shows that the authors understood the criticism and changed the manuscript where the claim was too broad.

### Section 6: It is acceptable to disagree, but not vaguely
Not every reviewer request should be accepted. A reviewer may ask for an analysis that does not match the design, request a claim that overreaches the data, or misunderstand a method already described correctly.

Disagreement is reasonable when:

- the reviewer request is methodologically inappropriate
- the requested analysis is impossible with the available data
- the point is already addressed clearly in the manuscript
- complying would introduce a less defensible interpretation

The key is to respond with evidence and logic, not irritation. Cite the relevant section, clarify the rationale, and, if needed, improve the wording that may have led to the misunderstanding.

### Section 7: Revision is also manuscript improvement
The best revision rounds do more than answer the exact reviewer comments. They use the reviews to identify patterns. If two reviewers were confused by the study setting, the problem may be structural. If one reviewer misunderstood the exposure definition, other readers might too.

This is why revision should be read as feedback on communication as well as content. A careful author asks: what did this review reveal about how the paper is currently being read?

### Section 8: Professional tone matters
Editors notice tone. A response letter should be calm, specific, and traceable. Avoid defensive language, sarcasm, or long emotional explanations. Thank reviewers briefly, but spend most of the letter on substantive responses.

Good response letters make it easy for the editor to say yes. They reduce friction by being organized, precise, and transparent.

## Key takeaways
- Peer review tests clarity, validity, relevance, and fit with the journal, not just whether reviewers "like" the paper.
- The first step after reviews arrive is to triage comments by severity and type.
- Response matrices make revisions traceable and reduce vague replies.
- Some comments require real analytic changes; others require clearer writing.
- It is acceptable to disagree with reviewers when the rationale is methodologically sound and clearly explained.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is it useful to classify reviewer comments before starting revisions?
2. What makes a response letter persuasive to editors?
3. When is it reasonable to disagree with a reviewer?
4. Why is "revised accordingly" usually not enough as a response?
5. How can reviewer confusion point to a broader manuscript problem?
6. What is the practical purpose of including line numbers or section references in the response letter?

## Answer key
1. Because it helps authors focus first on the issues most likely to determine the editorial decision.
2. Specificity, traceability, respectful tone, and clear links between each comment and the corresponding manuscript change.
3. When the request is inappropriate for the design, impossible with the data, or less defensible than the original approach.
4. Because it does not tell the editor what changed, where it changed, or why the response is adequate.
5. If one reviewer misunderstood a key point, future readers may also misunderstand it, suggesting a communication problem.
6. They allow editors and reviewers to verify quickly that the revision actually addresses the comment.
