# Literature Review Strategy

## Learning objectives
- Build a transparent and reproducible search strategy.
- Distinguish scoping, narrative, and systematic review purposes.
- Screen, organize, and synthesize literature efficiently.
- Use note-taking and thematic structure to make later writing easier rather than harder.

## Prerequisites
- Formulating research questions.

## Content

### Section 1: Why review strategy matters
A literature review is not simply "reading around the topic." It is a method for identifying what is already known, what remains uncertain, and where a new study can contribute. Weak review strategy often produces two bad outcomes:

- a shallow review that misses key work
- an unstructured pile of citations that cannot support a coherent argument

Good review strategy makes the search process explicit, organized, and aligned with the research question.

### Section 2: Start from the question
The search strategy should come from the question, not from arbitrary database browsing. The usual first step is to break the question into concepts such as:

- population or condition
- exposure or intervention
- outcome
- context or setting

Each concept then needs synonyms, spelling variants, and related terms. This is why strong searches are built conceptually rather than from a single favored phrase.

### Section 3: Review types differ
Not every review has the same purpose.

**Narrative reviews** are often used to build conceptual background and interpret a field without claiming exhaustive coverage.

**Scoping reviews** aim to map the breadth of literature, identify themes, and show what kinds of evidence exist.

**Systematic reviews** aim for a more comprehensive, reproducible search and a clearly documented screening and synthesis process.

The review type should match the purpose. A PhD introduction may not need a formal systematic review, while a methods paper or evidence-synthesis manuscript might.

### Section 4: Search strategy as method
A transparent search strategy usually specifies:

- databases searched
- key concepts and search terms
- date ranges
- language limits, if any
- inclusion and exclusion criteria
- screening process

Searches almost always evolve during a project, and that is normal. What matters is documenting meaningful changes rather than pretending the first search string was perfect.

### Section 5: Screening and selection
Once search results are assembled, screening should be structured. Typical stages include:

- title screening
- abstract screening
- full-text review
- final inclusion for synthesis

Even when the review is not a formal systematic review, explicit inclusion reasoning improves quality. It helps prevent a literature review from becoming a selective list of papers that support the author's preferred conclusion.

### Section 6: Worked example - surveillance review strategy
Suppose the question concerns sustainable post-elimination surveillance for HAT. A useful review strategy might combine:

- disease terms such as sleeping sickness or HAT
- surveillance terms such as case-based, passive, active, event-based, or elimination surveillance
- context terms such as elimination, post-elimination, or verification
- regional terms if the review is focused geographically

The results can then be organized thematically, for example:

- surveillance system design
- diagnostic confirmation
- health-worker vigilance
- geographic targeting
- sustainability and cost

That thematic organization is often more useful for writing than a chronological pile of article summaries.

### Section 7: Note-taking that supports writing
Good note-taking is less about storing every detail and more about extracting the right structure for later writing.

Useful fields often include:

- citation
- study design
- setting
- key finding
- relevance to your question
- methodological strength or weakness
- where it fits thematically

This saves time later because the review becomes an argument map rather than an archive of disconnected annotations.

### Section 8: Synthesis is not summary
A strong literature review does more than summarize paper after paper. It synthesizes.

Synthesis asks:

- where do studies converge?
- where do they disagree?
- what methods are repeatedly weak?
- what settings are underrepresented?
- what gap justifies the new study?

This is the point at which review strategy becomes writing strategy. The goal is not to show everything the author has read. The goal is to show how the field currently stands and why the next study matters.

### Section 9: Common mistakes
Several mistakes recur often.

- searching without a clear question
- failing to document major search decisions
- using too few databases or concept variations
- writing the review as a list of article summaries
- confusing volume of references with quality of synthesis

The practical lesson is that a literature review should be a method-backed argument, not a citation dump.

## Key takeaways
- Literature review strategy should flow directly from the research question.
- Different review types serve different purposes and should not be confused.
- Search strategy, screening, and note-taking are part of the method, not administrative overhead.
- Thematic synthesis is more useful than article-by-article summary for most research writing.
- A strong review clarifies both what is known and what gap remains.

## Self-check questions
1. Why should search strategy start from the research question?
2. What distinguishes a scoping review from a systematic review?
3. Why document search iterations and inclusion decisions?
4. Why is article-by-article summary usually weaker than thematic synthesis?
5. What kind of notes are most useful for later writing?
6. What is one sign that a literature review is becoming a citation dump?

## Answer key
1. Because the question determines which concepts, databases, and terms are relevant and helps prevent an unfocused search.
2. A scoping review maps the breadth and types of evidence, while a systematic review aims for more comprehensive and reproducible identification and synthesis of studies.
3. Because transparency improves reproducibility and helps readers understand how the evidence base was constructed.
4. Because thematic synthesis shows patterns, gaps, agreement, and disagreement across studies rather than merely listing them.
5. Notes that capture design, setting, key finding, relevance, and thematic role in the argument.
6. The review becomes a long list of paper summaries without a clear explanation of what the field collectively shows or what gap remains.
