# Privacy, Confidentiality, and Data Governance

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish privacy, confidentiality, and data governance.
- Explain why de-identification does not remove all risk.
- Describe good practice for secondary use and sharing of sensitive data.

## Prerequisites
- Consent, assent, and voluntariness.

## Content
Privacy concerns what information is collected in the first place. Confidentiality concerns how that information is protected once collected. Data governance covers the broader rules for storage, access, transfer, sharing, retention, and secondary use.

In epidemiology, data can remain sensitive even after direct identifiers are removed. Rare conditions, small communities, geolocation, and linkage variables can all increase re-identification risk. Good governance therefore requires access control, minimization of collected data, documentation of use, and clarity about who can re-use data and under what conditions.

## Key takeaways
- Privacy, confidentiality, and governance are related but distinct.
- De-identification reduces risk but does not eliminate it.
- Secondary use of data requires clear governance and justification.

## Self-check questions
1. What is one difference between privacy and confidentiality?
2. Why can de-identified data still be risky?
3. What is one good data-governance practice?
4. Why should researchers minimize collected data?
5. What should secondary-use requests clarify?

## Answer key
1. Privacy concerns collection; confidentiality concerns protection after collection.
2. Because rare combinations of variables can still identify people or communities.
3. Access controls, audit trails, or clear retention policies.
4. Because unnecessary data increase risk without clear benefit.
5. Purpose, access conditions, governance, and justification.
