# Equity, Ethics, and Priority Setting

## Learning objectives
- Explain why efficiency is only one value in health policy.
- Describe how equity concerns affect priority setting.
- Recognize the ethical limits of narrow economic reasoning.

## Prerequisites
- Budget impact and affordability.

## Content
Public health decisions are not based on efficiency alone. Policymakers may prioritize severe disease, marginalized groups, rural access, financial protection, or elimination goals even when a strict cost-effectiveness ranking would point elsewhere.

Equity matters because population averages can hide who benefits and who is excluded. Ethics matters because some services are considered socially necessary even when they are not the most efficient option in narrow economic terms. Good priority setting makes these value choices explicit rather than pretending that one ratio settles the question.

## Key takeaways
- Efficiency is important but not sufficient.
- Equity and ethics shape real allocation decisions.
- Transparent priority setting should state its value choices openly.

## Self-check questions
1. Why is cost-effectiveness not the only criterion in public health?
2. What equity issue can average results hide?
3. Why should value choices be explicit?
4. Name one ethical reason to fund a less efficient service.
5. What is the risk of using economics as the only decision rule?

## Answer key
1. Because decisions also involve fairness, severity, rights, and political legitimacy.
2. That disadvantaged groups may benefit less or remain underserved.
3. Because trade-offs are normative and should not be hidden behind technical language.
4. Protecting vulnerable groups, ensuring rural access, or meeting essential service obligations.
5. Important social goals may be ignored.
