# What Counts as an Outbreak?

## Learning objectives
- Distinguish an outbreak, cluster, epidemic, and surveillance signal.
- Explain why confirmation matters before mobilizing a full response.
- Recognize different ways outbreaks are detected.

## Prerequisites
- Basic epidemiology.
- Screening and surveillance concepts.

## Content

### Section 1: Outbreaks are deviations, not just large numbers
An outbreak is the occurrence of disease or another health event above what is normally expected for a place, population, and period. That means the same absolute number can be ordinary in one context and alarming in another. A single suspected rabies case in a previously unaffected area may trigger an investigation, while ten influenza cases in winter may not.

### Section 2: Detection pathways
Outbreaks are detected through routine surveillance, laboratory alerts, clinician concern, community reporting, media signals, or event-based surveillance. Good responders treat each alert as a signal that needs verification rather than as a confirmed outbreak.

### Section 3: Why confirmation comes first
False alarms happen. Reporting artefacts, duplicated records, laboratory contamination, or changes in case definitions can all create the appearance of an outbreak. The first question is therefore not "how do we explain this outbreak?" but "is there really an excess of cases and are we measuring the same event consistently?"

### Practical example
Suppose a district notices six meningitis notifications in one week. Before escalating, investigators should check reporting completeness, laboratory status, residence, and onset dates. If two reports are duplicates and one was miscoded from the previous week, the apparent cluster may shrink substantially.

## Key takeaways
- Outbreaks are defined against expectation, not raw counts alone.
- Signals can come from routine and non-routine sources.
- Verification prevents wasted effort and poor decisions.

## Self-check questions
1. Why can one case be considered an outbreak in some settings?
2. What is the difference between an alert and a confirmed outbreak?
3. Name three common outbreak detection routes.
4. Why are denominator and baseline information important?
5. What is one reporting artefact that can mimic an outbreak?

## Answer key
1. Because the expected baseline may be zero or the disease may be unusually serious.
2. An alert is a signal requiring verification; a confirmed outbreak is an excess event supported by evidence.
3. Routine surveillance, laboratory alerts, clinician reporting, community reports, or event-based systems.
4. They define what is normal and allow assessment of whether cases truly exceed expectation.
5. Duplicated records or delayed backlogged reporting.
